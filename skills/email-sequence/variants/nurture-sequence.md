# Nurture Sequence Playbook

## Role In the System

A nurture sequence is the long game. It assumes the subscriber is not ready to buy yet and will not be for weeks. The job is to stay in the inbox long enough, at a pace light enough, that when the creator does make an offer, the subscriber already holds the 8 required beliefs that make the buy decision feel inevitable.

The sequence treats the inbox as a classroom plus a newsroom plus a diary. Classroom content teaches the mechanism one sub-step at a time. Newsroom content ties current events or market shifts back to the mechanism. Diary content shows the creator's wins, losses, and contrarian takes so the subscriber builds a felt sense of who this person is.

A nurture sequence is not a broadcast list and is not a launch sequence. Broadcast lists send one-off value drops with no belief arc. Launch sequences compress urgency into 7 to 14 days. Nurture sits between them. Its cadence is slower. Its arc is longer. Its close is inevitable rather than pressured.

## Gate-in Conditions

Ship this sequence only if:

- Welcome sequence is live and already routing engaged subscribers into nurture
- Compartment 2 (Audience) is 70 percent or higher, so segmentation can be surgical
- Compartment 3 (Offer) is 60 percent or higher, so belief-installation has something real to install toward
- Compartment 1 brand voice has 20 or more `phrases_to_use` (long sequences wear out small voice libraries)
- Nurture operator has calendar time to review one monthly pattern report, not one weekly

## Belief Arc — The 8 Required Beliefs Across 45 to 90 Days

The subscriber must hold all 8 beliefs before they are ready for a direct offer. Nurture installs them in a specific order because belief stacking is sequential, not parallel.

| Phase | Days | Beliefs Installed | Emails |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 — Desire + Identity | 0 to 14 | 1 Reproduction (I want this), 2 Derivative desire (this ties to who I want to be) | 4 to 6 emails |
| Phase 2 — Possibility | 15 to 30 | 3 Can-fulfill (this CAN deliver), 6 Better/faster (beats my alternatives) | 4 to 6 emails |
| Phase 3 — Specificity | 31 to 50 | 5 This-offer (this specific thing is right), 8 Trust-expertise (they know how) | 5 to 7 emails |
| Phase 4 — Permission | 51 to 75 | 7 Trust-person (I trust them), 4 Urgency (I need this now) | 5 to 7 emails |
| Phase 5 — Bridge | 76 to 90 | All 8 reinforced, soft CTA warming up to offer | 2 to 4 emails |

Each phase has a teaching theme, a proof cadence, and a specific class of CTA. Mix them and the arc collapses.

## The 20-to-30 Email Skeleton

### Phase 1 — Desire + Identity (Emails 1 to 6)

**Teaching theme:** Who you become when you get good at this.

**Proof cadence:** Creator origin moments, identity-shift stories, quiet testimonials (not numbers yet).

**CTA class:** Back to pillar content, never to purchase.

Email patterns to deploy in order:
1. Identity reframe: "I thought I was a [old role]. I was actually a [new identity]."
2. The contrarian take: a belief the ICP holds that is quietly wrong.
3. Creator diary: a specific moment when the creator saw the new identity click for the first time.
4. Micro-case study with quiet numbers: "Jenna now calls herself a systems-first bookkeeper."
5. Lost-version story: what the creator would say to their Day-1 self. Same identity message, different angle.
6. The permission email: "If this identity doesn't fit, no harm done. Here's the unsubscribe link."

Writing rules for Phase 1:
- No mechanism teaching yet. Save it.
- No offer mention. Nothing to buy. The subscriber is deciding who they want to be, not what to purchase.
- Every email names the new identity in the exact same words.

### Phase 2 — Possibility (Emails 7 to 12)

**Teaching theme:** The new identity is achievable. The mechanism makes it achievable.

**Proof cadence:** Quiet mechanism reveals paired with proof that the mechanism works.

**CTA class:** Back to content that explains the mechanism deeper. Still not a purchase CTA.

Email patterns:
7. First mechanism reveal: name it, state the three to five sub-steps at a high level.
8. Sub-step one, taught at the conceptual level. Reader can name what it does.
9. Case study using sub-step one. Isomorphic to the ICP.
10. The anti-alternatives email: why common advice does not work, said specifically.
11. Sub-step two, taught at the conceptual level.
12. Case study using sub-steps one and two together.

Writing rules for Phase 2:
- Teach what the sub-steps do, not the full how-to. Full how-to lives in the paid offer.
- Every case study is isomorphic: same stage, same role, same core pain.
- Never compare by name to competitor mechanisms. Compare to "the usual way most people try this."

### Phase 3 — Specificity (Emails 13 to 19)

**Teaching theme:** Here is exactly what this looks like when it works. Here is exactly what it looks like when it does not.

**Proof cadence:** Dense, specific case studies. Named sub-steps. Named timelines. Named numbers.

**CTA class:** First warm CTA. Link to a longer-form asset like a free training, a VSL on the current state of the offer, or a community sign-up. Still not a purchase CTA.

Email patterns:
13. Sub-step three, taught with the first hands-on example.
14. Case study where the reader sees the full three sub-steps in a single client story.
15. Common failure mode #1, with the fix stated inside the mechanism.
16. Common failure mode #2, with the fix stated inside the mechanism.
17. The "what full activation looks like" email: a day in the life of someone after the mechanism is installed.
18. Case study #3, deliberately picked because the starting state is tougher than the ICP's average (proves the mechanism under load).
19. Warm CTA: free training, VSL link, or community invite. Makes the ask once. Moves on.

Writing rules for Phase 3:
- Specificity is the currency here. Vague numbers break the phase. "About six weeks" fails. "Forty-one days" works.
- Never fabricate the specificity. If the real number is vague, use the real number or swap the case study.
- The warm CTA in Email 19 is optional for the reader. Frame it as optional explicitly.

### Phase 4 — Permission (Emails 20 to 26)

**Teaching theme:** You can trust this creator with the next step. Also: the cost of waiting is real.

**Proof cadence:** Creator behind-the-scenes content, values statements, and quiet urgency tied to reader's own timeline.

**CTA class:** Warm to hot. Application link, booking calendar, VSL watch, cart if cart is open.

Email patterns:
20. Values email: three non-negotiable things the creator will not compromise on. Builds trust-person.
21. Behind-the-scenes: how the creator structures their week, or how a client session actually runs.
22. Reader letter: a long quote from a real client, with the creator's response.
23. The "why me" email: creator's edge, stated specifically. Not boastful. Factual.
24. The cost-of-waiting email: framed around the reader's timeline, not the creator's. "Another quarter like this one is another quarter at this income."
25. Soft offer framing: name the offer, name who it is for, name the next step. Do not stack urgency.
26. Warm CTA email: direct to the conversion asset (application, VSL, booking page, cart).

Writing rules for Phase 4:
- Never fake urgency. If no real deadline exists, the urgency lives in the reader's opportunity cost.
- Name the offer in Email 25 the same way it will be named on the landing page. Consistency of naming across the funnel compounds.
- The hot CTA in Email 26 is the first one that asks the reader to take a real step. If they do not click, they route to Phase 5.

### Phase 5 — Bridge (Emails 27 to 30)

**Teaching theme:** Stay-in content for the reader who is not ready now but is still engaged.

**Proof cadence:** Light. Case studies tied to timing.

**CTA class:** Soft. Back to a piece of content or an evergreen next step.

Email patterns:
27. The "not-yet-ready is fine" email: validates the reader who needs more time, reassures they belong on the list.
28. The "when you are ready" email: names the specific trigger that will make the reader know they are ready. "When you hit [milestone], this offer is here."
29. Weekly value reinforcement: a tactical idea they can use today. No pitch.
30. The handoff email: announces that the reader is now moving to the weekly broadcast cadence, not the sequence cadence. Sets expectation for ongoing contact.

Writing rules for Phase 5:
- Never pressure the non-converter. They are a 6-to-12-month lead, not a lost cause.
- Never resell the offer here. Save that for the next launch window.
- Phase 5 is optional; shorter 20-email sequences skip to weekly broadcast from Email 26.

## Subject Line Bank — 15 Ready-to-Adapt Patterns for Nurture

1. "I used to believe [common belief]. I was wrong."
2. "The thing nobody talks about at [stage]"
3. "[Case subject] hit [specific number]. Here's how."
4. "What [identity] actually looks like on a Tuesday"
5. "The three sub-steps of [mechanism]"
6. "Why [common advice] keeps failing"
7. "A reader asked me this yesterday"
8. "[Number] days. [Specific outcome]. The full breakdown."
9. "I almost didn't write this one"
10. "If [trigger] is happening, read this"
11. "The quiet version of [loud marketing term]"
12. "What I do every [day / week / month] that matters"
13. "Three things I would tell my Day-1 self"
14. "Here's the email I get most often"
15. "When you are ready, it will look like this"

## P.S. Bank — 10 Patterns Specific to Nurture

1. Pointer to last week's email if this email builds on it.
2. Pointer to a deep-dive content asset on the mechanism sub-step.
3. One-word reply prompt to raise ESP engagement signal.
4. A quiet reminder of where the subscriber is in the arc: "We're three weeks in. Still with me?"
5. Preview of next week's theme at a high level.
6. A question they can sit with between emails.
7. A quote from a case study that echoes the email's theme.
8. A pointer to the creator's best piece of public content on the topic.
9. The unsubscribe link, plainly stated, every 5 to 6 emails.
10. A thank-you line without sycophancy, once per phase maximum.

## Segmentation + Tag Logic

| Trigger | Tag Added | Tag Removed |
|---|---|---|
| Enter nurture sequence | nurture-active, nurture-phase-1 | welcome-active |
| Enter Phase 2 | nurture-phase-2 | nurture-phase-1 |
| Enter Phase 3 | nurture-phase-3 | nurture-phase-2 |
| Click warm CTA in Phase 3 | warm-mechanism | |
| Enter Phase 4 | nurture-phase-4 | nurture-phase-3 |
| Click hot CTA in Phase 4 | interested-offer, hot-lead | nurture-active |
| Reach Phase 5 without hot CTA click | nurture-bridge | nurture-phase-4 |
| Complete Phase 5 | weekly-broadcast | nurture-active, nurture-bridge |
| No opens for 30 consecutive days | cold-nurture | |

The tag map is what lets the launch sequence and the application sequence find hot leads without manual sorting.

## Cadence Fatigue Check

Nurture cadence averages every 2 to 4 days. Keep these rules to prevent fatigue collapse:
- No two emails in consecutive days except at the transition from Phase 4 to Phase 5 if a launch window opens.
- Pause broadcast list while the subscriber is in nurture. Resume at Phase 5 handoff.
- Audit the unsubscribe rate phase by phase. If any single phase exceeds 2 percent unsubscribe, pull the emails in that phase and rewrite before resuming.
- Run the full sequence through a plain-text reader once. If it reads like a chore, cut 20 percent.

## Benchmarks and What They Tell You

| Metric | Healthy Range | If Below | If Above |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 open rate | 45 to 55 percent | Wrong ICP in identity framing | Fine |
| Phase 2 open rate | 40 to 50 percent | Mechanism reveal landed wrong | Fine |
| Phase 3 click rate | 6 to 12 percent | Case studies not isomorphic | Fine |
| Phase 4 hot CTA click rate | 2 to 6 percent | Offer mismatch or trust not built | Offer fit is strong, scale traffic |
| Cumulative unsubscribe | 4 to 7 percent | Audience is unusually engaged | Cadence too heavy or content mismatched |
| Cold-nurture rate | 15 to 25 percent | Suspiciously low, check engagement | Normal, route to re-engagement later |

## Voice-Match and Compliance Rules Specific to Nurture

- Each email uses 3 or more `phrases_to_use` from Compartment 1.
- Long sequences burn through voice libraries. Refresh the voice library if the list exceeds 40 active `phrases_to_use` and the sequence still sounds repetitive.
- Every email body plus subject plus preview text passes the full banned-vocabulary sweep.
- No fake urgency. Urgency in Phase 4 comes from the reader's opportunity cost, not an invented deadline.
- No stacked testimonials in a single email. One case study per email, maximum.
- Every email carries the CAN-SPAM footer, physical address, and unsubscribe link.

## Common Failure Modes

1. Mechanism reveal happens in Email 2 instead of Phase 2. Subscribers learn the mechanism before they believe the new identity. They consume and leave. Fix: hold mechanism reveal until Phase 2.
2. Phase 3 case studies are not isomorphic. Reader thinks "that person is further along than me." Phase-3 click rate collapses. Fix: rank case studies by starting-state fit, not by impressiveness.
3. Urgency introduced in Phase 2 or 3. Sequence feels like a launch. Unsubscribe rate spikes in Phase 2. Fix: urgency is Phase 4 only, and it is framed around the reader's opportunity cost.
4. CTA in Phase 1 points at the cart. Nobody buys. Trust collapses. Fix: Phase 1 and Phase 2 CTAs only point to content.
5. Same subject line pattern across 10 consecutive emails. Open rate collapses around Email 12. Fix: rotate at least 5 different subject patterns across the sequence.
6. Phase 5 resells the offer. Non-converters unsubscribe at Email 29 or 30. Fix: Phase 5 is about staying engaged, not reselling.
7. Nurture running in parallel with weekly broadcast. Cadence doubles. Unsubscribe spikes early. Fix: pause broadcast during nurture; resume at Phase 5 handoff.
