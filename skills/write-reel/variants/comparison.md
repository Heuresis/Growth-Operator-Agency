# Reel Variant — Comparison

> **Signature move.** Two things put side-by-side — an expensive path vs. a cheap path, an old way vs. a new way, a common way vs. the creator's way, a competitor's method vs. ours. The tension is "which side is right." The resolution is the creator showing why one side wins on a specific dimension. The reel sells positioning, not a product.

## When to Use

- The creator's positioning depends on contrast — "we do it like this, everyone else does it like that."
- Pillar is Myth-Bust, Positioning, or Contrarian Take.
- Conversion stage is Consideration — the viewer is evaluating options and needs a frame.
- A specific, defensible difference exists. Comparison without a real difference reads as trash-talk.

## When NOT to Use

- The comparison is unfair or cherry-picked (comparing the creator's best case to the competitor's worst).
- The competitor is named and the comparison is legally risky (stick to category comparisons, not named-brand attacks).
- The creator's audience is already sold — comparison reels are for people shopping, not buyers.
- The two sides are so similar that the difference is hair-splitting — viewers tune out.

## Structure — 60-Second Grid

| Time | Beat | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:03 | Contrast Hook | Both sides named in one sentence — "$200K vs. $5K" or "The old way vs. my way" |
| 0:03-0:10 | Side A Setup | What most people do, one sentence of framing |
| 0:10-0:25 | Side A Specifics | 3 concrete points about Side A's approach, neutral tone |
| 0:25-0:40 | Side B Specifics | 3 concrete points about Side B's approach, creator's position |
| 0:40-0:50 | The Crux | The one dimension on which the comparison resolves |
| 0:50-0:58 | Takeaway Rule | What the viewer should take from the comparison |
| 0:58-1:00 | CTA or Loop | Single action or loop to opener |

## Hook Patterns (5 specific openers)

1. **Dollar-dollar contrast.** "$200,000 agency retainer vs. $5,000 fractional model. One wins on retention." Two numbers, one claim.
2. **Old-new contrast.** "The old way of selling a $10K offer was 8 sales calls. The new way is 1 DM and a Loom." Two methods, one outcome.
3. **Most-people vs. me contrast.** "Most coaches charge by the month. I charge per outcome. Here's why." Positioning contrast.
4. **Category-category contrast.** "SEO vs. paid ads for high-ticket. One pays off in year three. One pays off in month one." Trade-off comparison.
5. **Specific-entity vs. specific-entity (category safe).** "A $3K course vs. a $3K coaching program. Same price. Different economics." Comparing categories, not named brands.

## Body Patterns

### A. The Three-Point Parallel (default — symmetric)

Side A gets three bullets. Side B gets three bullets. Each bullet pair addresses the same dimension (cost / time / outcome / labor / scalability). Viewers can compare head-to-head.

- Dimension 1 — "Side A is X; Side B is Y"
- Dimension 2 — "Side A is X; Side B is Y"
- Dimension 3 — "Side A is X; Side B is Y"

### B. The David-vs-Goliath (for positioning against larger competitors)

Side A is the big, expensive, incumbent option. Side B is the creator's smaller, faster, cheaper option. The body frames Side B as the underdog and names the one thing David does that Goliath can't.

### C. The Before-Now Comparison (for showing creator's own evolution)

Side A is the creator's past self / past method. Side B is the current method. This removes the "hater" frame and reads as honest evolution. Works especially well for Consideration-stage audiences who have seen the creator's older content.

## CTA and Loop Patterns

- "Follow for more positioning breakdowns." — for Consideration-stage audiences.
- "Comment [word] and I'll send the comparison matrix." — data-capture-forward.
- Loop pattern — last line re-states the contrast hook with the resolved answer baked in. Viewers who replay feel they watched the conclusion first and the setup second.

## Voice-Match Requirements

- Three-plus `phrases_to_use` from Compartment 1 across the reel.
- Zero items from `spec/BANNED-VOCABULARY.md`.
- Comparison reels use the creator's actual philosophical position. If the creator doesn't hold a strong view on the dimension, the reel reads as manufactured. Voice-match includes position-match.

## Specificity Floor

Hook Specificity ≥ 8. The floor:

- Both sides named specifically (not "most marketers," but "the direct-response crowd") or with a number anchor ("agencies that charge $20K/month retainers").
- The dimension of comparison is named ("retention" / "time-to-revenue" / "unit economics"), not left implicit.
- The resolution is a specific claim, not a mushy "it depends."

## Visual Brief (scene-by-scene shot list)

- 0:00-0:03 — Split-screen graphic with both sides named. Creator voice-over.
- 0:03-0:10 — Cut to Side A visual (could be a competitor-style interface, a stock clip, a screenshot, or creator-on-camera describing).
- 0:10-0:25 — Side A specifics — three bullet points on screen, creator voice-over narrating each.
- 0:25-0:40 — Side B specifics — same visual treatment, text overlays swapped.
- 0:40-0:50 — Back to split-screen for the crux — the dimension that resolves.
- 0:50-0:58 — Creator on-camera for the takeaway rule.
- 0:58-1:00 — Closing split-screen card with CTA or loop-prompt.

## Platform Adaptations

- Instagram Reels: split-screen works well natively in 9:16 — stack Side A on top, Side B on bottom. Save-optimized because the split graphic is visually memorable.
- TikTok: pattern interrupt at 0:00-0:01 — both sides flash simultaneously in one frame. TikTok tolerates the fast visual setup.
- YouTube Shorts: title is the comparison explicit. "Agency retainer vs. fractional — which wins on retention" — the Shorts title is the hook literal.

## Compliance Check

- Banned vocabulary swept.
- Fair comparison — not cherry-picked.
- If named brands / named people are mentioned, the claims are factually defensible (no defamation risk).
- If the creator is a competitor's ex-employee or has a conflict of interest, disclosed.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Straw-man Side A.** The creator makes Side A look stupider than it actually is. Viewers who know the category spot this immediately and trust drops.
- **Fake dimension.** The comparison is set up so Side B always wins, but only on a dimension nobody cares about. Pick the dimension that matters.
- **Hair-splitting comparison.** The two sides differ by 5%. The viewer can't care. Only compare when the difference is 2x or more on a real dimension.
- **Too many dimensions.** Trying to compare 7 things in 60 seconds flattens everything. Stick to 3 bullets per side.
- **Personal attack disguised as comparison.** The reel reads as "everyone else is stupid, I'm smart." Viewers rebel against this frame. Keep the opponent side honest.

## Worked Example 1 — Agency vs. Fractional Retainer

```
0:00-0:03 | Split-screen graphic: "$20K/mo agency" | "$5K/mo fractional"
VO: "Twenty thousand a month agency vs. five thousand a month fractional. One wins on retention. Here's why."

0:03-0:10 | Cut to agency-style visuals (office, email chain, deck).
VO: "Most scaling founders default to agencies. Predictable, expensive, the whole deliverable."

0:10-0:25 | Three bullets on Side A.
TEXT: "Agency: 8-week ramp, 3-month retention, high switching cost"
VO: "Eight-week ramp before results. Average retention around three months. High switching cost because the agency owns the data."

0:25-0:40 | Three bullets on Side B.
TEXT: "Fractional: 2-week ramp, 14-month retention, full transparency"
VO: "Fractional: two-week ramp. Retention closer to fourteen months. The operator lives inside your stack, not theirs."

0:40-0:50 | Back to split-screen, zoomed to "retention" comparison.
VO: "Retention is the whole game. The long-term cost of a three-month retention is eight onboardings a year. Fractional is one."

0:50-0:58 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule: if retention is the constraint, fractional wins on every math sheet that matters."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow for more positioning teardowns"
```

## Re-Engage Pattern

- T+24h pinned comment: a specific counterargument to the reel's position, addressed thoughtfully. Invites debate without flame-wars.
- T+48h follow-up reel: a "part 2" addressing the most common objection in the comments.
- T+7d long-form: a YouTube teardown of the comparison with 10-15 minutes of depth.

## Hand-off to Editor

- Split-screen graphic style defined (horizontal vs. vertical stack).
- Text overlay timing synced to each bullet.
- Visual assets for Side A and Side B — avoid generic stock; use footage of actual workflows.
- Color coding if used (Side A one color family, Side B another) applied consistently.
