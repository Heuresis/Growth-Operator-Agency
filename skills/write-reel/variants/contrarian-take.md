# Reel Variant — Contrarian Take

> **Signature move.** The reel opens with a direct challenge to a piece of conventional wisdom the audience holds. The tension is "wait, everyone says the opposite." The resolution is a specific, defensible reason why the convention is wrong, backed by a concrete counter-example. The reel sells the creator's judgment, not a product.

## When to Use

- The creator has a defensible, specific counter-position to something the audience already believes.
- Pillar is Contrarian Take, Myth-Bust, or Positioning.
- Conversion stage is Awareness (to attract skeptics who are tired of the orthodox take) or Consideration (to plant a frame before the pitch).
- The creator is willing to lose some followers. Contrarian reels polarize — that is the mechanism.

## When NOT to Use

- The counter-position is not actually defensible (it's just a hot take for engagement). Audience spots this within a few posts and punishes the account.
- The creator is conflict-averse in real life and can't handle the comments. If the creator won't reply to pushback, the reel backfires.
- The convention being challenged is a norm with strong moral weight (anti-consumerist takes, political takes, anything that reads as punching down). Creator risks the account.
- The contrarian position is already the dominant take in the creator's specific niche — it's not actually contrarian.

## Structure — 60-Second Grid

| Time | Beat | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:03 | Challenge Hook | One sentence that negates the convention, specifically |
| 0:03-0:08 | Name the Convention | State what "everyone" says, neutrally |
| 0:08-0:18 | Why the Convention Fails | The specific reason the convention breaks |
| 0:18-0:35 | The Counter-Position | What the creator believes instead |
| 0:35-0:50 | Proof / Counter-Example | A concrete case that backs the counter-position |
| 0:50-0:58 | Takeaway Rule | The reframe the viewer should carry |
| 0:58-1:00 | CTA or Loop | Invite argument or follow-for-more |

## Hook Patterns (5 specific openers)

1. **Direct negation.** "Waking up at 5 AM is not the reason successful founders succeed. It's the reason they burn out by year four."
2. **Tool / tactic disown.** "I stopped using goal-setting in 2023. My revenue doubled. Here's why goals were the problem."
3. **Identity challenge.** "If you're a high-ticket coach charging by the month, you don't own a business. You own a job."
4. **Timing reversal.** "Most advice says launch when you're ready. I'm saying the opposite — launch before you're ready, or you'll never launch."
5. **Authority-puncturing.** "Every sales book says start with pain. Start with outcome. Pain is how you keep people, not how you win them."

## Body Patterns

### A. Convention → Specific Failure → Counter (default)

- State the convention cleanly, without straw-manning.
- Name the specific place the convention fails — a named stage of business, a named audience type, a named outcome.
- Offer the counter-position with equal specificity.
- Back with one concrete case.

### B. The "I Used to Believe X" Structure (personal credibility move)

- The creator admits they used to hold the convention.
- The specific moment / event that changed their mind.
- What they believe now and why.

This version defuses the "who are you to challenge the orthodoxy" attack because the creator already internalized the orthodoxy before rejecting it.

### C. The Category-Error Reveal

- State the convention.
- Show the convention is a category error — it's solving a problem the viewer doesn't actually have.
- Reframe the real problem.
- Solve the real problem.

Works best when the contrarian take is nuanced and needs the viewer to update their mental model, not just swap sides.

## CTA and Loop Patterns

- "Follow if you like takes like this." — explicit polarization invite.
- "Comment your position. I'll reply to the sharpest one." — invites engagement directly.
- "Agree? Disagree? Comment below." — weak; use only if creator's audience engages on prompts.
- Loop pattern — last line restates the original contrarian claim, amplified by the body's proof.

## Voice-Match Requirements

- Three-plus `phrases_to_use` from Compartment 1.
- Zero items from `spec/BANNED-VOCABULARY.md`.
- Voice must match the creator's real tone. A creator who is naturally diplomatic cannot suddenly write sharp contrarian takes — reads as performative. If the creator doesn't naturally hold contrarian positions, skip this variant.

## Specificity Floor

Hook Specificity ≥ 8. The floor:

- The convention being challenged is named specifically ("5 AM wake-up" not "morning routines").
- The failure mode is named specifically ("burnout by year four" not "long-term problems").
- The counter-position is specific and actionable ("launch before you're ready" not "just start").

## Visual Brief

- 0:00-0:03 — Creator on-camera, direct-to-lens. No b-roll. The delivery is the hook.
- 0:03-0:08 — Cut to a visual of the convention (a book cover blurred, a conference slide, a typical influencer post) with a red cross-through graphic.
- 0:08-0:18 — Creator on-camera naming the failure. Text overlay highlights the failure mode.
- 0:18-0:35 — Split-screen or sequential cuts showing the counter-position with visual anchors.
- 0:35-0:50 — Proof visual — a case study, a Stripe row, a before/after comparison.
- 0:50-0:58 — Creator on-camera close for the takeaway rule.
- 0:58-1:00 — Text-overlay CTA inviting argument.

## Platform Adaptations

- Instagram Reels: caption is the hook expanded. Saves and shares are the primary signal; comments are secondary (IG tamps down heated comment sections).
- TikTok: contrarian reels print on TikTok because the FYP algorithm loves polarization. First 0.5 seconds must be the negation phrase visually.
- YouTube Shorts: title is the contrarian position literal. "Waking up at 5 AM is burning you out" as the title. The description includes the long-form version link.

## Compliance Check

- Banned vocabulary swept.
- The counter-position is defensible (would survive a debate, not just a soundbite).
- No named-person attacks unless the creator is prepared for legal risk.
- No defamation — "everyone who does X is dumb" crosses the line.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Hot take without substance.** The reel negates the convention but never offers a defensible reason. Audience smells "trying to be edgy" within three seconds.
- **Strawman convention.** Creator misrepresents the convention to make it easier to attack. Viewers who know the original take push back in comments.
- **Counter-position is just the convention in different words.** "Don't set goals — set targets." Viewers notice the substitution and disengage.
- **Missing proof.** Contrarian takes require a concrete counter-example. Without it, the claim is "trust me." Viewers don't.
- **Performative contrarianism.** The creator takes a contrarian position because it's a growth hack, not because they believe it. Detected fast. Kills the account's long-term credibility.

## Worked Example 1 — The 5 AM Take

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator direct-to-lens.
VO: "Waking up at five AM is not why successful founders succeed. It's why they burn out by year four."

0:03-0:08 | Quick cut to stock footage of sunrise / alarm clock / productivity content.
VO: "Every productivity book tells you to wake up at five. Get the jump on the day."

0:08-0:18 | Creator on-camera.
TEXT: "The convention breaks at year 4"
VO: "Here's where it breaks. If you're running a business that already demands fourteen-hour days, adding ninety minutes of pre-dawn work doesn't make you sharper. It shortens your career."

0:18-0:35 | Cut to a series of b-roll clips — founder sleeping normally, founder exercising mid-morning, founder working a normal window.
TEXT: "Sleep when you're tired. Work when you're sharp."
VO: "What actually works is treating your energy like a budget, not a virtue. Sleep when you're tired. Work when you're sharp. Which for most people is eight AM, not five."

0:35-0:50 | Stripe / business dashboard b-roll from a specific creator who rejected the 5 AM frame.
VO: "I know three founders in the eight-figure range who sleep until eight or nine. They work in windows where their brain actually produces judgment calls that matter."

0:50-0:58 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule: founders don't win on wake-up time. They win on sustained sharp decisions over a decade. Burnout is cheating yourself out of the decade."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow if you like takes like this"
```

## Worked Example 2 — The Goal-Setting Reversal

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "I stopped setting goals in twenty-twenty-three. Revenue doubled. Here's why goals were the problem."

0:03-0:08 | Cut to journal / goal-setting worksheet b-roll.
VO: "Every business book tells you to set annual goals. SMART goals. Big hairy goals. Stretch goals."

0:08-0:18 | Creator on-camera.
TEXT: "Goals optimize for arrival. Not for staying"
VO: "Here's the problem. Goals are destination markers. They optimize for arrival. They do not optimize for what the business needs to be doing every Tuesday."

0:18-0:35 | Text-driven sequence.
TEXT: "Constraints, not goals"
VO: "What works instead — constraints. Not 'ten million by December.' Instead: 'I will only do work that compounds. I will only take meetings that replace tasks. I will only sell outcomes, not hours.' Constraints change what today looks like. Goals only change what December looks like."

0:35-0:50 | Before/after dashboard b-roll.
VO: "I ran the business on three constraints for twelve months. Revenue went from a flat line to doubled. Goals had been on the wall for five years and nothing moved."

0:50-0:58 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule: if the Tuesday doesn't change, the December doesn't change. Constraints change Tuesdays. Goals don't."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Comment YES if you're dropping goals. NO if you're keeping them"
```

## Worked Example 3 — The Job-Not-a-Business Challenge

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator direct-to-lens, tight frame.
VO: "If you're a high-ticket coach charging by the month, you don't own a business. You own a job. Here's the difference."

0:03-0:08 | Cut to b-roll of calendar full of recurring calls.
VO: "The monthly coaching model — everyone sells it. Recurring revenue, predictable cash, sounds like a business."

0:08-0:18 | Creator on-camera.
TEXT: "A business runs without the founder. A job does not"
VO: "Here's the test. Can you take sixty days off? If the answer is no, you own a job with monthly billing. That's not an insult. It's an accounting fact."

0:18-0:35 | Split-screen sequence showing "job" model vs. "business" model.
TEXT: "Job: you are the service | Business: the service runs"
VO: "In a job, the founder is the deliverable. In a business, the deliverable exists independent of the founder. The test of which one you own is what happens when you're not there."

0:35-0:50 | Cut to creator's own business metrics during a 60-day period off.
VO: "I took sixty days off last year. Revenue held. Onboardings continued. Retention held. That's only possible because the service is productized, not founder-gated."

0:50-0:58 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule: if the founder leaves for sixty days and cash stops, the founder owns a job. Fine. But name it what it is. That's the first step to fixing it."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow for more positioning teardowns"
```

## Worked Example 4 — Launch-Before-Ready

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "Most advice says launch when you're ready. I'm saying the opposite. Launch before you're ready — or you'll never launch."

0:03-0:08 | Quick cut to planning-docs b-roll.
VO: "Every consultant tells founders to get the brand right, the website right, the messaging right before launching."

0:08-0:18 | Creator on-camera.
TEXT: "Ready is a moving target"
VO: "Ready is a moving target. The standards rise as you approach them. Every fix reveals two new gaps. Founders who wait for ready wait for a number that doesn't exist."

0:18-0:35 | Text-driven sequence.
TEXT: "The two-date rule"
VO: "What works instead — pick two dates. First sale date. First public launch date. First sale is when a single paying customer exists. Public launch is thirty days later. You do not wait for perfect. You ship between those two dates, and you fix in market."

0:35-0:50 | Before/after sequence — a specific creator's launch that shipped early vs. one that waited.
VO: "I know one founder who waited fourteen months for ready. Zero customers when they launched. Another who shipped in two weeks. Fourteen customers in the first month."

0:50-0:58 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule: readiness doesn't create customers. Shipping creates readiness."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Comment your two dates"
```

## Worked Example 5 — Start With Outcome, Not Pain

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera, tight frame.
VO: "Every sales book says start with pain. Start with outcome. Pain is how you keep people. Not how you win them."

0:03-0:08 | Quick cut to b-roll of sales books.
VO: "The pain-first school dominates sales training. Agitate the problem, then offer the solution."

0:08-0:18 | Creator on-camera.
TEXT: "Pain-first attracts pain-tolerant buyers"
VO: "Here's the problem. Pain-first messaging attracts people who are comfortable talking about pain. That's not your best customer. Your best customer is pattern-recognizing the outcome before they name the pain."

0:18-0:35 | Sequence showing two ads — pain-first vs. outcome-first.
TEXT: "Outcome-first attracts the pattern-recognizer"
VO: "Outcome-first messaging — 'three discovery calls a week, closed by Friday' — attracts someone who wants the specific number. The pain is inferred. They don't need to be told they're struggling. They're too busy pattern-matching to the outcome."

0:35-0:50 | Dashboard comparison showing CTR on outcome-first vs. pain-first ad.
VO: "I A/B-tested this for six months. Outcome-first outperformed pain-first by roughly thirty percent in CVR. Not because pain doesn't work. Because outcome-first attracts the buyer who was going to pay."

0:50-0:58 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule: pain is the nurture channel. Outcome is the acquisition channel. Most founders confuse them."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow for more copy-strategy teardowns"
```

## Re-Engage Pattern

- T+0 to T+90 min: creator actively replies to the first 20 comments. This signals the position is genuinely held, not posted-and-abandoned.
- T+24h pinned comment: a specific clarification addressing the most common misread ("to be clear, I'm not saying X, I'm saying Y").
- T+48h follow-up reel: "the response to my take on X" — synthesizes the comments into a second reel.
- T+7d long-form: a YouTube video that argues the position at 12-20 minutes of depth, linked from the reel.

## Hand-off to Editor

- Direct-to-lens shots are primary — contrarian reels live on delivery, not production value.
- Text overlays are the convention (crossed out) and the counter-position (underlined / boxed).
- Music is minimal or silent — the words carry.
- Pacing is slightly slower than other reel variants — viewers need to process a reframe, not absorb information.
