# Reel Variant — Behind the Scenes

> **Signature move.** The reel takes the viewer inside a moment most creators sanitize or hide — the actual workflow, the specific tool, the mid-day chaos, the mess of a specific decision, the embarrassing version of an email the creator sent before the polished one. Tension is "this is more real than what I see on other feeds." The reel sells trust and humanness, not a product.

## When to Use

- The creator has authority but feels remote to the audience — Behind the Scenes closes the distance.
- Pillar is Lifestyle, Process, or Personality.
- Conversion stage is Consideration — the viewer has seen the polished content and wants the unvarnished version before they buy.
- The creator is comfortable showing imperfection. If the creator is brand-obsessed about sanitized content, this variant fights the voice.

## When NOT to Use

- Showing behind-scenes would violate client privacy (client faces, client revenue, unrleased launches).
- The "mess" is so unprofessional it would erode trust (actual chaos, not charming chaos).
- The creator is in a regulated niche where the behind-scenes involves protected information (finance, law, medical).
- The behind-scenes is literally just a studio shot of the creator recording content — that is meta-content about content, which doesn't scratch the itch.

## Structure — 60-Second Grid

| Time | Beat | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00-0:03 | Access Hook | "Here's what [specific polished output] actually looks like behind the scenes" |
| 0:03-0:10 | The Expected Version | Quick show of what the audience usually sees |
| 0:10-0:30 | The Actual Version | The unpolished version — messy workspace, first-draft email, real decision moment |
| 0:30-0:45 | The Tension / Surprise | The one detail that is not what the audience would expect |
| 0:45-0:55 | The Rule Revealed | What the behind-scenes actually teaches about how the work gets done |
| 0:55-0:58 | Normalize / Humanize | One line that gives permission to the viewer |
| 0:58-1:00 | CTA or Loop | Follow or re-state opener |

## Hook Patterns (5 specific openers)

1. **The "what this actually looks like" hook.** "This is what a forty-thousand-dollar sales call actually looks like five minutes before it starts." Specific outcome + behind-scenes framing.
2. **The draft-vs-published hook.** "The polished version of this email went out yesterday. Here's the draft I wrote at 11 PM the night before."
3. **The decision-moment hook.** "I have to decide whether to close enrollment today or extend forty-eight hours. Here's the argument I'm having with my team."
4. **The workspace hook.** "Here's the actual workspace where I write every email that ships to fifty thousand people. It's not aspirational."
5. **The routine-breakdown hook.** "Everyone asks what my morning routine looks like. Here's Tuesday. No filter."

## Body Patterns

### A. Polished-vs-Raw Contrast (default)

- Show the published / polished version for 5-7 seconds. The viewer recognizes it.
- Cut to the raw version — the same artifact or moment, but unedited.
- Spend 15-20 seconds on the raw version with text-overlay commentary.
- End with one detail that surprises — the one thing the polished version hid.

### B. Real-Time Decision Narration

- Creator in the middle of an actual decision — not recreated, ideally filmed in the moment or same-day.
- Voice-over explains what's being weighed.
- End with the decision made and the reasoning.

Works for launch-day decisions, pricing decisions, hiring decisions, firing decisions.

### C. Day-In-The-Life Vignette

- 3-5 short scenes (8-12 seconds each) from an actual day.
- Each scene is named with a text overlay.
- Ends with the one moment that defines the work for the creator.

Not the same as "Day in Life" variant (which is full vlog-style); this is an abbreviated form focused on the work-reality contrast.

## CTA and Loop Patterns

- "Follow for more unfiltered." — direct invitation.
- "Comment if this resonates." — soft engagement for community-stage audiences.
- "DM me if you want the full template I use." — works when the behind-scenes shows a specific asset.
- Loop pattern — last frame returns to the polished version briefly, creating a full circle from "expected" to "actual" back to "expected."

## Voice-Match Requirements

- Three-plus `phrases_to_use` from Compartment 1.
- Zero items from `spec/BANNED-VOCABULARY.md`.
- Behind-scenes voice-over is typically looser / more casual than the creator's polished content. Keep voice-match by staying within the creator's natural informal register, not inventing one.

## Specificity Floor

Hook Specificity ≥ 8. The floor:

- The polished output being "behind-scened" is named specifically — which email, which launch, which call.
- The timestamp is specific — which day, which moment.
- The detail that surprises is specific — not "it's messy" but "I had seven tabs open and a cold coffee."

## Visual Brief

- 0:00-0:03 — Creator on-camera or voice-over over a familiar "polished" image (a cover photo, a published email screenshot, a product-page screenshot).
- 0:03-0:10 — Full visual of the polished version for recognition.
- 0:10-0:30 — Cut to raw footage — handheld camera, desk shot, screen recording of the draft, voice memo of the actual conversation (with permission).
- 0:30-0:45 — Zoom / highlight the surprising detail.
- 0:45-0:55 — Creator on-camera or voice-over with the rule.
- 0:55-0:58 — Normalizing line — creator on-camera, direct-to-lens.
- 0:58-1:00 — Text-overlay CTA.

## Platform Adaptations

- Instagram Reels: works well because IG audiences expect some behind-scenes content. Caption is a short "honest version" of the polished caption.
- TikTok: behind-scenes reels print on TikTok because the FYP rewards authenticity signals. Keep raw footage truly raw — no polish passes.
- YouTube Shorts: title frames the contrast explicitly. "What a $40K sales call actually looks like beforehand" — Shorts title is the access claim.

## Compliance Check

- Banned vocabulary swept.
- No client privacy violations — client faces, client names, client numbers not shown unless permission is on file.
- No confidential business information (upcoming launches, price changes) shown.
- If the creator is in a regulated niche, the behind-scenes does not expose protected information.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Too performative.** The "raw" version is actually staged — perfectly lit, composed, scripted. Viewers smell performance within 3 seconds and the reel loses its premise.
- **Boring raw version.** The behind-scenes is not actually interesting — just creator at a desk. If the raw version is mundane, the reel fails. Pick a moment with genuine tension or surprise.
- **No rule revealed.** The reel shows the mess but doesn't teach anything. Every Behind the Scenes should end with a concrete rule or reframe.
- **Privacy risk.** The creator shows a client's face, a client's number, a client's name without permission. Even if it works for the reel, it erodes creator-client trust long-term.
- **Over-messy.** The raw version is so chaotic it reads as unprofessional. Charming-chaos has a floor: the work is still working, just not sanitized.

## Worked Example 1 — Sales Call Prep

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera, half-dressed for a call, tight frame.
TEXT: "$40K sales call. 5 minutes from now."
VO: "This is what a forty-thousand-dollar sales call looks like five minutes before it starts."

0:03-0:10 | Cut to the creator's polished-looking LinkedIn profile photo.
VO: "You usually see this version of me."

0:10-0:25 | Cut to handheld footage of creator's actual pre-call workspace — sticky notes, coffee, half-eaten snack, three monitors open.
TEXT: "3 monitors. 7 tabs. Cold coffee."
VO: "Here's what the five minutes actually look like. Left monitor — the prospect's LinkedIn. Center — my pre-call checklist. Right — the last three emails they sent. I'm reading all three one more time."

0:25-0:40 | Zoom to creator's pre-call checklist — handwritten, 4 bullet points.
TEXT: "Pre-call list — 4 items, every time"
VO: "Four items on the list every time. The specific outcome they want. The one objection I expect. The case study that matches their stage. The number I'll quote if they ask."

0:40-0:50 | Cut to creator's face — about to start the call.
VO: "Thirty seconds to go. Phone goes on silent. Tabs except prospect profile get closed. Water on desk."

0:50-0:55 | Creator on-camera, direct-to-lens.
VO: "The rule — a forty-thousand-dollar call is won in the five minutes before it starts. Not during."

0:55-0:58 | Normalize line.
VO: "Not glamorous. Effective."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow for more unfiltered"
```

## Worked Example 2 — Polished Email vs. Draft

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The polished version of this email went out yesterday. Here's the draft I wrote at eleven PM the night before."

0:03-0:10 | Cut to the published email in the email platform — clean, formatted, well-designed.
VO: "This is what fifty thousand people got in their inbox."

0:10-0:30 | Cut to a screenshot of the actual draft — typos, crossed-out phrases, notes to self, placeholder headlines.
TEXT: "Draft 1. 11:04 PM. Not good."
VO: "Here's draft one. I wrote this at eleven PM. Look — placeholder headline. Three sentences struck through. A note to myself that says 'is this the story or is the pricing the story.' I hadn't even decided what the email was about."

0:30-0:45 | Zoom to the key change from draft to final.
TEXT: "The only real change"
VO: "The only real change between draft one and what shipped — the subject line. The body stayed ninety percent the same. The subject line changed four times. The last change was made three minutes before send."

0:45-0:55 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule — the body is the product. The subject is the title of the product. Most writers over-polish bodies and under-test subjects. Reverse it."

0:55-0:58 | Normalize line.
VO: "I'm not writing better than you. I'm just shipping drafts and testing subjects."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Comment SUBJECT for the 4 subject lines I tested"
```

## Worked Example 3 — Cart-Close Decision

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera at desk, Slack or messaging-app screen visible over shoulder.
VO: "Cart closes in four hours. My team is arguing whether we should extend."

0:03-0:10 | Cut to a stock-style "launch mode" image to set context.
VO: "Usually you'd see the numbers post-launch. You'd never see the argument."

0:10-0:25 | Cut to Slack / messaging app showing a real thread between creator and two team members — names redacted, content authentic.
TEXT: "Team split 2-1"
VO: "Head of ops wants to extend twelve hours. We're short of the stretch target. Copy lead wants to hold the cart-close exactly as announced. I'm here trying to decide between the revenue and the promise."

0:25-0:45 | Creator on-camera, thinking aloud.
TEXT: "The rule that wins this"
VO: "The rule that wins this — once. An extension happens once. Trust on the list is permanent. If I extend today to hit a stretch target, every close I announce for the rest of my career is a negotiation. The cost of extending is not the hours. It's the permanent discount on my word."

0:45-0:55 | Text on screen: "Decision: hold."
VO: "I'm holding the cart-close. I'll miss the stretch target. Next launch I'll build a bigger runway."

0:55-0:58 | Creator direct-to-lens.
VO: "The rule matters more than the number."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow for more launch decisions in real time"
```

## Worked Example 4 — The Actual Workspace

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera in their real workspace.
VO: "This is the workspace where every email I ship to fifty thousand people gets written. It's not aspirational."

0:03-0:10 | Cut to stock "aspirational workspace" images — clean desk, expensive lamp, plants.
VO: "You usually see workspaces that look like this."

0:10-0:30 | Hand-held tour of the creator's actual workspace — messy desk, papers, cables, mismatched monitors, a cat blanket in the background.
TEXT: "3 monitors from 3 different years"
VO: "Three monitors from three different years. A fourteen-dollar desk lamp I've had since college. A notebook I've had for four months. No plants. No aesthetic objects. The desk chair is the most expensive thing in the room."

0:30-0:45 | Zoom to the one thing that's actually optimized.
TEXT: "The only thing that matters: the chair"
VO: "The one thing that matters — the chair. I sit here for six hours a day. Everything else is decoration. The chair is the decision that changes how much good work happens."

0:45-0:55 | Creator on-camera.
VO: "The rule — optimize the thing your body touches for six hours a day. Everything else is aesthetic."

0:55-0:58 | Normalize.
VO: "The content isn't better because the room looks better."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Follow for more anti-aspirational"
```

## Worked Example 5 — Tuesday, No Filter

```
0:00-0:03 | Creator on-camera, clearly mid-day, slightly tired.
TEXT: "Tuesday. No filter."
VO: "Everyone asks what my morning routine looks like. Here's Tuesday."

0:03-0:10 | Quick cut of aspirational morning-routine content — someone meditating, green juice, 5 AM gym.
VO: "You usually see something like this."

0:10-0:35 | Real footage from the creator's morning — phone alarm going off late, coffee being made while half-asleep, breakfast that's just toast, work desk with three urgent tabs open.
TEXT: "7:42 AM. Alarm didn't work. Toast. 3 urgent tabs."
VO: "Alarm snoozed twice. Coffee is whatever's left in the pot. Breakfast is toast. I'm at the desk by seven forty-two with three urgent tabs already open because one of them is an email I forgot to send yesterday."

0:35-0:50 | Creator on-camera.
TEXT: "The actual rule"
VO: "Here's the actual rule. The morning doesn't matter as much as the first ninety minutes of work. Which for me is nine to ten-thirty. That's where the day gets made. Everything before that is logistics."

0:50-0:58 | Creator direct-to-lens.
VO: "The morning routine isn't the moat. The ninety-minute block is."

0:58-1:00 | CTA.
TEXT: "Comment BLOCK for how to set yours up"
```

## Re-Engage Pattern

- T+24h pinned comment: the creator shares one more behind-scenes detail not in the reel.
- T+48h follow-up reel: "the reaction to yesterday" — often converts behind-scenes into sequences.
- T+7d long-form: a full workflow video on YouTube that expands the behind-scenes into a 15-minute walkthrough.

## Hand-off to Editor

- Raw footage should stay raw — avoid color grading that sanitizes the lighting.
- Text overlays are hand-written style if that matches the creator's brand.
- Music is ambient or silent — the behind-scenes should feel quiet, not produced.
- No fast cuts — the pacing is intentionally slower than other variants.
