# X Thread Variant — Case Study Thread

> **Signature move.** A specific client outcome told as a narrative across 8-14 tweets. The thread opens with the outcome (dollar figure, time compression, identity shift), walks through the starting situation and the specific decisions, and closes with the rule that generalizes. The thread sells proof, positioning, and authority — with the case study as the evidence.

## When to Use

- Creator has a specific, documented client outcome with permission to share.
- Pillar is Case Study, Proof, or Positioning.
- Conversion stage is Consideration or Decision.
- Case study is isomorphic to the target audience.

## When NOT to Use

- No permission from the client, even anonymized.
- Case study is the creator's biggest outcome but non-isomorphic to most readers.
- Outcome is still unfolding — case study threads need a clean ending.
- Numbers or details can't be shown without breaking client confidentiality.

## Opener (Hook Tweet)

Case study hooks lead with the outcome.

Opener patterns:

1. **Dollar-outcome hook.** "A SaaS founder went from $40K MRR to $128K MRR in 90 days. Same product. Different funnel. Full breakdown:"
2. **Time-compression hook.** "She closed $180K in 11 days after 18 months of being stuck below $10K/mo:"
3. **Identity-shift hook.** "A freelancer became a $30K/mo retainer agency in 7 months. Full teardown:"
4. **Counter-intuitive outcome hook.** "He raised his prices 3x and close rate went UP, not down. Here's exactly what happened:"
5. **Comparison hook.** "Same niche. Same starting stage. One 10x'd in a year. One stayed flat. Here's the decision they made differently:"

Hook tweet rules:

- Outcome specific and upfront.
- Named client role (anonymized or named with permission).
- 150-220 characters.
- Teaser of what readers will learn — "full breakdown," "exactly what happened," "the decision."

## Body Structure

### The Chronological Narrative (default)

- Tweet 2: Starting situation — specific numbers, specific stage.
- Tweet 3: The bottleneck — what was broken before.
- Tweet 4-7: The decisions — one per tweet, each with context.
- Tweet 8-9: The execution — what actually shipped.
- Tweet 10: The setback — what went wrong mid-way.
- Tweet 11-12: The outcome — specific numbers, specific timeframe.
- Tweet 13: The rule that generalizes.
- Tweet 14 (final): CTA.

### The Decision-First Structure

- Tweet 2-4: The three decisions that drove the outcome (one per tweet).
- Tweet 5-7: Context for why those decisions worked.
- Tweet 8-9: The numbers at each stage.
- Tweet 10-11: What could have gone wrong.
- Tweet 12: The generalizable rule.
- Tweet 13 (final): CTA.

### The Pattern-Frame Structure (for multi-client patterns)

- Tweet 2: The pattern observed across multiple clients.
- Tweet 3-5: Three specific clients that fit the pattern.
- Tweet 6-8: The decisions each one made.
- Tweet 9-10: The common outcome.
- Tweet 11: The rule.
- Tweet 12 (final): CTA.

## Bookmark-Bait Patterns

- **The numbers tweet.** One middle tweet (usually tweet 7 or 8) compresses the before / after numbers in a clean format. This tweet gets screenshot-bookmarked.
- **The decision-list tweet.** A tweet that names all 3-4 decisions as a numbered list. Readers save for reference.
- **The rule tweet.** The final synthesis tweet — the generalizable rule — is highly bookmarked when specific enough to apply.

## Re-Engage Structure

- **Reply-to-thread (T+24-48h).** Creator quotes own thread with the one detail that got cut — "fun fact that didn't fit in the thread."
- **Quote-tweet (T+48-72h).** The outcome teased again with a different angle — "the client almost quit this process on day 4. Here's what kept them in."
- **Related thread (T+7d).** A follow-up thread on a related case study using the same pattern — builds a "case study series" over weeks.

## Retweet Invitation

- The rule tweet (second-to-last) retweets most because it's the generalizable takeaway.
- The numbers tweet retweets second-most because it's the bookmark-screenshot.
- The specific decisions tweets retweet in the middle range.
- Hook and CTA tweets retweet least in case study format.

## CTA / Close

Single action.

- "Follow @[handle] for more case study teardowns like this."
- "DM 'CASE' and I'll send the template the client used."
- "What stage are you at? Drop it in the replies — I'll tear down a case from your stage next." (Engagement-forward.)

## Character Limits

- 280 max per tweet.
- 180-260 for body tweets.
- 150-220 for hook.

## Voice-Match Requirements

- 3+ `phrases_to_use` across the thread.
- Zero items from `spec/BANNED-VOCABULARY.md`.
- Narrative voice is reportorial — "here's what happened" — not performative.

## Specificity Floor

- Starting numbers specific.
- Each decision named specifically — not "improved funnel" but "replaced ebook with quiz."
- Outcome numbers specific.
- Timeline specific.
- Isomorphism explicit — named role, named stage.

## Worked Example 1 — SaaS Founder Teardown

```
Tweet 1 (HOOK):
A SaaS founder went from $40K MRR to $128K MRR in 90 days.

Same product. Same traffic budget. Different funnel.

Full teardown:

🧵

Tweet 2:
Starting state (Day 0):

• $40K MRR
• $3K/mo Meta ad spend
• 120 leads/week
• 2 qualified sales calls/week
• Close rate: 18%
• ACV: $6K
• Flat for 6 months

The funnel was leaking. Not traffic.

Tweet 3:
The bottleneck:

Leads → qualified-demo stage was dying.
Only 1.6% of leads converted to a qualified demo.

Industry median for this category: 6-8%.
He was at 1/4 of the floor.

Tweet 4:
Decision 1: Replaced the ebook lead magnet with a 7-question quiz.

Quiz output: one of three tiers.
- "Ready" → calendar
- "Close" → retargeted sequence
- "Not yet" → nurture

Qualified routing shot from 1.6% to 11%.

Tweet 5:
Decision 2: Rewrote the 8 primary ads.

Before: product-feature-first.
("The best tool for X.")

After: outcome-first.
("30 qualified demos a week without an SDR.")

CTR doubled. Application completion rate rose.

Tweet 6:
Decision 3: Founder became the closer for 60 days.

Previously, an SDR handed to AE.
Now, qualified apps → founder's calendar directly.

Close rate: 18% → 42%.
(Founder saw every objection. Product roadmap benefited.)

Tweet 7 (NUMBERS TWEET):
The 90-day transformation:

Day 0: $40K MRR, 2 calls/week, 18% close
Day 30: $64K MRR, 14 calls/week, 31% close
Day 60: $98K MRR, 22 calls/week, 38% close
Day 90: $128K MRR, 28 calls/week, 42% close

Same product. Same spend. Three decisions.

Tweet 8:
What went wrong:

Day 4 of the quiz launch.
Over-filtering — only 1 in 100 qualifying as "ready."
Lost ~6 possible applications before we caught it.

Loosened criteria Day 5. Routing normalized.

Tweet 9:
Why founder-closing matters:

At $40K-$80K MRR, the founder still needs to see every objection.
An SDR can't translate friction back into product decisions fast enough.

60 days of founder-closing = 60 days of product feedback at zero delay.

Tweet 10:
The decision order mattered:

Quiz (Decision 1) before the ads (Decision 2).
Outcome ads would have flooded a still-broken routing layer.

Founder-closing (Decision 3) came last. By then, the pipeline was full enough to justify founder time.

Tweet 11 (RULE):
The rule:

Most SaaS funnels at $40K-$80K MRR are not traffic-limited.
They're routing-limited.

Quiz + outcome-ads + founder-closing is the fix when traffic exists but calls don't.

Tweet 12 (CTA):
Follow @[handle] for more teardowns.

I write one of these every week — real clients, real numbers.

DM "FUNNEL" and I'll send the quiz framework.
```

## Worked Example 2 — Identity Shift Teardown

```
Tweet 1 (HOOK):
A freelancer became a $30K/mo retainer agency in 7 months.

Three structural decisions. No new skills. No new audience.

Full breakdown:

🧵

Tweet 2:
Starting state:

• $8K/mo, solo
• All project-based
• 11 services on the menu
• Burned out on scope creep
• 4 years in, flat for 18 months

He wasn't behind. He was structured wrong.

Tweet 3:
The bottleneck:

Every new client meant renegotiating scope.
Every project finishing meant a revenue gap to fill.
His time was capped and his model resisted compounding.

Tweet 4:
Decision 1: Dropped hourly / project pricing.

Moved to outcome-based contracts.
Each deliverable priced at what the outcome was worth to the client.

Month 1 feel: unnerving.
Month 3 result: average deal size 2.4x.

Tweet 5:
Decision 2: Cut the 11-item service menu to 3.

Three productized services. Each with a named deliverable and fixed price range.

Referrals tripled in 60 days.
(Because now people could describe him in one sentence.)

Tweet 6:
Decision 3: Hired a setter before a delivery person.

Common mistake is hiring the delivery person first.
"But I'm at capacity."

Setter generated pipeline for the next 6 months.
Then the delivery hire was easy to justify.

Tweet 7 (NUMBERS TWEET):
The 7-month transformation:

Month 0: $8K/mo, solo, project-based
Month 2: $14K/mo, solo, outcome-priced
Month 4: $19K/mo, solo + setter
Month 6: $27K/mo, solo + setter + delivery
Month 7: $31K MRR on retainer

Tweet 8:
The retainer shift:

Month 5 — converted 4 existing outcome-based clients into recurring retainers.
$2-4K/mo each.

Revenue became compounding.
Pipeline pressure dropped.

Tweet 9:
What could have gone wrong:

Month 3, he almost went back to the old menu.
Two prospects wanted services that weren't in the new 3.

He said no to both. Held the line.
One came back in month 5 for one of the 3 services.

Tweet 10:
Why hire order matters:

Delivery-first: new hire sits idle, you burn cash.
Setter-first: pipeline grows, delivery hire is obvious.

Most freelancers-becoming-agencies flip this. Pay for months. Stall.

Tweet 11 (RULE):
The rule:

Freelancers don't scale by working more.
They scale by pricing for outcomes, productizing the menu, and hiring demand before supply.

No new skills required. Just structural rework.

Tweet 12 (CTA):
Follow @[handle] for more teardowns.

DM "AGENCY" and I'll send the 3-service menu template.
```

## Worked Example 3 — Counter-Intuitive Outcome

```
Tweet 1 (HOOK):
He raised his prices 3x.
Close rate went UP, not down.

Most pricing advice predicts the opposite. Here's what actually happened:

🧵

Tweet 2:
Starting state:

• Coach at $3K/mo, 1:1
• Close rate on discovery calls: 28%
• 12 clients active, room for 3-4 more
• Had been at $3K for 2 years

Tweet 3:
The price change:

$3K → $10K.
Announced on a Tuesday morning.
Existing clients grandfathered for 6 months.
All new prospects quoted at $10K.

Expected close rate drop: 28% → maybe 12-15%.

Tweet 4:
Actual outcome (60 days later):

Close rate: 28% → 41%.

Yes, higher.
Fewer calls, but more closes per call.

(The number I had projected was wrong by 3x in the opposite direction.)

Tweet 5:
What I think happened:

1. Calls now filtered for serious buyers.
   Tire-kickers self-selected out at the quote.

2. The higher price signaled higher quality.
   Buyers assumed the coaching was worth it.

3. Objections shifted from price to fit.
   Fit objections close faster than price objections.

Tweet 6:
The counter-intuitive math:

Old model:
20 calls/mo × 28% close × $3K = $16,800

New model:
11 calls/mo × 41% close × $10K = $45,100

2.7x revenue. 55% fewer calls.

Tweet 7 (NUMBERS TWEET):
Before / After:

Calls/mo: 20 → 11
Close rate: 28% → 41%
Price: $3K → $10K
Revenue: $16.8K → $45.1K

Fewer reps. Higher conversion. Larger deals.

Tweet 8:
Who this worked for:

• Coach with 2+ years of proof
• Existing waitlist of 12+ prospects
• Mechanism clear enough to justify premium

If any of those missing, close rate would have dropped.

Tweet 9:
What didn't help:

• A long sales script
• "Overcoming" price objections
• Discounts to warm leads

Buyers either wanted the $10K outcome or they didn't. No persuasion closed the gap.

Tweet 10 (RULE):
The rule:

Raising prices doesn't always drop close rate.
When the price matches the positioning + proof, higher price filters for better-fit buyers.

Close rate can rise.

Test with 3-5 cold prospects before announcing to the list.

Tweet 11 (CTA):
Follow @[handle] for more pricing teardowns.

DM "RAISE" for the pre-raise audit checklist.
```

## Compliance Check

- Banned vocabulary swept.
- Client permission on file.
- FTC disclosure if compensated / paid client testimonial.
- Numbers verifiable — creator has the underlying dashboard or can produce it.
- Anonymization consistent — if name redacted in one tweet, redacted throughout.
- No income guarantees ("this client did X" vs. "you can do X").

## Common Failure Modes

- **Non-isomorphic outcome.** Case is real but the audience is at a different stage. Reads as impressive but not applicable.
- **Generic decisions.** "He improved the funnel." Need specificity — "he replaced the ebook with a 7-question quiz."
- **Missing setback.** Thread reads too clean. Real case studies have friction. Include the Day-4 problem or equivalent.
- **Numbers without context.** "Revenue 3x'd" without the starting and ending anchors. Always include both sides.
- **Over-attribution.** Creator takes credit for work the client did. Share credit honestly.
- **Sanitized timeline.** "It took 90 days" when it actually took 6 months. Viewers smell timeline compression.
- **No rule tweet.** Ends on the numbers without extracting the generalizable lesson. Rule tweet is the thread's whole payoff.

## Hand-off Checklist

- Hook tweet leads with the specific outcome.
- 8-14 body tweets.
- Numbers tweet included (highly bookmarked).
- Decision tweets named specifically.
- Setback or "what could have gone wrong" included.
- Rule tweet at second-to-last position.
- CTA tweet — single action.
- Client permission form signed.
- Zero banned vocabulary.
- Voice-match verified.
