# Curation Thread — Format Playbook

> **When it wins:** You want to earn goodwill / reciprocity, demonstrate taste, and warm relationships with other creators. Low creative effort, high relationship-building effect.

## Structure

### Tweet 1 — Frame
Establish what the curation is about and why this one matters.

- Pattern: *"10 essays I've recommended more than any others in 2025 on [topic]:"*
- Pattern: *"The [N] best thinkers I follow on [domain] — each one changed how I think:"*
- Pattern: *"If you want to [outcome], these [N] resources are the only ones I'd send:"*

**Constraints:**
- ≤ 280 chars
- Set the criterion — why THESE resources made the cut
- Promise specificity; no "great resources" generic filler

### Tweets 2 to N-1 — The picks
Each pick = one tweet. Format:

```
[Handle or resource name]
One-line description of what they bring
What specifically the reader will get from following / reading
[Optional: the exact essay / post / work that's the entry point]
```

**Rules per pick:**
- Include @handle so you notify the creator (builds relationship)
- Make each description specific (not "they post great stuff")
- Include 1 thing about WHY this matters — what unique perspective or work they bring
- If citing a specific piece, link it in the reply to the tweet (not in the main tweet — preserves reach)

### Tweet N — Close
- Pattern: *"Save this for a slow reading week. I'll update the list in 6 months."*
- Pattern: *"What one of these missing? Reply with a recommendation."*
- Pattern: *"Following any of these folks has paid dividends on my thinking. Hope it does for you."*

## Relationship mechanics

Curation threads earn three distinct things:

1. **Reach via tagged creators.** When you tag @handle, you land in their notifications. If they re-share, their audience finds you. Best when tagged creators have 10x+ your follower count.
2. **Goodwill.** Curators are the people other creators want to be near. You become one.
3. **Reciprocity.** A subset of the curators you tag will return the favor — curating you in their own thread or DMing to connect.

Do NOT run curation threads purely for these mechanics. Readers (and the creators tagged) can smell transactional curation.

## Common failure modes

1. **Generic descriptions.** "@handle writes great stuff." Feels like padding. Fix: specific-work, specific-perspective per pick.
2. **Too many picks.** 20+ pick thread = no one finishes. Fix: 5–10 max.
3. **No criterion.** Doesn't say why THESE made the cut. Fix: tweet 1 establishes the criterion.
4. **Over-crediting your own relationships.** "My friends on X who also…" reads as nepotism. Fix: mix of people you know + people you don't. Emphasis on the work.
5. **All recent work.** Looks like you just read them last week. Fix: include long-time reads + recent discoveries.
6. **No re-read cadence.** One-and-done. Fix: signal you update this list annually or biannually.

## Variant sub-archetypes

- **Annual curation:** "Best X I read in 2025." Evergreen; shareable throughout year.
- **Category curation:** "Best newsletters on health / AI / finance / writing." Scoped enough to feel definitive.
- **Foundational curation:** "The first 5 essays I'd send someone starting out on X." Authority-builder.
- **Counter-curation:** "The X I stopped recommending and why." Higher-friction, higher-signal.

## When NOT to use curation thread

- You don't actually read / use the work you're curating. Readers sense fake recommendations.
- The curators listed are direct competitors and you don't want to re-share them. Pick a different format.
- You want fast engagement / virality. Curation threads tend to build slower, compound longer.
