# X Thread Variant — Educational Thread

> **Signature move.** One concept taught cleanly across 7-12 tweets. Each tweet is one idea. The thread teaches a specific mental model or framework the reader didn't have at the start. The final tweet delivers the synthesis. The thread sells authority through density of correct thinking per tweet, not through hype.

## When to Use

- Pillar is How-To, Authority Build, or Framework-Teaching.
- Conversion stage is Awareness or Consideration.
- Creator has a specific teaching with defensible depth — not a surface synthesis of other people's ideas.
- The concept is specific enough to teach in 7-12 tweets. Larger concepts are threads-of-threads; smaller concepts are single tweets.

## When NOT to Use

- The concept takes 20+ tweets to explain honestly. Steep drop-off past 15. Use long-form (newsletter, LinkedIn article, YouTube) instead.
- The concept is too simple for a thread — 1-3 tweets would cover it. Don't pad.
- The concept is creator-specific and can't generalize — use Personal Narrative Thread instead.
- Audience has already seen this framework from the creator — the educational thread pattern fatigues if repeated without enough variation.

## Opener (Hook Tweet)

The hook tweet earns every open. First 50 characters are visible on most clients.

Opener patterns:

1. **Named framework opener.** "The 3-variable framework behind every pricing decision in my last 400 engagements:"
2. **Specific outcome opener.** "How to go from 2 sales calls a week to 30 applications, without adding traffic:"
3. **Counter-intuitive claim opener.** "Most coaches price by looking at competitors. That's the mistake. Here's what works instead:"
4. **Number + authority opener.** "12 client results in 6 months. One mechanism kept repeating. Here it is:"
5. **Question-as-hook opener.** "What actually separates a $3K offer from a $30K offer? (It's not the deliverable.)"

Hook tweet rules:

- 120-200 characters for optimal mobile preview.
- Specificity ≥ 8 — number, named concept, or specific claim.
- Ends with a colon if the thread is listing, a period if the thread is narrative.
- Never "a thread 🧵" alone — the thread indicator goes AFTER the hook, not as the hook.
- One idea. Not two. Save the second idea for the next thread.

## Body Structure (Tweets 2-N)

### The Listing Educational Thread (default)

- Tweet 2: State the framework overview — "three variables" or "five stages" or "four decisions."
- Tweet 3-N-1: One variable / stage / decision per tweet. Each tweet:
  - Name the concept (first line)
  - Explain what it is (second line)
  - Give one specific example (third line)
  - 180-280 characters total
- Tweet N-1: The synthesis — how the parts connect.
- Tweet N (final): CTA.

### The Narrative Educational Thread

- Tweet 2: The setup — who / what / when.
- Tweet 3-5: Rising action — the specific events.
- Tweet 6-8: The discovery — what the creator learned.
- Tweet 9-11: The generalizable lesson.
- Tweet 12 (final): CTA.

### The Problem-Solution Educational Thread

- Tweet 2: The specific problem most people have.
- Tweet 3-5: Why the problem persists (the hidden structure).
- Tweet 6-9: The solution — broken into 3-4 sub-steps.
- Tweet 10-11: How to apply it.
- Tweet 12 (final): CTA.

## Bookmark-Bait Patterns

Threads that get bookmarked get re-surfaced by the X algorithm. Optimize for bookmarking by structuring the thread so the saved version stands alone.

- **Framework-in-one-tweet.** One middle tweet (usually tweet 6-8) compresses the entire framework into a single screenshot-worthy tweet. This is the "save this tweet" tweet.
- **Named list.** Each tweet in the body names its concept at the top. Readers can visually skim and return.
- **Clean formatting.** Line breaks between concepts in each tweet. White space makes tweets screenshot-friendly.
- **No external links in bookmark-able tweets.** Links break the save-ability. Keep the middle tweets link-free.

## Re-Engage Structure

Educational threads re-engage through three moves:

1. **Reply-to-thread (T+24-48h)** — quote the creator's own thread with one additional specific insight not in the thread. Re-surfaces the whole thread in the algorithm.
2. **Quote-tweet (T+48-72h)** — creator quote-tweets their own hook with the thread's best takeaway. Second-chance reach.
3. **Comment-pinning** — pin the one reply that extends the thread most productively. Signals that the creator engaged with the audience.

## Retweet / Reshare Invitation

- Final tweet ends with a soft reshare invitation: "If this was useful to someone you know, share it."
- The synthesis tweet (second-to-last) is the most-retweeted tweet because it's the compressed version.
- Never beg for retweets. "Please RT" tanks engagement.

## CTA / Close

Single action. Never stack.

- "Follow for more frameworks like this."
- "DM me the word [specific] and I'll send the full breakdown."
- "Full write-up: [newsletter link]" — only if the newsletter is the intended capture.
- "Book a call: [link]" — only for high-trust late-stage audiences, rarely used.

## Character Limits

- 280 max per tweet.
- 180-280 ideal for body tweets.
- 120-200 ideal for hook tweet.
- Never leave a tweet under 100 characters unless it's a one-line punchline.

## Voice-Match Requirements

- 3+ `phrases_to_use` from Compartment 1 across the thread.
- Zero items from `spec/BANNED-VOCABULARY.md`.
- Voice-match includes sentence length — if the creator writes in short punchy sentences, the thread mirrors that. If the creator writes longer analytical sentences, the tweets tolerate more length.

## Specificity Floor

Every tweet earns its spot by specificity:

- The hook has a number, a named concept, or a specific outcome.
- Each body tweet has a specific example, a specific decision, or a specific claim.
- The synthesis compresses into one concrete rule.
- The CTA is one specific action.

## Worked Example 1 — 3-Variable Pricing Framework

```
Tweet 1 (HOOK):
The 3-variable framework behind every pricing decision in my last 400 engagements:

Most coaches price by looking at competitors.
That's the mistake.
Here's the framework that actually works.

🧵

Tweet 2:
Before the variables:

Pricing is not a confidence problem. It's a math problem with a confidence layer.

Get the math right, the confidence follows.
Get the confidence right without the math, you overprice and stall.

Tweet 3:
Variable 1: Outcome Value

What the outcome is worth to the buyer in dollars.

Not your cost. Not your time. Not the industry median.

Ask: "If this works, what is the result worth to you?"
The buyer's number is variable 1.

Tweet 4:
Variable 2: Mechanism Scarcity

How replaceable your mechanism is.

If 10 other coaches sell the same thing, scarcity is low.
If you have a specific process your competitors don't have, scarcity is high.
Price follows scarcity, not effort.

Tweet 5:
Variable 3: Proof Density

How many case studies you have at the stage your next buyer is at.

Not your biggest case study. Not your oldest.
Same-stage proof.

5+ isomorphic cases = premium pricing unlocked.
1-2 = hold.

Tweet 6 (Bookmark-bait):
The formula:

Price Range = (Outcome Value) × (Mechanism Scarcity) × (Proof Density)

Your job is to calculate the range, then pick a point.

Most coaches pick before calculating. That's the mistake.

Tweet 7:
How to use this:

1. Estimate outcome value from 3 recent conversations.
2. Rate mechanism scarcity 1-10 vs. competitors.
3. Count same-stage case studies.
4. Multiply.

You'll get a range. Ship at the middle.

Tweet 8:
The signal to raise:

All three variables are trending up but your price hasn't moved.
= Leaving money on the table.

All three are weak but price is already high.
= The market is about to tell you.

Tweet 9:
The most common mistake:

Pricing in the middle of the competitor set.

Competitors don't have your mechanism.
They don't have your proof.
They don't solve your specific outcome.

Pricing to the median = pricing like you're generic.

Tweet 10 (SYNTHESIS):
The 3-variable framework:

• Outcome Value (what buyer gets)
• Mechanism Scarcity (how unique)
• Proof Density (isomorphic cases)

Multiplied, they give a range.
Price inside the range.
Raise when all three trend up.

Tweet 11 (CTA):
If this was useful, I write one of these every Tuesday.

Follow @[handle] for more frameworks.

DM "PRICING" and I'll send the worksheet I use with clients.
```

## Worked Example 2 — From 2 Calls to 30 Applications

```
Tweet 1 (HOOK):
How a SaaS founder went from 2 sales calls a week to 30 applications in 9 days.

Same product. Same traffic budget.
Three decisions.

🧵

Tweet 2:
Starting state:

$40K MRR.
120 leads a week (from $3K/mo ad spend).
2 qualified sales calls per week.
Flat for 6 months.

The funnel was leaking at the "leads to qualified" stage. Not traffic.

Tweet 3:
Decision 1: Replaced the ebook with a quiz.

7 questions.
Output: three tiers — "ready," "close," "not yet."
"Ready" routed to calendar immediately.
"Not yet" routed to nurture sequence.

Qualified routing: 2% → 11%.

Tweet 4:
Decision 2: Rewrote ads outcome-first.

Before: "The best tool for X."
After: "30 qualified demos a week without an SDR."

Same product.
CTR doubled. Application completion rose.

Apps per $1K spend: 3 → 9.

Tweet 5:
Decision 3: Founder closed for 60 days.

Was handing calls to an SDR.
We moved the founder back into the seat.

Every qualified app → founder's calendar.
Close rate: 18% → 42%.

(Founder saw every objection. Fed back into the product.)

Tweet 6:
What went wrong:

Day 4, the quiz was over-filtering.
Only 1 in 100 qualifying as "ready."
Lost a few possible applications in the over-filter.

Loosened the criteria on Day 5.
Routing normalized.

Tweet 7:
The final numbers:

Day 1: 2 applications.
Day 4: 11.
Day 9: 31.

Close rate on calls: 42%.
New ACV in 9-day window: $58K.

Same product. Same traffic budget.

Tweet 8 (Bookmark-bait):
The full sequence:

• Qualifier before the call (filters and routes)
• Outcome-first ads (not feature-first)
• Founder closing (while the pattern is new)

Traffic was never the problem.
Routing was.

Tweet 9 (SYNTHESIS):
The rule:

Most SaaS funnels leak at the qualifier, not the traffic.
A 7-question quiz + outcome-first ads + founder-as-closer fixes most of it.

If you have traffic but not calls, look at routing before you look at spend.

Tweet 10 (CTA):
```

## Worked Example 3 — The Framework-in-One-Tweet Pattern

```
Tweet 1 (HOOK):
Most coaches treat "pricing" and "positioning" as separate.

They're the same thing.
Here's why — and the 4-step fix.

🧵

Tweet 2:
Positioning determines price.
Price signals positioning.

If you position as premium but price at the market median, the buyer senses the mismatch.
If you position as generic but price premium, you stall at 2x consideration time.

Both cost revenue.

Tweet 3:
Step 1: Write your positioning in one sentence.

"[Specific outcome] for [specific customer] without [specific objection]."

If you can't fit it in one sentence, you don't have positioning yet.

Tweet 4:
Step 2: Calculate your price range (3-variable framework):

Outcome Value × Mechanism Scarcity × Proof Density.

Three numbers. Multiplied.

Output: a price range, not a single number.

Tweet 5:
Step 3: Align the range to the positioning.

Generic positioning → low end of the range.
Premium positioning → high end.
Specialist positioning (narrow ICP, deep outcome) → top of the range.

Tweet 6 (Bookmark-bait):
The positioning ↔ price test:

Read your positioning out loud.
Show your price.
If a stranger's first reaction is "that seems low" or "that seems high" — you have a mismatch.

Fix the one that's weaker first.

Tweet 7:
Step 4: Test with 3 new conversations.

Don't announce the price change to your list yet.
Test on cold prospects first.
If close rate holds or rises, the new positioning + price is calibrated.

Tweet 8 (SYNTHESIS):
Positioning and price are the same conversation:

• One-sentence positioning
• 3-variable range
• Alignment check
• Cold-prospect test

Fix them together, not separately.

Tweet 9 (CTA):

DM "ALIGN" for the worksheet.
```

## Compliance Check

- Banned vocabulary swept.
- No income guarantees.
- FTC disclosure if a thread references a paid client or sponsored relationship.
- Numbers verifiable.

## Common Failure Modes

- **"A thread 🧵" as whole hook.** Lazy. Kills engagement.
- **Concepts mashed together.** Tweet 4 and tweet 5 cover the same idea. Separate cleanly.
- **External links in thread body.** Breaks save-ability. Links only in final tweet or reply.
- **Over-threading.** 15+ tweets. Steep drop-off. Cut to 7-12.
- **No synthesis tweet.** Body ends, CTA follows — viewers don't get the compressed version for sharing.
- **Generic CTA.** "Thoughts?" or "Agree?" — no specific action. Wasted tweet.
- **Voice drift.** Creator's usual voice is replaced by "Twitter voice." Reread aloud; fix.

## Hand-off Checklist

- Hook tweet < 200 characters, specificity ≥ 8.
- 7-12 body tweets, each 180-280 characters.
- One synthesis tweet (second-to-last).
- One CTA tweet (last).
- One bookmark-bait tweet in the middle (usually tweet 6-8).
- Reply-to-thread scheduled T+24-48h.
- Quote-tweet scheduled T+48-72h.
- Zero banned vocabulary.
- Voice-match verified.
