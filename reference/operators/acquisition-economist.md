# the acquisition economist

Founder of Acquisition.com (Austin, TX), a portfolio holding company that publicly states it has scaled to $250M+ in annual revenue across its portfolio. the acquisition economist is the author of *$100M Offers*, *$100M Leads*, and the 2025 private-collection playbook series (*Hooks*, *Closing*, *Marketing Machine*, *Lead Nurture*, and the *Offers Implementation Guide*). His operator education is distributed as free/near-free book funnels that double as top-of-funnel ads for the Acquisition.com Scaling Workshop and portfolio-company deal flow. His two prior operating businesses, Gym Launch (sold for $46.2M in 2021 per the *Marketing Machine* PDF) and Prestige Labs, supply the case material most of his writing draws from.

## Thesis

Business math is simpler than it's made out to be, and most operators lose money at predictable points in the funnel. the acquisition economist reduces the whole game to a handful of named equations and ratios: value is a function with two multipliers on top and two dividers on the bottom; there are only four ways to get attention; most sales are lost to "maybes" the salesperson never converted, not to "nos"; and show rate is the single biggest under-managed number in appointment businesses. The consistent move is: take a thing the market treats as vibes, write an equation for it, then optimize the equation's weakest term.

The second thesis is that offers win or lose at the denominator. Most operators pile dream outcomes into the numerator (bigger claims, fancier copy) and ignore Time Delay and Effort & Sacrifice, which are where the real leverage sits. The *Offers Implementation Guide* makes this explicit: for every problem on the customer journey, map the Time, Effort, Achievement, and Financial problems separately and design a component that erases each one. This is why a Grand Slam Offer looks, on paper, like a bundle of obvious things: because the denominator work is where the compounding is.

The third thesis is that distribution is linear until you make other people do it for you. The Core Four (warm outreach, paid ads, cold outreach, posted content) all scale with operator effort. Customers, employees, agencies, and affiliates ("Lead Getters") scale past operator effort. The *Marketing Machine* PDF takes this one step further: the customer base itself becomes an ad-generation system, with 40 of the top 50 Gym Launch ads containing no the acquisition economist footage - they were customer-generated.

## Distinctive Moves

**1. Burn the boats on the denominator.** The Implementation Guide's Problem Mapping Exercise forces operators to list every obstacle in four separate columns - Time, Effort, Achievement, Financial. This surfaces the denominator problems (Time + Effort) that sloppy operators bundle into "other stuff." Each problem then gets a named solution that becomes a core component or bonus. The point: stop optimizing copy; start removing friction from the product itself.

**2. Price is a signal, not a cost.** From the Closing Playbook's "It's Good That It's A Lot" pattern: "the fact that this feels like a big investment means you're more likely to take it seriously. If this were just $1, it might be easy to overlook." And from the guide: "Charge 5-10x your cost to deliver." Cheap prices signal not-serious; expensive prices signal competence. Never drop price on the spot - the acquisition economist's rule 11 of closing: "Never change your price on the spot to get a sale."

**3. Guarantees are a value-equation lever, not a refund policy.** The guide lists five guarantee types in ascending strength: unconditional, conditional, service, anti-guarantee, performance. The strongest available guarantee should always be used because it moves Perceived Likelihood upward and reduces Risk (a component of Effort & Sacrifice) simultaneously - a compound effect on the equation.

**4. Scarcity and urgency must be true.** From Implementation Guide Step 9: the scarcity options (total business cap, growth rate cap, cohort cap, limited bonuses) and urgency options (rolling cohorts, seasonal, pricing, bonus deadlines) must map to actual capacity limits. Fabricated scarcity degrades into a brand signal that the offer is desperate.

**5. Hook obsession. If you have ten hours to advertise, spend eight on the hook.** "The longer I have been advertising, the less I care about anything else." The 70-20-10 innovation rule (Google's): 70% proven winners, 20% winner-adjacent permutations, 10% entirely new ideas. Hooks have two parts - the call-out (cocktail-party effect; gets "this is for me") and the condition for value (implicit or explicit value promise).

**6. Closing is about decision-making power, not persuasion.** From the Closing Playbook: "The goal of closing isn't to get the prospect to buy. Closing gives the prospect enough power to overcome decision avoidance." The Onion of Blame (Circumstances → Other People → Self) is a peeling protocol - expect the prospect to cite multiple blame sources, treat each layer as a re-framing opportunity, never disagree (Rule 2: "Acknowledge or agree, but never disagree"). "Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust" (Rule 22).

**7. Answer the phone in 5 minutes. Schedule within 72 hours.** From the Lead Nurture Playbook: 391% increase in conversions when leads are contacted within 60 seconds (Velocify); 78% of customers buy from whoever responds first (Lead Connect). the acquisition economist's standard: call within 5 minutes, limit scheduling to 3 days out, double-dial immediately (because the first call often gets blocked), pull appointments forward to today whenever possible.

**8. Customers are the ad department.** The Marketing Machine PDF's nine-component system (Lifecycle / Social Media / Events / Community Scrape / Bonuses & Awards / Competitions / Delivery / Chat Scrape / Reviews Scrape) turns normal operations - support chats, community posts, milestone celebrations - into ad creative pipelines. This is what made Gym Launch sellable: 40 of the top 50 ads didn't contain the acquisition economist.

**9. Yellows are golds. Volume negates luck.** From both the Closing and Lead Nurture playbooks: the best closers practice on unqualified (yellow) leads because the reps compound. The rule (Closing #9): "The best way to guarantee that you'll be world class is to do so many sales that it would be unreasonable that you aren't." the acquisition economist's Gym Launch sales rep Jacob took every "garbage" lead the team rejected and became top salesman of the quarter out of 26 guys.

**10. Ask for support, not permission.** Closing Playbook decision-maker handling: "Ask for support not permission. Remind them that they are their own person and should make the decision for themselves." The Three-Day No-Sweat Guarantee (required by most states anyway) is the downsell form of this - they lock in the spot but still have time to talk it over.

## Real Cases

**Gym Launch - January 2017 crisis.** the acquisition economist recounts in the Closing Playbook being $10K in debt after three days of ads burning $3,300/day each. Sales reps were scoring 2-for-14, 0-for-7, 0-for-18. Diagnosis: the reps weren't lazy, they'd never been taught to handle objections. the acquisition economist built the first version of the closing playbook that weekend ("I'll make us a playbook before the next huddle. We'll drill it every day"). By month-end, the business broke even. The 13-month processing record shown in the PDF tracks the ascent: from $100K in January 2017 to $1.5M in January 2018, $6.8M total for the period.

**Gym Launch - 2019 exit mechanism.** The Marketing Machine PDF opens with the PE rejection: "The business depends on your face. If anyone would buy it, you'd have to stay in the business." The solution came from watching gym owners at an awards dinner: record sales/support/upsell calls, capture lifecycle testimonials, run customer-generated ads. 12 months later, customer-generated ads were outperforming direct-to-camera ones. Sale closed at $46.2M.

**ALAN software (2020-2021).** Built during COVID as "Artificial Lead Automation & Nurture" - a pay-per-show appointment-management tool for gyms, pivoted to agencies when brick-and-mortar closed. Vlad (Russian ML-olympics winner) analyzed ~1M appointment rows across 4,000+ daily appointments and found the four correlates of show rate: availability slots, schedule-to-appointment delay, follow-up count, follow-up response rate. These became the Four Pillars. ALAN was sold to a larger competitor in 2021.

**Prestige Labs.** Referenced in the Marketing Machine PDF as a supplement company using the `#poweredbyprestige` hashtag scrape. Supports the Social Media Scrape mechanic - scrape your branded hashtag weekly, save the winners.

**The Dean Graziosi / Larry King hook story.** Opens the Hooks Playbook: Graziosi spent heavily recreating a Larry King-style interview set, ran it as an infomercial, and it broke records. Years later he got Larry King himself to record an interview - and the ad bombed. Diagnosis: Graziosi's ego-serving opener ("Tonight on Larry King, I'm here with my guest Dean Graziosi...") centered Graziosi. The re-recorded hook ("Have you ever in your adult life looked at yourself in the mirror and thought 'I should be further ahead by now'?") centered the viewer. The infomercial went on to sell millions of books. the acquisition economist's takeaway: "I've spent as much thinking about the first sentence as everything else combined."

## When To Invoke the acquisition economist's Thinking

1. **When an operator is adding features instead of cutting friction** - run the Value Equation and ask which of Time Delay or Effort & Sacrifice just got worse. If the new feature raises the denominator, it destroyed value.
2. **When a sales team's close rate is low on warm traffic** - this is a Closing Playbook problem, not an offer problem. Map their current objection-handling to the Onion of Blame and drill the top 3 closes per category.
3. **When show rates are under 50%** - go to the Lead Nurture Four Pillars in order: fix Availability first (most slots, 7 days/week), then Speed (5 minutes, 72 hours max), then Personalization, then Volume. The ALAN data says availability is the biggest single lever.
4. **When an operator wants to "create a lead magnet"** - force the Core Four test first: is this paid ads, content, warm outreach, or cold outreach? If the operator has no existing channel performance, warm outreach is the mandatory starting point.
5. **When the business is founder-dependent and becoming unsellable** - invoke the Marketing Machine. Start with Competition (fastest influx), then Chat/Community/Review Scrapes (zero extra work), then Bonuses and Awards (small prep cost), then Events (big cost, high yield).
6. **When a prospect says "I need to think about it"** - this is decision avoidance, not rejection. Apply "What's Your Main Concern" → "Reason" Close → "Best Case Worst Case." Do not accept the maybe as a no.
7. **When an operator is buying ads at 2x their actual CAC** - hooks. Per the Playbook: "If you have ten hours to advertise, focus your first eight hours on the hooks." Pull their current top 10 winners, run 70-20-10 allocation on new creative.
8. **When choosing between tiered offer vs. single Grand Slam** - if the audience is cold and untrained, one Grand Slam wins because it forces the equation to be maxed out. Tiers work when an existing audience already believes the base thesis and needs ascension.
