# The 4-Pillar Content Mix (40/30/20/10)

> **Why this matters:** Channels that go 100% offer-pillar burn out trust and stop growing. Channels that go 100% education get views but no sales. The 40/30/20/10 split keeps the algorithm fed, authority compounding, audience warm, and offer converting — simultaneously.

## The Ratio

| Pillar | % of Content | Purpose | CTA Rule |
|--------|--------------|---------|----------|
| Education | 40% | Build authority, teach mechanism | Soft CTA to playlist or lead magnet |
| Authority | 30% | Prove you belong, position above peers | Soft CTA to case study or newsletter |
| Entertainment | 20% | Para-social trust, ICP affinity | No CTA or minimal end-card |
| Offer | 10% | Convert to calls/applications | Hard CTA to VSSL or booking link |

For a creator publishing 2 videos/week (8/month), that's:
- 3 Education videos
- 2–3 Authority videos
- 1–2 Entertainment videos
- 1 Offer video

---

## Pillar 1 — Education (40%)

### Purpose

Teach the mechanism. Frame the problem. Give the viewer a tool they can apply without buying anything. Education is where authority compounds.

### Example topics (high-ticket operator)

- "The 4-step framework I use to qualify leads in under 60 seconds"
- "How to write a cold email that books 3 calls a week (full template)"
- "7 positioning mistakes killing your high-ticket offer"
- "The exact 12-minute sales call structure that closes $15K deals at 40%"
- "Why your VSL isn't converting — teardown of 3 real examples"

### Format fit

- Educational Deep-Dive (primary)
- Tutorial
- Listicle (fast education)

### CTA rules

- End-card: link to a related deep-dive or playlist (not to a sales page)
- On-screen: occasional lead magnet (PDF, template, checklist) — but no more than 1 in 3 Education videos
- Avoid sales pitches in Education pillar. Viewer stays because they trust you're not selling; break that trust and retention crashes

### What separates good from bad

- Good Education: one named framework, worked example, specific numbers
- Bad Education: generic advice, stock b-roll, no mechanism, no proof

---

## Pillar 2 — Authority (30%)

### Purpose

Prove you belong at the top of the category. Show the receipts. Position above peers without trash-talking them. Authority is where trust gets manufactured.

### Example topics

- Case studies (specific client, specific result, dates and numbers)
- Teardowns (of your own launches, of competitors' funnels, of industry examples)
- Income breakdowns (real, not vanity)
- Behind-the-scenes of your process or team
- Hot takes on industry trends — with data
- Debates / contrarian positions backed by proof

### Format fit

- Case Study (primary)
- Reaction-Interview (borrows authority)
- Educational Deep-Dive (when it features your own results)
- Lifestyle-Vlog (when the "day" contains authority moments)

### CTA rules

- Soft CTA to related case study or newsletter
- End-card to a deep-dive that teaches what the case study demonstrated
- Never hard-pitch in an Authority video — it reads as bragging

### What separates good from bad

- Good Authority: specific, dated, screenshotted, with the mechanism explained
- Bad Authority: "I did $500K last month" with no context, no mechanism, no proof

---

## Pillar 3 — Entertainment (20%)

### Purpose

Para-social trust. The viewer decides "I like this person" — which is different from "I trust their expertise." Both are required for high-ticket conversion.

### Example topics

- Behind-the-scenes of your life / business
- "A day in my life building [X]"
- Reflection on a failure or expensive lesson
- Travel + work stories
- Team interactions, culture moments
- Opinion pieces on non-business topics (sparingly — must tie back to your ICP's worldview)

### Format fit

- Lifestyle-Vlog (primary)
- Reaction-Interview (if the guest is interesting apart from the business angle)

### CTA rules

- No CTA in most Entertainment videos
- End-card to the next video in a vlog series or to a related Education video
- Avoid asking for anything in Entertainment — it breaks the para-social relationship you just built

### What separates good from bad

- Good Entertainment: specific scenes, genuine tension, a reflection that ties to the viewer's life
- Bad Entertainment: performative "look at my life," no substance, no reflection

---

## Pillar 4 — Offer (10%)

### Purpose

Convert. This is the sales content. Viewers who've been through the other pillars are ready — Offer content gives them the path.

### Example topics

- VSSL (the quarterly flagship)
- Case study that ends with a hard CTA to work with you
- "How to work with me" / "Who I'm looking for" video
- Launch video for a new cohort or offer
- Webinar replay (if hosted on YouTube)

### Format fit

- VSSL 5-chapter or 15-step (primary)
- Case Study with hard CTA

### CTA rules

- Hard, single, specific CTA
- Application link or booking link in description, pinned comment, and end-card
- Mid-video soft CTA around minute 20–30 for early converters
- End-of-video recap + final CTA

### What separates good from bad

- Good Offer: clear mechanism, real proof, honest disqualification, one next step
- Bad Offer: feature list, no proof, vague CTA, multiple links

## Weekly Cadence Example (2 videos/week, 4 weeks = 8 videos)

| Week | Video 1 | Video 2 |
|------|---------|---------|
| 1 | Education | Authority |
| 2 | Education | Entertainment |
| 3 | Education | Authority |
| 4 | Offer | Authority |

This hits 3 Education (37.5%), 3 Authority (37.5%), 1 Entertainment (12.5%), 1 Offer (12.5%). Not exactly 40/30/20/10 but within band.

For a 3-month cycle (24 videos):
- 10 Education
- 7 Authority
- 5 Entertainment
- 2 Offer (1 VSSL + 1 supporting case study with CTA)

## Common Violations

### "All-Education Burnout"

Creator publishes 100% Education, builds a channel, then tries to pivot to Offer. Audience revolts because the implicit contract was "you give value, I subscribe." Solution: mix from day one, not at scale.

### "Offer Spam"

Creator publishes Offer pillar at 40%+. CTR drops because thumbnails start looking salesy. Session-time drops because viewers learn "this channel sells, doesn't teach." Solution: keep Offer at 10% max.

### "Vanity Authority"

Creator publishes 50%+ Authority — screenshots of Stripe, private jets, revenue updates. Looks like bragging, attracts wrong audience (aspiring amateurs instead of peers). Solution: 30% cap, always pair with mechanism.

### "Entertainment-Only"

Creator builds a lifestyle channel, can't convert. Audience likes them but doesn't trust their expertise. Solution: 20% cap on Entertainment unless the business model is ad revenue / sponsorships, not high-ticket.

## Pillar Tagging (operational)

Every video brief gets tagged with its pillar before scripting. Quarterly audit: pull the pillar distribution of the last 12 videos and compare to target 40/30/20/10. Rebalance next quarter's calendar accordingly.

## Non-applicable scenarios

- Pure lead-gen channels with paid ad spend (different ratio — more Offer)
- Content licensing / MRR from views (ratio tilts toward Entertainment + high-volume Listicles)
- B2B SaaS with long sales cycles — Authority goes to 50%+, Offer disappears from YouTube (moves to LinkedIn + webinar)
