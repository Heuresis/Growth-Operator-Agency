# The 5-Phase Viewer Psychology Model

> **Why this matters:** A video doesn't fail at one point — it fails at the phase transition the creator didn't engineer. Knowing which psychological phase the viewer is in at minute 3 vs. minute 23 tells you what beat has to land, or retention collapses.

## The 5 Phases

| Phase | Duration Window | Viewer Question | What Fails Here | What Wins Here |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1. Attention | 0:00–0:30 | "Should I watch this?" | Weak hook | Pattern interrupt + stakes |
| 2. Commitment | 0:30–3:00 | "Is this going to pay off?" | No roadmap | Explicit promise + proof snippet |
| 3. Belief | 3:00–40% mark | "Is this actually true?" | Hand-wavy claims | Mechanism + proof stacking |
| 4. Desire | 40%–80% mark | "Do I want this result?" | Feature-list | Transformation vivid-casting |
| 5. Action | 80%–end | "What do I do now?" | Multiple CTAs | One clear next step |

The 5 phases are sequential. A viewer can't feel desire before belief — if you skip a phase, the conversion collapses regardless of how strong the later phases are.

---

## Phase 1 — Attention (0:00–0:30)

### What's happening in the viewer's head

They're deciding in 3–5 seconds whether to stay or click away. Autoplay has dumped them into your video from somewhere else. They're assessing: Is this on-topic for what I care about? Does this look high-quality? Does the host look credible?

### What triggers this phase

- Visual contrast (thumbnail-to-first-frame match)
- Auditory contrast (voice modulation, sound design)
- Content contrast (pattern interrupt from expected norm)
- Credential flag (something that signals "this person knows")

### What beat engineers it

The first 15 seconds of the hook. See `hook-library-20-formulas.md` for 20 working patterns. Key: do NOT spend Attention phase on your intro card, channel branding, or "welcome back everyone." Both kill retention.

### Failure mode

"Hey guys, today we're going to talk about…" — this generic opener loses 40–60% of viewers in the first 15 seconds.

### Benchmark

>80% still watching at 0:15. >70% at 0:30.

---

## Phase 2 — Commitment (0:30–3:00)

### What's happening in the viewer's head

They've decided to stay for now. But they're asking: "Will this actually pay off? What am I going to learn? Is this host going to waste my time with filler?"

### What triggers this phase

- An explicit promise ("By minute 8 you'll know X")
- A proof snippet (a quick data point, screenshot, or credential that backs the promise)
- A roadmap (what we're going to cover, in order)
- A small value-drop (one useful thing in the first 2 minutes)

### What beat engineers it

The "promise + roadmap + credential" sequence.

Template:
> "In this video I'm going to show you [specific outcome]. We're going to cover [point 1], [point 2], [point 3]. And by [midpoint] I'll show you [specific proof]. The reason I can show you this is [credential in one sentence]."

### Failure mode

The creator launches into the content without establishing payoff. Viewer stays for 90 seconds, doesn't see where it's going, leaves. This is the single biggest drop-off point on most educational channels.

### Benchmark

>65% at 1:30. >55% at 3:00.

---

## Phase 3 — Belief (3:00–40% mark)

### What's happening in the viewer's head

They believe you have something worth saying. Now they're testing whether it's TRUE. Every claim you make is being evaluated against their existing beliefs. If you assert without proof, their internal skeptic activates and they check out.

### What triggers this phase

- Specific mechanism (not "strategies" — a named, numbered, explicit framework)
- Proof at every level of claim (quantitative > qualitative > testimonial)
- Worked examples (show the mechanism applied)
- Pre-empting objections ("You might be thinking X. Here's why that's not right…")
- First-principles reasoning (why this works, not just that it works)

### What beat engineers it

The mechanism section. In a 25-minute educational video this runs from roughly minute 4 to minute 14. In a VSSL it's Act 3.

Density rule: every major claim needs 1 proof point. Every controversial claim needs 2 — one data, one example.

### Failure mode

Generic advice. "You need to have good positioning." → belief breaks. What's "good"? How do I know? Prove it. Specificity is the belief currency.

### Benchmark

>45% at the 15-minute mark (on a 30-min video).

---

## Phase 4 — Desire (40%–80% mark)

### What's happening in the viewer's head

They believe it works. Now the question is: "Do I want this badly enough to act on it?" They're imagining their life with the outcome. If you don't help them imagine it in vivid, specific terms, desire stays theoretical and they don't act.

### What triggers this phase

- Transformation casting (describe the future state in sensory detail)
- Contrast (current pain vs. future gain, side-by-side)
- Identity language ("the kind of [founder/operator/creator] who…")
- Social proof — specifically, people like them
- Loss aversion (what happens if they don't act)

### What beat engineers it

The proof stack + the future-casting close. In the VSSL, this is Act 4 and the first half of Act 5.

Template for vivid transformation:
> "Imagine it's [specific date, 6 months from now]. You open your email. The first message is [specific desirable outcome]. You look at your calendar — [specific contrast to their current state]. This is what [offer outcome] actually feels like. Not abstract. Not some day. [Date]."

### Failure mode

The creator describes features instead of transformation. "You'll get 12 modules, a workbook, and weekly calls" — weak. "You'll have a book-a-call link in your Instagram bio that converts at 4.2%, bringing you 15 calls a week without you posting every day" — desire activates.

### Benchmark

>35% at the 24-minute mark.

---

## Phase 5 — Action (80%–end)

### What's happening in the viewer's head

They want it. Now they need to know exactly what to do next — and it has to be ONE thing, with zero friction. Any ambiguity at this stage turns desire back into procrastination.

### What triggers this phase

- Single, specific next step
- Explicit removal of decision friction ("click the link, fill out 4 questions, book a time")
- Urgency (real only — fake urgency kills trust)
- Guarantee / risk reversal
- Restatement of the core promise

### What beat engineers it

The CTA. See VSSL Acts 4–5. For non-sales videos, the CTA is to the next video in the binge-path or to a lead magnet.

Rule: ONE CTA per video. Offering "book a call OR download the PDF OR subscribe to the newsletter" splits attention and kills conversion. Pick one.

### Failure mode

Multiple CTAs. Vague CTA ("check out my stuff"). Long final minute that trails off. The close should be punchy and decisive.

### Benchmark

Viewers who watch past 90% convert at 8–15x the rate of viewers who drop off at 60%. The phase-5 close is where most of the conversion math happens.

## Mapping Phases to Video Types

| Video Type | Phase 1 | Phase 2 | Phase 3 | Phase 4 | Phase 5 |
|------------|---------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Educational Deep-Dive | 0–30s | 30s–3m | 3–20m | 20–28m | 28–30m |
| VSSL 5-chapter | 0–30s | 30s–3m | 3–20m | 20–32m | 32–38m |
| VSSL 15-step | 0–30s | 30s–5m | 5–38m | 38–58m | 58–75m |
| Shorts | 0–3s | 3–10s | 10–40s | 40–55s | 55–60s |
| Tutorial | 0–15s | 15s–1m | 1–12m | 12–14m | 14–15m |

## Phase-Failure Diagnostic

When retention drops unexpectedly, map the drop to the phase the viewer was in:

- Drop 0:00–0:30 → Hook (Attention) — rewrite first 15s
- Drop 0:30–3:00 → Promise/roadmap (Commitment) — add explicit promise
- Drop 3:00–40% → Proof/mechanism (Belief) — add specificity, worked example, data
- Drop 40%–80% → Transformation (Desire) — add vivid future-casting
- Drop 80%–100% → CTA clarity (Action) — simplify to one step
