# VSSL 15-Step Structure (Jon Benson) — Adapted for YouTube

> **Why this matters:** The VSSL is the single highest-converting long-form format for offers above $3K. YouTube's algorithm rewards it (long AVD) and warm viewers convert 5–12x higher than cold ad traffic on the same letter.

## The Hero Pattern (beat-by-beat)

Total run-time: 45–75 minutes for most $3K–$30K offers. Longer (60–90 min) for $30K+ coaching and agency retainers. Every step below has a timing window — the full VSSL is paced, not listed.

### Step 1 — The Big Promise Hook (0:00–1:30)

Open with the single largest believable claim, stake it, and give a timeline.

- "In the next [X minutes] I'm going to show you how [specific, quantifiable outcome] — without [3 most common objections]."
- Avoid vague promises ("more leads," "grow faster"). Quantify: dollars, time, ratios.
- End the step with a tease: "And I'm going to prove all of this with [specific proof vehicle]."

### Step 2 — The Pattern Interrupt / Disqualification (1:30–3:00)

Immediately disqualify tire-kickers. This paradoxically increases qualified viewers staying.

- "Before I start — this video isn't for you if [criteria A, B, C]. But if [criteria X, Y, Z], this might be the most important 60 minutes you spend this quarter."
- This step eliminates the 20% who will waste your time and anchors the 80% who fit.

### Step 3 — The Named Enemy (3:00–6:00)

Name the external force, industry lie, or broken system responsible for the viewer's current state. Not the viewer's fault.

- "The reason [outcome] isn't working isn't you. It's [specific named mechanism / industry / trend]."
- The enemy must be specific (not "the gurus") — e.g., "the 2018 funnel playbook," "the scale-at-all-costs VC model," "the DIY agency model."

### Step 4 — The Credibility Transfer (6:00–9:00)

Why listen to you. Not a resume dump — a story of how you discovered the mechanism.

- Mention 3 concrete credentials (revenue numbers, clients, years, case studies)
- Transition: "But the real reason I'm qualified is because I lived through [the enemy] myself."

### Step 5 — The Origin Story (9:00–14:00)

The moment you realized the old way was broken. Specific time, place, emotional tension.

- Open with sensory detail: date, location, what you were wearing, what someone said
- Build to the rock-bottom moment — be specific with numbers
- Exit on: "That was the moment I realized there had to be another way."

### Step 6 — The Discovery (14:00–18:00)

How you found the mechanism. Make it feel accidental, almost unlikely — never "I'm a genius."

- A conversation, a book, a data point, a client who did X differently
- Name the mechanism here for the first time: "I call this [named framework]."

### Step 7 — The Mechanism Reveal (18:00–28:00)

The longest single section. Teach the framework with depth. Not enough to believe it — enough to apply it.

- Name each step of the mechanism (3–5 steps works best)
- Diagram on screen
- Worked example with real numbers
- Anticipate and answer the 3 biggest "but what about…" objections

### Step 8 — Proof Stack (28:00–38:00)

Stack 5–9 concrete proof elements in sequence. Each different.

- Client case study (dollars, dates, screenshots)
- Your own result (dashboard, P&L)
- Third-party endorsement (screenshot, quote)
- Data study or research citation
- Live demo (if applicable)
- Before-after numbers
- Video testimonial clip (30–60s)
- Press or authority mention
- A contrarian data point that only your mechanism explains

The rule: proof must get SPECIFIC. Generic "we helped 100 clients" is weaker than "Sarah, March 2024, $0 to $47K in 90 days, here's the screenshot."

### Step 9 — The Pivot to Offer (38:00–40:00)

Transition from "here's what works" to "here's how I can help you do it."

- Acknowledge the objection: "You might be thinking 'I could do this myself.' And maybe you can. But here's why most people won't."
- Name the three reasons DIY fails: time, pattern recognition, accountability
- "So I built [offer name] to solve this."

### Step 10 — The Offer Introduction (40:00–46:00)

Describe what the offer IS, not yet what it costs.

- Name it
- Describe the outcome it produces (not the features)
- Who it's for (reiterate disqualification)
- Format (calls, cohort, 1:1, done-for-you)

### Step 11 — The Deliverable Stack (46:00–52:00)

Itemize every component and assign a standalone value to each. This is Hormozi's stack logic applied inside Benson's VSSL.

- Component 1: [description] — worth $X alone
- Component 2: [description] — worth $X alone
- …
- Total stack value: $Y (must be 5–10 times the offer price)

### Step 12 — The Price Reveal (52:00–55:00)

Anchor high, drop to actual.

- "If I sold each of these separately, this would be $Y."
- "The fair market rate for this kind of engagement is $Z."
- "But for [specific reason that's believable — cohort launch, beta, capacity], you can get this for [actual price]."

The "specific reason" must be true or the whole VSSL collapses.

### Step 13 — The Guarantee (55:00–58:00)

Risk reversal. Specific, quantified, written.

- "If by [date/milestone] you haven't [specific outcome], [specific remedy — refund, extended work, money back]."
- Bolder guarantees convert better IF they're believable. Money-back after 90 days + a keep-the-work clause outperforms vague "satisfaction guarantee" 3:1.

### Step 14 — The Urgency / Scarcity (58:00–61:00)

Real scarcity only. Fake urgency kills trust and triggers YouTube's demotion for spam-language.

- Capacity: "I'm only taking 7 clients this cohort."
- Deadline: "Applications close Friday at midnight."
- Price: "Price goes up $2K on [specific date] for the next cohort."

Pick one. Don't stack three — that reads as scammy.

### Step 15 — The Close + Next Step (61:00–end)

Specific call-to-action. One step only.

- "Click the link below to book a 20-minute call. On the call we'll [specific thing you'll do — not 'learn more']."
- Re-state the promise: "Remember — in [timeframe] you'll have [specific outcome]."
- Final recap in 60 seconds: Problem → Mechanism → Offer → Guarantee → CTA

## YouTube-Specific Adaptations

These are the changes from Benson's sales-page VSL to the YouTube VSSL:

1. **Cold-open hook is shorter** — 15s max before the first retention check. Benson's sales-page VSLs could breathe for 2 minutes before the first value drop; YouTube can't.
2. **Visual B-roll every 20–40 seconds** — the YouTube audience is used to cuts. Static talking-head for 90+ seconds tanks retention.
3. **Chapter markers** — YouTube's chapter feature helps retention. Mark 5–7 chapters.
4. **Two CTAs, not one** — place a soft CTA around minute 30 (for viewers ready to act early) and the hard CTA at the end. Hormozi data: the mid-video CTA captures 30–40% of eventual conversions.
5. **End-card to a 2nd asset** — book-a-call link + a follow-up deep-dive. The 2nd asset captures viewers not ready yet.
6. **No auto-play music beds** — YouTube VSSLs perform better with minimal background music; over-produced music reads as "ad."

## Retention Benchmarks for YouTube VSSL

- 0:30: >70% still watching
- 5:00: >55%
- 15:00: >45%
- 30:00: >35%
- 45:00: >28%
- 60:00: >22%

Below these, the VSSL needs restructuring — usually the mechanism step (Step 7) is too long, or the origin story (Step 5) is too self-indulgent.
