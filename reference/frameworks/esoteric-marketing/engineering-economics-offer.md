# Engineering the Economics Into the Offer

> **Why this matters:** Buyers don't calculate ROI during a sales conversation. They *feel* whether the economics are obvious or forced. When an offer is economically engineered well, the price feels low by the close — the buyer is doing the math silently and it keeps working out. When it's engineered poorly, the creator has to push, justify, and overcome objections that wouldn't exist in a well-engineered offer. This file is the architecture of that feeling.

## The Principle — Economics Should Feel Obvious

The buyer's internal state by the close of a well-engineered offer:

> "The math works out. Not buying would be the weird choice."

Not: "the seller told me the math works out." Not: "I need to run a spreadsheet to check." Not: "I'll think about it." **Obvious.**

This feeling is engineered. The creator pre-does the math inside the offer structure, so the buyer doesn't have to. Four levers build the feeling:

| Lever | Produces the feeling of... |
|-------|-----------------------------|
| Guarantee math | "My downside is tiny or negative" |
| Bonus value math | "The value is many times the price" |
| Payment-plan psychology | "The friction to start is trivial" |
| ROI framing | "The upside is concrete and fast" |

All four operating together produce the "obvious" close. Missing any one forces the creator to push on the weak lever — which reads as pressure.

## Lever 1 — Guarantee Math

**What it does:** Converts perceived risk into a quantified number; that number should be at or below zero.

### The three levels of guarantee math

**Level 1: Refund** — Buyer's worst case = time spent, money refunded.
- Perceived downside: ~hours of time + opportunity cost
- Psychological: "I can undo the money, I can't undo the time"

**Level 2: Refund + Keep Something** — Buyer's worst case = positive, because a bonus is retained.
- Perceived downside: negative (they're better off even if they refund)
- Structure: "If it doesn't work for you, keep [bonus worth $X] and get your money back"
- Psychological: "The floor is winning"

**Level 3: Work-Until** — Buyer's worst case = the creator keeps working with them past the original term until the outcome hits.
- Perceived downside: none (ultimate state is getting the outcome)
- Structure: "If you haven't hit [specific outcome] in [timeframe], I continue working with you at no additional cost until you do"
- Psychological: "The outcome is guaranteed in time"

### The guarantee-math placement

The guarantee is stated in three places in the funnel:
- **Sales page section** — one clear paragraph, math spelled out
- **VSL Beat 6 (Choice)** — delivered verbally with the mission framing
- **Checkout page** — reinforced at point-of-purchase

### The guarantee-math calculation

Spell out the downside literally:

> "Worst case: you enroll, try the protocol for 60 days, don't see the first-reset result, and request a refund. You get 100% of your money back. You keep the [bonus 1] ($497 value) and [bonus 2] ($997 value). You're $1,494 ahead of where you started. That's the worst case."

That paragraph is doing load-bearing work. It converts abstract guarantee language into a specific negative downside.

### What breaks guarantee math

- Guarantee buried in fine print (reduces the feeling even if the terms are identical)
- Guarantee structured so the buyer feels they have to "prove" they qualified (activation energy for refund should be low; refund rate drops counterintuitively)
- Guarantee that contradicts earlier copy (if the VSL said 90-day transformation and the guarantee requires 6 months of compliance before refund eligibility, the dissonance kills the offer)

## Lever 2 — Bonus Value Math

**What it does:** Inflates the perceived value-to-price ratio using structurally legitimate bonuses.

### the acquisition economist value-equation refresher

> Perceived Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort)

Each bonus pushes one of those four variables:

| Bonus type | Variable pushed | Buyer-side effect |
|------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Template / SOP | Effort ↓ | "I don't have to build this from scratch" |
| Software / tool access | Effort ↓ + Time Delay ↓ | "This is already done for me" |
| Done-for-you setup | Time Delay ↓ | "I skip weeks of ramp" |
| Coaching calls | Perceived Likelihood ↑ | "Someone will catch me if I'm off" |
| Case study library | Perceived Likelihood ↑ | "I can see what worked" |
| Community access | Dream Outcome ↑ + Likelihood ↑ | "Peer acceleration + accountability" |
| Higher-tier preview | Dream Outcome ↑ | "The ceiling is higher than I knew" |

### The bonus-stack structure

Standard high-ticket structure:

- Core program ($X value — often priced individually to anchor)
- Bonus 1: Time-Delay Killer ($Y value)
- Bonus 2: Effort Killer ($Z value)
- Bonus 3: Perceived-Likelihood Amplifier ($W value)
- Bonus 4 (ethical cap): one mission-aligned bonus that would cost real money to deliver alone

### The value-math rule

Total stack value / price ≥ a factor of ten before the offer feels "obvious." Some rules:

- Bonuses must be **deliverable** (if you can't ship it, don't count it)
- Bonus values must be **defensible** (priced at what they'd cost standalone on the market)
- Bonuses must be **relevant** to the core outcome (irrelevant bonuses reduce trust)

### The bonus-naming move

Each bonus gets a proprietary name (not "Bonus 1: Email Templates"). Names do two things: raise perceived specificity, and join the sacred-vocabulary layer (see `cult-methodology/cult-to-customer-translation.md`).

- Weak: "Bonus 1: 30 Email Templates"
- Strong: "The Deliverability Rescue Kit — 30 pre-engineered email templates that land in inbox 94% of the time"

### What breaks bonus-value math

- Inflated bonus values the buyer doesn't believe (a "$10,000 value" bonus that's clearly a PDF kills trust in the whole stack)
- Too many bonuses (>5-6 starts diluting each bonus's perceived weight)
- Bonuses that are actually part of the core (the bonus "you also get the Week 3 module" is a disguised core feature, not a bonus)

## Lever 3 — Payment-Plan Psychology

**What it does:** Lowers activation energy at close. Converts a large-sum decision into a small-sum monthly decision the buyer can swallow.

### The psychological mechanics

cognitive-bias research + Thaler: humans have mental accounting buckets. A $4,997 single payment hits the "big purchase" bucket (requires deliberation, spousal conversation, serious thought). A $497/mo for 12 months hits the "subscription / ongoing" bucket (lower cognitive friction).

The *total* is higher on payment plan (almost always — creators should charge a ~20% premium on payment plans), but the *decision weight* is lower. Close rate on payment plan typically 15-30% higher than full-pay-only offers.

### The standard structure

Present three options:
- **Pay-in-full**: $4,997 (anchor; ~30% of buyers)
- **3-pay**: $1,797/mo × 3 = $5,391 (~45% of buyers)
- **6-pay**: $997/mo × 6 = $5,982 (~25% of buyers)

The 3-pay is usually the winner. Pay-in-full anchors the "real" price. 6-pay rescues the price-sensitive.

### The payment-plan math framing

In the VSL close or sales page, framing:

> "Investment is [price pay-in-full]. Most people split it into 3 payments of [price] — which is less than [relatable reference: a phone bill / a weekend dinner / a Costco run]. If you want to spread further, 6 payments at [price] gives you a full 6 months to recoup from the first-reset result alone."

The "relatable reference" is load-bearing. It maps the monthly payment onto a known-small expense.

### Interest-free vs. financed

Creators fall into two camps:

- **Creator-financed** (interest-free to buyer, creator carries the cashflow): works for margins >60%, cash-rich businesses
- **Third-party financed** (Affirm, Klarna, Afterpay): works for low-cash businesses; extracts financing margin to third party

Third-party financing is increasingly standard. Creator-financed is a brand signal — "I trust my customers."

### The payment-plan ethical line

See `cult-methodology/ethical-guardrails.md` Red Flag 5. Payment plans must not be used to push distressed buyers into commitments they can't afford. The sales page should include a "don't buy if you can't afford the single payment without the payment plan" statement for high-ticket offers.

### What breaks payment-plan psychology

- Only one option (missing anchor)
- Payment plans that feel like loans (over-legal language at checkout)
- Payment plan monthly price that isn't psychologically small ("$1,997/mo" reads big; "$997/mo" reads manageable; "$497/mo" reads trivial)

## Lever 4 — ROI Framing

**What it does:** Converts the price from a cost into an investment with a specific expected return.

### The ROI math structure

Every high-ticket offer should surface an explicit ROI argument. Structure:

1. **Expected outcome** — the concrete result the buyer is buying
2. **Time to outcome** — 30 / 60 / 90 / 180 days
3. **Dollar value of outcome** — converted to a number
4. **Price vs. outcome ratio** — stated as a multiplier
5. **Payback period** — how long until the buyer has recouped the price

### The canonical "one sale pays for it" frame

For business/B2B offers:

> "If this produces one additional $10K client for your business, it's paid for itself 4x. If it produces two, it's paid for 8x. If you're running a business and one extra client in the next 90 days sounds plausible, the math is not in question."

That frame does mental calculation for the buyer. The buyer's nervous system registers: "one client. One. That's not hard. This pays for itself."

### Category-specific ROI frames

| Category | ROI frame |
|----------|-----------|
| B2B services | "One new client pays for it [X]x over" |
| Ecommerce / DTC | "Pays back at [X] ROAS — your current ROAS × [Y] months" |
| Health / fitness | "Pays back the first year in healthcare-cost avoided + productivity recovered" |
| Personal finance | "Pays back the first year in [specific saved expense]" |
| Relationships / life | "Pays back in [specific reclaimed state] — the return is the rest of your life lived differently" |

For non-business categories, ROI is framed in time-and-state, not dollars.

### The ROI-frame placement

- VSL: stated once in Beat 6 (Choice)
- Sales page: a dedicated section after the offer stack
- Email sequence: one dedicated "math email" in the pre-close sequence

### What breaks ROI framing

- ROI claims the buyer doesn't believe (a $10K course that claims "your first 3 sales will pay for this in Week 1" triggers skepticism in sophisticated audiences)
- Missing time specificity ("eventually pays back" reads as hedge)
- Overly optimistic base rates ("buyers typically return ten times their investment" — without supporting data, reads as hype)

## The Four Levers Together — The "Obvious" Close

When all four levers are engineered, the buyer at close is running this silent calculation:

> "Stack value is roughly ten times price. Guarantee means downside is at or below zero. Payment plan means I can start now without a big upfront. ROI math means I recoup price in [specific near-term outcome]. Not doing this is the weird choice."

The creator did not push. The buyer did the math.

## The Common Failure — Forced Economics

The forced version shows up when only 1-2 levers are present and the creator tries to make up the deficit with pressure. Patterns:

- Strong bonus stack, weak guarantee → creator leans on bonuses too hard; buyer smells inflation
- Strong guarantee, weak bonus stack → price feels bare; buyer delays on "let me think"
- Strong ROI, weak payment plan → creator pushes against cashflow objection
- Strong payment plan, weak ROI → creator sells on accessibility but buyer doesn't see the win

The fix in every case is to engineer the missing lever, not to push harder on the present levers.

## The Economics Engineering Audit

Running this on a draft offer:

| Lever | Audit question | Score 0-5 |
|-------|----------------|-----------|
| 1. Guarantee math | Is the buyer's downside explicitly calculable to ≤$0? | |
| 2. Bonus value math | Does stack value / price reach a factor of ten, with defensible individual bonus values? | |
| 3. Payment-plan psychology | Are there 3 options (PIF / mid / long) with psychologically-small monthly prices? | |
| 4. ROI framing | Is there a specific, believable payback calculation (dollars or state)? | |

Total 18-20 = engineered; the close should feel obvious. 12-17 = functional but with a weak lever the creator is compensating for. <12 = the offer is going to require pressure to close; economics need re-engineering before scaling traffic.

- **Impulse-purchase price points ($7-$47 tripwires)** — the economic engineering is over-specified; guarantee + one bonus suffices.
- **Pure B2B enterprise sales** — economic case is carried by RFP/procurement, not offer-stack; different discipline.
- **Commodity repeat purchases** — no offer-stack logic; economics are unit-cost vs. competitor.
