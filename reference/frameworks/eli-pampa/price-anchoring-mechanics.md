# Price Anchoring Mechanics — Pampa

> **Source:** Eli Pampa — *🚀 SECTION 9: PRICE ANCHORING*
> **Position in VSL**: Step 9 of 15, ~60% through runtime, after Testimonials and before Guarantee.
> **Target psychological state**: the prospect sells themselves on the value before the price is stated.

## The Core Distinction

Pampa opens Section 9 with an explicit rejection of the dominant copywriting convention:

**Value Stacking (what Pampa rejects)**:
> "This course is worth $5,000. This template is worth $2,000. This bonus is worth $1,500. Total value: $8,500. But today, you get it all for just $497!"

Why it fails, per source: "Completely disingenuous. Nobody believes it. Insults the viewer's intelligence. Hurts credibility." → `🚀 SECTION 9 PRICE ANCHORING` line 17-22. Pampa credits Russell Brunson but labels this approach "flawed in modern markets" because sophistication has risen.

**Price Anchoring (what Pampa uses)**: get the prospect to convince themselves of the value through their own math — then state the price into that self-generated anchor.

## The 5-Step Framework

### Step 1: The Question

Ask the prospect to estimate value themselves.

**Formula**: "How much do you think [result] is worth?"

**Source examples**:
- "How much do you think it's worth to add $50K per month to your business?"
- "What's it worth to never have to worry about lead generation again?"
- "How valuable would it be to have a VSL that converts at 15% instead of 2%?"

**Why it works**: gets them thinking about value from *their* perspective, not yours. Their number is always more credible to them than yours would be.

### Step 2: Analogies & Examples

Compare the outcome to known costs in their world.

**Coach example from source**: "A single high-ticket client pays you $5,000. If this system helps you close just ONE additional client per month, it's paid for itself 10x over in the first year."

**E-commerce example**: "You're probably spending $3,000-$5,000 per month on ads right now. If this VSL increases your conversion rate by even 3%, you'll make an extra $10,000+ per month. That's $120,000 per year from a one-time investment."

**Info product example**: "Most people spend $10,000-$50,000 trying to figure this out through trial and error. They hire multiple copywriters, test dozens of funnels, waste months of time. This gives you the proven system for a fraction of that cost."

**Why it works**: known-cost references ground the math. Abstract value claims don't convert; specific comparisons to actual spending do.

### Step 3: Cost of Inaction

Make the cost of NOT solving the problem explicit — usually much larger than the price.

**Business example from source**: "Every month you don't fix this, you're losing money. If your VSL is converting at 2% instead of 10%, and you're sending 10,000 visitors per month... that's 800 lost sales. At $500 per sale, that's $400,000 per month you're leaving on the table. $4.8 million per year. Can you really afford NOT to fix this?"

**Coach example**: "If you keep doing what you're doing now, you'll probably stay at the same income level for the next 6-12 months. That's 6-12 months of your life you'll never get back."

**Info product example**: "The average person spends 18 months trying to figure this out on their own. That's 18 months of lost revenue. If you value your time at even $100/hour, that's $216,000 in opportunity cost."

**Why it works**: inverts the prospect's default decision frame. Instead of "should I spend $X on this?" they compute "can I afford to NOT spend $X?" The perceived cost of inaction is usually 10-100x the price of the offer.

### Step 4: Let Them Conclude

Guide them to the value conclusion without stating it directly.

**Source example**: "So ask yourself: If this system could add even $10,000 per month to your business — and we've proven it can do much more than that — what would that be worth to you? $10K per month is $120K per year. Over 5 years, that's $600K. Would you invest $5K today to make $600K over the next 5 years? Of course you would. That's a 120x return on investment. Now, I'm not asking you to invest $5K..."

**Why it works**: the prospect arrives at the value number through their own reasoning. Self-generated conclusions are durable; told conclusions are forgotten.

### Step 5: The Price Reveal

Two approaches:

**Approach A: Contrast Price**
> "Based on that math, I could easily charge $10,000 for this program. And honestly, it would be worth it. But that's not what I'm charging. The investment to join Cloud Profit Scaling Secrets is $2,997."

**Approach B: Direct Statement**
> "The investment to join Cloud Profit Scaling Secrets is $2,997. One time. No recurring fees. Full access to everything."

Choose Contrast when the offer is significantly below the mental anchor they've just built. Choose Direct when the price itself is positioned as a selling point (low-ticket) or when over-justification would feel salesy.

## When to State Price vs. When Not To

**Always state the price if**:
- **Low ticket (<$297)**: the low price IS a selling point. Hiding it creates friction.
- **Getting too many unqualified leads on high-ticket**: stating price filters out non-buyers, improves sales call economics.

**Don't state the price if**:
- **High-ticket + qualified leads**: price becomes a negotiation point instead of a conversion point. Better handled on the sales call.
- **Price is high and offer is complex**: needs explanation/context, requires consultation.

**Exception**: if hiding price reduces volume so much it hurts profits, you have three options: (a) state the price, (b) improve the offer/VSL, (c) lower the price. Don't just keep hiding and hope.

## Price Anchoring by Offer Type

**High-Ticket Coaching ($5K-$20K)**:
> "How much is it worth to add $100K to your annual revenue? Most people would pay $10K for that result without hesitation — it's a 10x ROI. But before I tell you the investment, let me ask you this: What's the cost of staying where you are for another year? The opportunity cost is probably $200K+. The investment to work with us is $12,500. Now, I know that might sound like a lot. But when you compare it to the $200K you'll lose by NOT solving this, or the $100K+ you'll GAIN by implementing our system, it's actually the easiest decision you'll make this year."

**Mid-Ticket Course ($997-$2,997)**:
> "Think about what you've already spent trying to figure this out. Courses. Coaches. Trial and error. Most people spend $5,000-$10,000 and still don't have a system that works. We're giving you the exact system that's generated over $100M in sales for a one-time investment of $1,997. That's less than a single high-ticket sale for most of you. And if this helps you close even ONE extra deal per month, it pays for itself in 30 days."

**Low-Ticket Product ($47-$297)**:
> "How much is it worth to have a proven, plug-and-play system instead of guessing for the next 6 months? Most people waste $1,000-$2,000 on tools, courses, and failed attempts before they find something that works. The investment is just $97. One time. That's less than you'd spend on dinner and drinks this weekend. And this could change your entire business."

## Invariants

- **Self-generated value first, stated price second** — never reverse this.
- **Cost-of-inaction math must be specific**, with real numbers from the prospect's world. Generic "you're losing money" doesn't anchor.
- **Never use disingenuous value stacks** — the modern market sees through them and punishes credibility.
- **Price reveal must follow the cost-of-inaction beat** — otherwise the prospect compares price to cash in hand, not to opportunity lost.
- **Transition directly into Guarantee (Step 10)** after the price reveal — don't leave the price hanging without risk reversal.

## Common Mistakes

(a) **Stating price too early** (before the 5-step anchor is built) — price sounds expensive because there's no anchor above it.
(b) **Using value-stack math ($X + $Y + $Z = total value)** — signals "old-school guru" to sophisticated audiences.
(c) **Weak cost-of-inaction math** — round-numbered, generic, non-specific. "You'll lose out" doesn't anchor.
(d) **Telling them the value instead of letting them conclude it** — self-generated conclusions stick; told ones evaporate.
(e) **Contrast Price when not needed** — over-justifying a fair price with "I could charge $10K" can feel salesy if the price is already clearly reasonable.
(f) **Stating price on high-ticket with unqualified lead flow** — trains the market to negotiate instead of apply.

## Price Anchoring Checklist

- [ ] Asked them to estimate value themselves (Step 1)
- [ ] Used analogies/comparisons to make value tangible (Step 2)
- [ ] Showed cost of inaction with specific numbers (Step 3)
- [ ] Guided them to value conclusion through their own math (Step 4)
- [ ] Stated price clearly if appropriate for offer type (Step 5)
- [ ] Used contrast pricing if price is materially below the anchor
- [ ] Made the price feel like a no-brainer investment
- [ ] Transitioned into Guarantee / Risk Reversal
