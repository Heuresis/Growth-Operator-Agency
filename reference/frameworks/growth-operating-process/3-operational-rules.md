# 3 Non-Negotiable Operational Rules for Scaling to $2M/yr

> **Why this matters:** Scaling a coaching business past $1M is almost always an operational problem, not a marketing problem. These three rules — revenue-threshold hiring, single source of truth, and weekly operating rhythm — are the minimum floor. Operators who skip any one of them stall between $1M and $2M. Operators who enforce all three move through.

## Source / Lineage

Sanjay Slings — operating-process practitioner. These three rules are drawn from his observed pattern across 100+ high-ticket operators, and overlap with the Eli Pampa hiring model (revenue-threshold hiring) and classical single-source-of-truth data discipline. They are the non-negotiable floor — not the ceiling — of an operating system.

## Rule 1 — Revenue-Threshold Hiring (Pampa Model)

### Statement

**Do not hire a role until revenue has crossed the threshold that mathematically requires it. Hire from cash flow, not from hope.**

### Why It Works

Pre-threshold hires fail in two predictable ways: (a) the hire drains cash before they can generate it, and (b) the founder under-manages them because the workload isn't yet real. Either way, the hire underperforms and gets blamed for an operating mistake.

### Threshold Table (high-ticket coaching)

| Hire | Revenue trigger | Why this trigger |
|------|-----------------|------------------|
| Setter / DM responder | $30k MRR | Founder's inbox saturates at ~15–20 qualified DMs/day; past this point the setter pays for themselves within 60 days. |
| Closer / sales rep | $80k MRR or 40+ booked calls/month | Below this, the founder should still be closing to keep pulse on objections and positioning. |
| Client-success manager | 30 active clients OR 50% of founder's time on delivery | Below 30 clients the founder still has a direct feedback loop; past this, delivery quality decays without a CSM. |
| Content producer / editor | $50k MRR AND founder spending 15+ hrs/week on editing | Editing is the first outsource; producing is the last. Producer is usually a later hire. |
| Operations manager | $1M ARR OR 5+ team members | Below this, the founder is the ops manager. Above this, founder bandwidth is the binding constraint. |
| Media buyer / paid ads lead | $50k/month ad spend OR $200k MRR | Below $50k/mo spend, a contractor outperforms a full-time hire on cost basis. |
| CFO / fractional finance | $2M ARR | Below this, a bookkeeper + founder review is sufficient. |

### Enforcement Mechanism

- Maintain a **hiring ledger** (not just a hiring plan). Each potential hire has a written trigger metric and threshold.
- Review in the weekly operating rhythm (Rule 3): is any threshold being approached? Is any threshold being crossed without the hire being planned?
- Hires made outside the ledger require a written justification entered into the decision log (part of Rule 2).

### Failure Modes

- **Hiring on ambition:** "We're about to 2x, let's hire now." Ambition is not a trigger. Booked revenue is.
- **Hiring on fatigue:** "I'm exhausted, I need help." Burnout is real, but the fix is usually process (SOP, automation) before headcount.
- **Hiring a role instead of a deliverable:** hiring a "Head of Marketing" with no defined weekly output produces overhead, not outcomes. Hire deliverables first, titles later.

## Rule 2 — Single Source of Truth for Every Metric

### Statement

**Every metric the business runs on must have one authoritative source. Competing dashboards are the number-one cause of decision paralysis and false confidence.**

### Why It Works

When two dashboards disagree, the team defaults to the one that tells the more flattering story. Decisions get made on the wrong number. Over a quarter, this compounds into wasted spend and missed constraints.

### Implementation

1. **Metric inventory.** Start with the metrics from the 8-stage customer-journey audit. Typically 15–25 metrics for a $1–$2M coaching business.
2. **Assign ownership.** Each metric has one human owner who is accountable for the number being correct, current, and in one location.
3. **Designate the source.** For each metric, name the authoritative tool (CRM, ad platform, payment processor, calendar tool) and the authoritative field within it.
4. **Build one dashboard.** A single weekly ops dashboard reads from the authoritative sources. No parallel dashboards. If a team member wants a new view, they extend this one — they do not build a second.
5. **Decision log.** Every decision > $5k or > 1 week of team time is logged alongside the metric snapshot it was based on. This makes retrospective analysis possible.

### The Canonical Metric Set (high-ticket coaching, $500k–$2M)

- **Acquisition:** reach, engagement rate, new followers, opt-ins, cost per lead.
- **Conversion:** lead-to-MQL, MQL-to-call, show rate, close rate, cash-collected.
- **Delivery:** onboarding completion, time-to-first-win, refund rate, NPS.
- **Retention / expansion:** active clients, churn, upgrade rate, referral rate.
- **Financial:** revenue (contracted), revenue (cash), gross margin, CAC, LTV, runway.

### Enforcement Mechanism

- The weekly operating meeting (Rule 3) reads from the dashboard. No screens from other tools are shared. If a number isn't on the dashboard, it isn't discussed.
- Quarterly audit: the metric-inventory list is reviewed. Metrics no longer driving decisions are removed. New metrics require a named owner before being added.

### Failure Modes

- **Shadow dashboards:** a team member maintains their own spreadsheet. Tolerated once, permanent after three months. Kill early.
- **Stale data:** the dashboard exists but the data is a week out of date. The fix is automation or a named human refresh — not "we'll check manually."
- **Vanity metrics:** tracking reach without tracking conversion. Every metric on the dashboard must trace to a decision that gets made with it.

## Rule 3 — Weekly Operating Rhythm

### Statement

**A standing weekly meeting, same day and time, same attendees, same agenda, with decisions closed in the room. Remove either the rhythm or the closure and operations drift within 6 weeks.**

### Why It Works

Weekly cadence matches the marketing / sales cycle for most coaching businesses. Fortnightly is too slow — constraints aren't surfaced fast enough. Daily is too frequent — it surfaces noise, not signal. Weekly, with discipline, is the operator's pulse.

### Agenda (60–90 minutes, fixed)

1. **Metrics read (10 min)** — the dashboard is walked. Leading indicators first (applications, calls booked), then lagging (revenue, churn).
2. **Red / amber / green (10 min)** — each area owner flags status vs. target. No debate yet.
3. **Constraint identification (10 min)** — what is the single biggest constraint this week?
4. **Decisions (20 min)** — what are we changing? Each decision has an owner, a deliverable, and a deadline (usually next week's meeting).
5. **Blockers (10 min)** — what's stuck? Who owns unblocking it?
6. **Debrief from last week's decisions (10 min)** — did last week's decisions ship? If not, why?
7. **Close (5 min)** — next meeting time confirmed, action items logged.

### Lagging vs. Leading Indicators

- **Lagging (end-of-week read):** revenue, customers closed, refunds, NPS.
- **Leading (mid-week signals):** applications, calls booked, show rate, content engagement, DM volume.

Leading indicators predict next week's lagging indicators. If the weekly meeting only reads lagging, the team is always reacting. Leading-first agenda is the fix.

### Enforcement Mechanism

- Meeting happens even if one key attendee is out. Decisions get made without them; they review the log afterwards.
- Every decision has a next-week checkpoint. Decisions without checkpoints rot.
- Meeting notes are written live in one document. The document is the record. Verbal agreements without the log do not exist operationally.

### Failure Modes

- **Status-reporting theatre:** the meeting becomes "here's what I did this week" with no decisions made. Fix: move status reporting to async (Slack, Loom) and keep the live meeting for decisions only.
- **Meeting bloat:** attendee count creeps from 3 to 8. Each added attendee halves decision velocity. Protect attendee list.
- **Decision drift:** decisions are made but no one owns shipping them. Fix: every decision has a named owner and a deadline, logged in the same document.
- **Skipping weeks:** founder travels, meeting gets skipped. Two skipped weeks = operational drift that takes a quarter to repair. Never skip; reschedule.

## Interaction Between the Three Rules

- **Rule 1 depends on Rule 2.** Revenue-threshold hiring only works if the revenue metric is reliable (single source of truth).
- **Rule 2 depends on Rule 3.** A single source of truth that isn't read weekly becomes shelfware within a quarter.
- **Rule 3 depends on Rule 1.** A weekly operating rhythm without enough staff (or with the wrong staff) degrades into founder-only monologue.

Missing any one rule breaks the other two within 60–90 days. Operators who implement one at a time should do so in the order: Rule 2 (data) → Rule 3 (rhythm) → Rule 1 (hiring).

## Non-applicable scenarios

- Pre-$300k businesses where the founder is the only operator. Weekly self-review is sufficient; standing meetings are overhead.
- VC-backed startups with heavy finance/ops infrastructure — they have different rules driven by board cadence.
- Agencies with fortnightly client cycles — the weekly rhythm may need to be replaced with a bi-weekly ops + weekly client rhythm.
