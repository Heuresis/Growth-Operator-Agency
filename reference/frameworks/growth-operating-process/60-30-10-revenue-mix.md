# 60 / 30 / 10 Revenue Mix Rule

> **Why this matters:** Coaches and consultants who scale past $1M/year almost always destabilise by over-investing in experiments before the core is defendable, or by riding a single offer too long. The 60/30/10 mix is a budgeting rule that keeps the business solvent, focused, and still learning.

## Source / Lineage

the operations director — operating-process practitioner. The 60/30/10 rule is his default portfolio allocation for single-founder and small-team coaching businesses moving from $500k to $5M in annual revenue. It is deliberately blunt: it forces the operator to pick one core offer and defend it, rather than building four mediocre offers at once.

## The Rule

Of **revenue** (not time, not team headcount — revenue), target the mix:

| Bucket | Share | What it is | Why it gets this share |
|--------|-------|------------|------------------------|
| Core offer | 60% | The flagship high-ticket offer. One avatar, one transformation, one price point. | Concentration creates compounding: testimonials, SOPs, content all reinforce the same offer. Below 60% and the brand stops being legible. |
| Ascension | 30% | Next-tier service (mastermind, consulting retainer, done-with-you program) or recurring layer (membership, community, tooling). | Customers who succeed at the core need a next step. Without ascension, churn leaves the ecosystem and LTV caps out. |
| Experimentation | 10% | New offers being tested, affiliate revenue, speaking fees, licensing, small digital products, workshops. | A fixed experimentation budget keeps the operator learning without bleeding focus from the core. |

## Why This Mix (Not 70/20/10 or 50/40/10)

- **60% core, not 70%:** above 70%, single-offer concentration risk dominates. A single bad cohort, a platform change, or an avatar shift can halve the business overnight.
- **30% ascension, not 20%:** below 25%, the ascension layer is too small to justify the operational overhead (delivery team, separate content, separate sales). It has to be a real second line, not a token.
- **10% experimentation, not 20%:** above 15%, experimentation starts pulling founder attention away from the core. Experiments that win get promoted into the ascension bucket — they don't stay at 20%+ indefinitely.

## How the Mix Drifts (and What That Signals)

### Drift 1: Core > 75%

- **Symptom:** "We just had our best quarter" quickly followed by a flat or declining quarter.
- **Root cause:** no ascension layer. Customers finish, have nothing to buy next, churn.
- **Fix:** build or formalise the ascension offer within 90 days. Start with a beta price to existing customers.

### Drift 2: Core < 50%

- **Symptom:** pipeline is confused, content feels generic, sales calls ask "what exactly do you do?"
- **Root cause:** too many offers competing for audience attention. Brand has split.
- **Fix:** kill or park 1–2 offers. Re-consolidate content and sales around the core for 1–2 quarters.

### Drift 3: Ascension > 40%

- **Symptom:** revenue looks healthy, but new-customer acquisition has stalled.
- **Root cause:** the operator is milking the existing base without feeding the top of funnel. Unsustainable past 12 months.
- **Fix:** re-fund acquisition (ads, content, setter capacity) from the ascension cash flow for 1–2 quarters.

### Drift 4: Experimentation > 20%

- **Symptom:** founder feels scattered, team is confused about priorities, weekly operating rhythm is drifting.
- **Root cause:** too many bets, no conviction on any of them.
- **Fix:** pick the top 1–2 experiments, promote to ascension (if validated) or kill (if not). Reset to 10%.

### Drift 5: Experimentation < 5%

- **Symptom:** revenue is flat quarter-over-quarter. Nothing new is being tested.
- **Root cause:** operator is over-indexed on stability. Business is fragile to external change.
- **Fix:** fund one experiment per quarter, even if it's small. The point is learning, not the revenue.

## Decision Tree for Reallocating

```
Has the mix drifted beyond ±10% of target in any bucket for 2 consecutive quarters?
├── NO → keep operating. Review next quarter.
└── YES → which bucket?
    ├── Core drift
    │   ├── Too high (>70%) → build ascension. Start with existing customers.
    │   └── Too low (<50%) → kill competing offers. Re-consolidate.
    ├── Ascension drift
    │   ├── Too high (>40%) → re-fund acquisition for 1–2 quarters.
    │   └── Too low (<20%) → diagnose: no ascension offer, or existing customers not upgrading?
    │       ├── No ascension offer → build one.
    │       └── Customers not upgrading → fix delivery on the core (no first-win = no upgrade).
    └── Experimentation drift
        ├── Too high (>15%) → pick top 1–2 experiments, promote or kill the rest.
        └── Too low (<5%) → fund one new experiment this quarter. Small is fine.
```

## Example Allocations by Revenue Stage

### $500k – $1M / year (solo + 1–2 contractors)

- Core: $300–600k (60%) — 1:1 coaching or small-group program.
- Ascension: $150–300k (30%) — retainer or mastermind.
- Experimentation: $50–100k (10%) — one workshop series, one digital product.

### $1M – $3M / year (small team, 3–6 people)

- Core: $600k–1.8M (60%) — flagship cohort program.
- Ascension: $300–900k (30%) — mastermind + alumni retainer.
- Experimentation: $100–300k (10%) — licensing pilot, affiliate, speaking.

### $3M – $5M / year (team of 7–15)

- Core: $1.8–3M (60%) — flagship, possibly two variants (entry + enterprise).
- Ascension: $900k–1.5M (30%) — mastermind, done-with-you, advisory.
- Experimentation: $300–500k (10%) — software pilot, partnership revenue, IP licensing.

## Why This Rule Survives Scaling

Most revenue-mix rules break at scale because they assume a consumer model. This one survives because it assumes: (a) the founder's attention is the binding constraint, (b) LTV expansion is where margin lives, and (c) experimentation is a budgeted cost, not a hope. Those three assumptions hold from $500k through $5M, which is the band this rule targets. Above $5M, the business usually needs to split into product lines and this mix no longer applies cleanly.

## Non-applicable scenarios

- Pre-$500k businesses — there is no ascension layer yet; over-index on core (80–90%).
- Agencies with retainer-heavy revenue (75%+ recurring) — use a retention-weighted mix instead.
- Info-product businesses with no high-touch offer (pure digital at <$500) — portfolio economics differ; use a content-velocity model.
- Enterprise consulting with 3–5 clients — concentration is the model; the rule doesn't apply.
