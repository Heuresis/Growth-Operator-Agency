This file encodes the VSL director's own source material section-by-section. For each of the 15 steps: what it does, example dialogue from source, and the most common failure modes.

## Pre-VSL: Market Research

Before writing a single word, complete research protocol (`🎯 PRE-VSL MARKET RESEARCH REQUIREMENTS`):

- DON'T read Reddit threads, random comment sections, or conduct surface-level interviews.
- DO read the exact language of people who have already purchased, study people marketed to successfully, and analyze conversion data from existing campaigns.
- Key distinction: study those who converted, not those who browsed.

Checklist: identify top 5-10 competitor VSLs, collect testimonials from actual buyers, document customer's exact language, identify emotional triggers that led to purchase, map current beliefs vs. required beliefs.

## Step 1: Hook (0-30 seconds)

**What it does.** Pattern interrupt plus a slippery slope where each line makes them watch the next. First line earns the second; last line earns the CTA. Installs Belief #1 (Reproduction — "I want this outcome").

**The 7 hook elements** (source Section 1):

1. **Explains benefits** — "This framework will double your sales calls."
2. **Elaborates on differences** — "Our fitness plan focuses on micronutrients, not macros."
3. **WTF Factor** — "This Google Doc made me $200K and I didn't have a website."
4. **Time constraint** — "Once we fill capacity, we're closing doors."
5. **Teases the story** — "When I sat down at the piano, they laughed at me..." (classic from 1920s, still prints millions).
6. **Authority** — "This framework is used by Spotify, NASA, and Apple."
7. **Magic** — the intangible X-factor from writing thousands of hooks.

**11 strengthening techniques**: include an example (specificity), modalities (5 senses), paradox, question format, newness + authority + exclusivity, metaphor/simile, name the problem ("Unscalable Consulting" printed $500K+/month), address who can/can't buy, show how easy it is, dramatize the claim, ChatGPT variations.

**Example from source** (high-ticket coaching): "They call me the king of $50K closes. I've closed over $47M in high-ticket sales using a single framework. And in the next 27 minutes, I'm going to show you exactly how it works — even if you've never sold anything before." → `🚀 SECTION 1 THE HOOK` line 320.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Leading with backstory — the hook is not about you, it's about them. (b) Generic benefit language ("make more money") instead of specific ("$30K extra per month"). (c) Missing the curiosity gap — the hook tells too much; they don't need to watch. (d) No WTF factor — nothing to resolve, brain moves on.

## Step 2: Lead (30 seconds-2 minutes)

**What it does.** Maintains the slippery slope. Deepens the desire, elaborates the problem teased in the hook, teases the unique mechanism without revealing it. Installs Belief #2 (Derivative Desire).

**5 functions**: (1) build mystery and intrigue, (2) elaborate on the problem, (3) tease the unique mechanism, (4) establish authority quickly, (5) invite them to keep watching.

**Example from source**: "In the next 23 minutes, I'm going to show you exactly why your VSL is converting at 2% when it should be converting at 15%. And no, it's not your hook. It's not your offer. It's not even your traffic. The real problem is something called 'belief fragmentation' — and 99% of marketers have never even heard of it. This single concept cost me $127,000 in failed campaigns before I figured it out..." → `🚀 SECTION 2 THE LEAD` line 127.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Revealing the mechanism too early — the tease is the engine. (b) Full backstory dump before Step 5 — that's Hero Story's job. (c) Weak authority signal — "I help people" vs. "This method generated $47M across 12 industries." (d) No explicit payoff promise for watching.

## Step 3: Qualification (2-4 minutes)

**What it does.** Self-select in and out. Creates tribal identity. Uses psychological disqualification — humans want what they can't have. Reinforces Belief #1.

**Structure**: (1) describe who this IS for, (2) describe who this is NOT for using "silver lining" method — every disqualification must use negative characteristics the viewer rejects, so they self-qualify. (3) Create "I'm in" moment.

**Example from source**: "We do not take lazy or dumb people in this program. This will not work for them." Effect: nobody wants to identify as lazy or dumb → "that's not me, so this IS for me." → `🚀 SECTION 3 QUALIFICATION` line 53.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Soft qualification ("this might be for you if...") — no tribal identity formed. (b) Realistic disqualifications that people actually identify with — kills conversion instead of creating it. (c) Skipping this step — audience doesn't feel chosen, stays passive.

## Step 4: Problem & Question Introduction (4-7 minutes)

**What it does.** Treat the problem at psychological depth (not surface-level). Begin answering the "how" question teased in hook and lead. Start installing Belief #4 (Urgency).

**2 objectives**: (1) problem analysis at depth — state the obvious problem, reveal the deeper hidden problem underneath, explain why conventional solutions fail. (2) Answer the "how" question — deliver on the promise, explain the mechanism partially.

**Example of the "hidden problem" move**: "You think your problem is that you don't have enough leads. But that's not the real problem. The REAL problem is that you're attracting the WRONG leads — people who will never buy, no matter how good your sales process is." → `🚀 SECTION 4 PROBLEM & QUESTION INTRODUCTION` line 27.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Staying at surface-level problem description — prospect thinks "I already know that." (b) Revealing full mechanism here — steals thunder from Solution section. (c) No story integration — facts without narrative don't install belief.

## Step 5: Hero Story & Guru Introduction (7-12 minutes)

**What it does.** Emotional crescendo + vulnerability + breakthrough. Installs Belief #7 (Trust-Person) by showing you overcame the same struggle. Integrates the Guru Intro — not a separate bio section.

**6-phase Hero's Journey**: Ordinary World → Call to Adventure → Trials & Failures → Death & Rebirth → The Gift → The Return. (See `hero-story-architecture.md` for full treatment.)

**Length calibration**: low-ticket/low-sophistication → 5-8 min, high emotion, high detail. High-ticket/high-sophistication → 2-4 min, moderate emotion, relevant details only.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Skipping the Dark Night phase — no "all hope lost" moment, no catharsis on the rebound. (b) Credentials listed separately as an "about me" section — boring and breaks the emotional arc. (c) Over-exaggerating the struggle — loses believability. (d) Mismatching length to audience sophistication.

## Step 6: Solution Introduction (12-15 minutes)

**What it does.** Reveal the mechanism, name it, introduce the product as the vehicle for the mechanism. Installs Belief #3 (Can-Fulfill) and Belief #5 (This-Offer).

**2 approaches**: (1) Solution = Product when the product was built specifically for this problem. (2) Solution → Product when the concept needs explanation before packaging reveal.

**3-level mechanism explanation**: Level 1 "What it is" → Level 2 "How it works" → Level 3 "Why it works" (psychology/science/data grounding).

**Example from source**: "So I took this entire framework — everything I learned from generating over $100M in VSL sales — and packaged it all together inside the Cloud Profit 8-Figure VSL System. It's the exact blueprint I use to write VSLs that convert at 10%+ for myself and my clients." → `🚀 SECTION 6 SOLUTION INTRODUCTION` line 105.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Naming the product before explaining the mechanism — product feels like sales pitch. (b) Mechanism too technical — prospect loses comprehension. (c) No "why it works" grounding — feels like opinion, not proof.

## Step 7: Features & Benefits (15-20 minutes)

**What it does.** Feature-benefit mapping. Features tell, benefits sell. Reinforces Belief #3 and Belief #6 (Better/Faster).

**Formula**: `[Feature] → which means → [Benefit]`. Apply the 7 hook elements to each feature. Use power words (proven, guaranteed, breakthrough, secret, exclusive, advanced, elite, complete, step-by-step, comprehensive).

**Example**: "Feature: Done-For-You VSL Audit & Rewrite. → Benefit: This alone could 5-10x your conversion rate, which means this entire program could pay for itself in the first 30 days." → `🚀 SECTION 7 FEATURES & BENEFITS` line 121.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Feature dump with no benefit translation — "you get 8 modules" is not a benefit. (b) Too many features — cognitive overload. (c) Features without fascination — treating the list as a manifest, not a sell.

## Step 8: Testimonials & Receipts (20-23 minutes)

**What it does.** Spam social proof. Removes doubt through third-party validation. Installs Belief #3 + Belief #7 + Belief #8 (Trust-Expertise).

**4 types of proof**: (1) written testimonials (screenshot format + face + specific results), (2) video testimonials (30-90s, emotional delivery, before/after contrast), (3) receipts (revenue dashboards, Stripe notifications, bank statements), (4) case studies (full client journey — Problem → Solution → Result).

**Story-based testimonial structure**: BEFORE (the problem, what they tried, how they felt) → DISCOVERY (finding you, why they bought) → IMPLEMENTATION (what they did, how long, challenges) → AFTER (what's different now, specific results, emotional state, what they'd say to others).

**Objection-matched testimonials**: pair each common objection with a testimonial that directly refutes it — "won't work in my niche" → someone from their exact niche, "don't have time" → someone who was busy and succeeded, "not technical" → someone who was non-technical.

**Source quote**: "Testimonials are the only thing you cannot fake in marketing because there's energy behind them." → `🚀 SECTION 8 TESTIMONIALS & RECEIPTS` line 11.

**Common mistakes.** (a) All testimonials clustered in one block — better to sprinkle throughout + a dedicated section. (b) Generic "it was great!" testimonials — no specifics, no energy. (c) No receipts — claims feel speculative. (d) Testimonials that don't match the offer type / audience sophistication.

## Step 9: Price Anchoring (23-27 minutes)

**What it does.** Get them to sell themselves on the value before the price is stated. Reinforces Belief #5 (This-Offer) + Belief #6 (Better/Faster).

**Critical distinction from Russell Brunson's value stacking** — the VSL director rejects "$5,000 course + $2,000 template + $1,500 bonus = $8,500 value, today only $497" as disingenuous. Nobody believes it; insults the viewer.

**5-step framework**: (1) ask them to estimate value, (2) analogies & examples, (3) compare cost of inaction, (4) let them conclude value, (5) reveal price (contrast or direct).

Full treatment: `price-anchoring-mechanics.md`.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Stating the price before anchoring the value — price sounds high. (b) Using Brunson-style value stacks — sounds fake to sophisticated audiences. (c) No cost-of-inaction math — prospect only compares price to cash in hand, not to the opportunity lost.

## Step 10: Guarantee & Risk Reversal (27-30 minutes)

**What it does.** Remove risk. Make the decision feel safe. Reinforces Belief #3 (Can-Fulfill) and Belief #7 (Trust-Person).

**5 guarantee types**: (1) Money-Back (standard, low-mid ticket), (2) Results Guarantee ("get $X or refund + pay you $500"), (3) Better-Than-Money-Back (refund + extra compensation), (4) Conditional ("do the work + get your refund if no results"), (5) No Guarantee (ultra-high ticket $50K+ custom work).

**Strengthening techniques**: extend timeline (30 → 60 → 90 → lifetime), make unconditional ("no questions asked"), add value on refund ("keep the bonuses"), show ease ("one email, 24hr processing").

**Guarantee presentation structure**: (1) state the guarantee clearly, (2) explain why you can offer it, (3) show how easy to use, (4) reframe risk (it's on us, not you), (5) transition to urgency.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Weak guarantee for strong offer — communicates lack of confidence. (b) Guarantee mismatched to realistic fulfillment. (c) Complicated refund process — friction signals fear.

## Step 11: Urgency Story (30-33 minutes)

**What it does.** Legitimate reason why action must happen now. Finalizes Belief #4 (Urgency).

**Golden rule**: "If you have a BELIEVABLE reason why this offer won't last forever, include it here." Key word: believable.

**4 types**: (1) Limited Spots (strongest — "we can only work with 25 clients per quarter because..."), (2) Market Conditions ("recession, platform shift, algorithm window"), (3) Price Increase (needs strong justification — tell the story), (4) Enrolment Windows (cohort-based programs).

**Full urgency story structure**: state the urgency → explain WHY it's limited → show consequences of waiting → give specific deadline → transition to close.

**Example of the "why it's limited" story**: "Three months ago, we tried scaling to 60 clients at once. Quality dropped. Support response times went from 2 hours to 2 days. So we made the decision to cap at 25 per cohort." → `🚀 SECTION 11 URGENCY STORY` line 137-147.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Countdown timers that reset on refresh — kills credibility. (b) "Only 3 spots left" that never closes — market sees through it. (c) Arbitrary deadlines without justification. (d) Urgency without story — facts without narrative don't install belief.

## Step 12: Crossroads Close (33-38 minutes)

**What it does.** Force the decision. Present two futures — Path 1 Nightmare (inaction) vs. Path 2 Dream (action) — then ask a burden-of-proof question that demands an answer.

**Structure**: nightmare scenario (specific, emotional, real, not exaggerated) + dream scenario (specific, achievable, transformational) + burden-of-proof question (direct, emotional, logical, or identity-based).

**4 question variants**: (a) direct ("Path 1 or Path 2?"), (b) emotional ("which version of yourself do you want to be a year from now?"), (c) logical ("$2,997 once vs. $50K+/year in lost revenue — which makes sense?"), (d) identity ("are you the kind of person who takes action?").

Full treatment: `../sales/crossroads-close.md`.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Soft Crossroads ("some people do X, some do Y") — no force. (b) Pressure-laden Crossroads ("only losers stay stuck") — manipulative, kills trust. (c) Generic alternatives — be specific about what each path looks like. (d) No dignified framing of both choices.

## Step 13: Takeaway Close & Re-Qualification (38-41 minutes)

**What it does.** Carrot on a stick. Slightly take the opportunity away → desire spikes → give it back → they're ready to buy. Reinforces Belief #5 + Belief #6.

**Psychological sequence in the viewer's mind**: disqualification ("wait, they might not want me?") → desire increase ("I want this more now") → self-assessment ("am I that person?") → conclusion ("yes, I'm not lazy/dumb/uncommitted") → re-qualification ("this IS for me") → action ("I need to join before they decide I'm not qualified").

**Structure**: (1) list who this is NOT for — mindset-based, work-ethic-based, commitment-based, readiness-based. (2) Create takeaway feeling — "I'd rather work with 25 committed people than 100 tire-kickers." (3) Re-qualify with positive traits they have.

**Example from source**: "This program is for people who are SERIOUS. People who are willing to do the work. I'm not interested in working with people who won't succeed. It's bad for them, bad for me, and wastes everyone's time." → `🚀 SECTION 13 TAKEAWAY CLOSE & RE-QUALIFICATION` line 87-107.

**Common mistakes.** (a) Disqualifications that realistic people identify with — loses them. (b) Skipping re-qualification — leaves them feeling rejected. (c) Over-doing the exclusivity — starts to feel gimmicky.

## Step 14: Help Them Buy (41-43 minutes)

**What it does.** Assume the sale. Remove all friction. Show them exactly how to purchase.

**Source quote**: "If your product is amazing, then it's basically obvious that they need what you have to offer." Approach: don't ASK if they want to buy; ASSUME and show how.

**5-step framework**: (1) state the price again directly, (2) show all payment options (single payment, payment plan, multiple methods), (3) visual walkthrough (screen record the checkout — show where to click), (4) remove friction (tech concerns, time concerns, complexity concerns), (5) assumptive language throughout ("once you join..." / "after you get access..." — never "if you decide...").

**Full example from source**: "Here's exactly what you do next. You'll see a button below this video that says 'Join Now.' Click that button. It'll take you to a secure checkout page — same technology used by Apple and Amazon... Within 60 seconds, you'll get an email with your login credentials." → `🚀 SECTION 14 HELP THEM BUY` line 114-130.

**Common mistakes.** (a) "If you decide to buy..." language — reopens the decision. (b) No visual walkthrough — leaves friction at the checkout step. (c) Burying payment options — prospect fears the wrong commitment. (d) Skipping the 60-second framing — purchase feels like a commitment, not an action.

## Step 15: Future Pace (43-45 minutes)

**What it does.** Paint the post-purchase transformation — 60 seconds after → first week → first month → 6-month identity. Reinforces Belief #1 + Belief #2 (the original derivative desire, now felt as inevitable).

**5 components**: (1) what happens after purchase (immediate next steps), (2) tease benefits coming their way (Week 1 → Month 6), (3) tell them it's within their grasp (one decision away), (4) describe who they become (identity transformation), (5) final CTA.

**Emotional vs. logical calibration**: low-sophistication / emotional audience → go hard on dreams ("$12,000 in sales overnight, you smile, this is normal now"). High-sophistication / logical audience → stay grounded ("you'll have a VSL that consistently converts at 10%+, predictable revenue, systems you can scale").

**Language rule**: "once you buy" / "after you complete your purchase" / "when you log in" — never "if you buy" / "should you decide."

**Common mistakes.** (a) Benefit-recap instead of identity-level close — Future Pace is not a feature summary. (b) Mismatched emotional intensity — hitting logical audiences with lifestyle fantasy destroys credibility. (c) No final CTA — runway without takeoff.

## Copy-Invariants (Never Violate)

- Never sell before Step 9 — revealing price or offer earlier kills conversion.
- Mechanism must thread 5 places: Hook / Problem / Solution / Testimonials / Close.
- Testimonials must be isomorphic (same starting situation → same outcome → same process).
- Crossroads Close is non-optional (Step 12).
- Future Pace is always last (Step 15) — identity-level close, not a benefit recap.

## 5-Phase Psychological Compression (Short VSLs)

For 8-12 min paid-cold variants, compress to 5 phases:

| Phase | Time | Steps mapped |
|---|---|---|
| Pattern Interrupt | 0-3 min | 1-3 |
| Identity Crisis | 3-8 min | 4-5 |
| New Worldview | 8-15 min | 6-8 |
| Future Vision | 15-22 min | 9-10 + testimonials |
| Decision Stack | 22-30 min | 11-15 |

## Production Rules

- On-camera, on-location filmed (never remote for top-conversion VSLs) → `B2 VSL ARCHITECTURE` line 156.
- Record every take.
- 99% text-based review comms (Telegram), minimal meetings.
- Post-production: cut ruthlessly — every sentence earns the next.

## Optimization Loop

- Track: play rate (70%+ benchmark), watch time (40%+ completion strong), drop-off points, conversion rate (10%+ elite), engagement hotspots.
- Normie Feedback Experiment (`🎬 SECTION 17`): 5-10 non-marketers, headphones, observe — when they take notes, laugh, look away, check phone, lean forward, lean back. This is data Vidalytics cannot give you: the *why*, not just the *what*.
- Iterate every 60-90 days on the #1 drop-off first.
