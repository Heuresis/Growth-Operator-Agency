# influence-principles research — 6 Principles of Influence (+ Unity, 7th)

> **Why this matters:** the 6 Principles' principles are not tricks; they are the observable defaults of human decision-making under load. Every high-performing VSL, webinar, and funnel uses all seven, whether the author knows their names or not. Naming them lets you audit for absence and overuse.

## Source / Lineage

- influence-principles research, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984)
- influence-principles research, *Pre-Suasion* (2016) — adds Unity as the 7th
- Field-tested refinements via Jeff Walker (PLF), Russell Brunson (DotCom Secrets), the offer architect, the campaign director

## The Seven Principles

1. Reciprocity
2. Commitment & Consistency
3. Social Proof
4. Authority
5. Liking
6. Scarcity
7. Unity (2016 addition)

## 1. Reciprocity

**Definition.** When given something of value, people feel a felt obligation to give something in return.

**Operational use.**
- VSL: deliver the core insight in the first 8 minutes before the pitch. The viewer owes you attention.
- Webinar: content-training segment (40–55 min) must stand alone as valuable if pitch is skipped.
- Funnel: lead-magnet must be usable without the upsell. Cheap PDFs do not trigger reciprocity.
- Sales call: send a tailored pre-call brief the night before. The prospect arrives feeling indebted.

**Field examples.**
- "Here is the full Cognitive-Sediment audit template. Use it tonight. If it works, you will know what our $4,800 program does in week one."
- Ramit Sethi's "Earn $1,000 in 30 days" free course runs 3 weeks before the paid launch.

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Stacking gifts until the asker signals "obligation trap" — recipient refuses on principle.
- Low-value gift + high-value ask. Reciprocity is proportional; a $7 ebook does not earn a $30,000 contract.
- Reciprocity used as sole close. It accelerates but does not replace belief.

## 2. Commitment & Consistency

**Definition.** Once a small public commitment is made, behavior aligns to stay consistent with it.

**Operational use.**
- VSL: ask the viewer to type "YES" in comments when they recognize the problem. The typed commitment primes the close.
- Webinar: 3 polls inside the training — each is a micro-commitment.
- Funnel: application form before the call. Writing down "I want to earn $500K this year" in a form field pre-commits.
- Sales call: quote the prospect's own words back. "You said you are tired of broken systems — is that still true?"

**Field examples.**
- Jeff Walker's PLF prelaunch comments — each comment is a commitment that predicts launch-day buying.
- "Reply with your number one goal" email sequences — commitment-rate strongly correlates with conversion.

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Six commitment asks in one email. Reads as manipulation; unsubscribes spike.
- Forcing public commitment on cold traffic before reciprocity has been delivered.
- Binary "yes/no" pressure — "are you in or not?" — triggers reactance when belief is not yet built.

## 3. Social Proof

**Definition.** When uncertain, people look to peers' behavior as the correct behavior.

**Operational use.**
- VSL: 3 testimonial cutaways at 5-min intervals. Each under 30 seconds, each with a specific number.
- Webinar: case-study slides that match the viewer's starting point, not the impressive extremes.
- Funnel: review count and star average above the fold. Written reviews in a sidebar.
- Sales call: name-drop a client who matches the prospect's profile within the first 10 minutes.

**Field examples.**
- "4,200 founders. Average time saved: 22 hours per week. 94% would recommend." (Specific > round numbers.)
- Testimonial: "I was a solo coach at $8K months. Eight months in, I am at $82K months." (Named starting point, named ending point.)

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Celebrity proof for average-person offers. Disqualifies rather than persuades.
- Hundreds of testimonials that all sound the same. Triggers "bought reviews" pattern-match.
- Social proof without specifics. "Thousands of happy clients" reads as empty.

## 4. Authority

**Definition.** People defer to expertise-signals when the cost of being wrong is high.

**Operational use.**
- VSL: credentialing in the first 90 seconds — years in market, client count, publications, media mentions.
- Webinar: one teaching moment that only an authority could deliver (a non-obvious diagnostic, a counter-intuitive model).
- Funnel: about-page with research-track record, not just awards.
- Sales call: use technical vocabulary only an expert would use, then define it.

**Field examples.**
- "Author of *Breakthrough Advertising*, 1966. The book copywriters still buy at $125 used." — awareness-spectrum research's posthumous authority still sells.
- A surgeon selling a back-pain program opens with anatomical specificity before the pitch.

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Wall-of-certifications page with no outcomes. Authority without utility.
- Borrowed authority — "as seen on Forbes" when the mention was a pay-to-play directory. Sophisticated buyers detect it.
- Authority without warmth. Reads as clinical. Must pair with Liking.

## 5. Liking

**Definition.** People say yes more often to those they like. Liking comes from similarity, compliments, cooperation, and physical attractiveness.

**Operational use.**
- VSL: the founder tells a personal story of the same pain the viewer has. Similarity-based liking.
- Webinar: self-deprecation in the intro. Five seconds of "I was the worst at this" purchases an hour of attention.
- Funnel: founder photos that match the customer aesthetic, not corporate stock.
- Sales call: mirror the prospect's vocabulary and pace within the first 3 minutes.

**Field examples.**
- "I was a broke immigrant kid who could not book a client to save my life." — the acquisition economist's signature opener uses similarity.
- Hand-written thank-you cards after onboarding — liking, post-sale, drives referrals.

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Forced rapport — "I saw you are from Chicago, me too!" when it is not true. Discovered, it destroys the sale.
- Liking without authority. Reads as a friend asking for money.
- Over-familiarity with strangers — "hey buddy" to cold leads.

## 6. Scarcity

**Definition.** People value what is rare or threatened. Loss aversion amplifies this 2x over gain framing.

**Operational use.**
- VSL close: real deadline with a real reason — "I take 12 clients per quarter because each gets 90 min a week."
- Webinar: fast-action bonus with a real expiration — "decide inside the 24-hour window or the 1:1 audit is gone."
- Funnel: inventory counter (real, not faked) on spots.
- Sales call: "I have two slots this month" — only if true.

**Field examples.**
- "Cohort 14 opens April 15. Closes April 22. Next cohort: September." Real quarterly cadence.
- "Price goes to $4,800 at midnight" — if the page changes at midnight, scarcity is real.

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Fake countdown timers that reset on refresh. Detected once, trust gone forever.
- Perpetual scarcity — "only 3 left!" every week. Becomes background noise.
- Scarcity without reason. "Buy now!" with no "because" triggers reactance.

## 7. Unity (2016 addition)

**Definition.** Shared identity — not similarity, but "we are the same kind" — drives the strongest compliance. Family, tribe, religion, nation, profession-as-identity.

**Operational use.**
- VSL: speak to a named tribe ("for founders who refuse to run their company by Slack") rather than a demographic.
- Webinar: opening ritual that only the tribe recognizes (an insider joke, a shared enemy, a shared vocabulary).
- Funnel: apply-to-join framing instead of buy-to-enter.
- Sales call: "this program is only for operators who have already crossed $30K months" — Unity via gatekeeping.

**Field examples.**
- "Built by operators, for operators." — the word "operator" creates Unity where "entrepreneur" creates none.
- Private communities named with identity-first language ("The Studio," "The Order") rather than category ("Coaching Group").

**Overuse failure patterns.**
- Claimed Unity without enforcement. If everyone gets in, it is not a tribe.
- Identity-exclusion without substance. "You are the 1%" without the operator standard behind it collapses on first interaction.
- Unity without Authority. Tribe needs a credible center of gravity.

## The 7 in a VSL (mapped)

| Minute | Principle | Move |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–1:30 | Authority + Liking | Credential + personal story of same pain |
| 1:30–8:00 | Reciprocity | Deliver the core insight / free template |
| 8:00–15:00 | Social Proof | Three micro-cases (30s each) with specifics |
| 15:00–22:00 | Commitment | "Type YES if this matches you" + poll |
| 22:00–30:00 | Unity | Name the tribe this offer is for (and is not for) |
| 30:00–40:00 | Scarcity | Cohort close date + fast-action bonus |
| 40:00–close | Commitment + Authority | Offer + application + guarantee |
