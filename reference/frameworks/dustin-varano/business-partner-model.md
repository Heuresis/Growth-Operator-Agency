# Business-Partner Model (vs. Agency-Client)

> **Source / Lineage:** Dustin Varano (Lifecycle pillar). The operating model for how a growth operator engages with a 7-figure-adjacent info creator, contrasted against the agency-client default. This is the canonical reference for `skills/retention-check` and the Lifecycle SOP set.

## Why This Matters

Most operators who work with creators default, by habit and by contract, to an agency-client relationship: scoped project, retainer pricing, weekly-at-most cadence, reactive response model. Varano's central claim is that this relational contract cannot run a 7-figure info business. A creator at $100K-$400K/mo needs someone inside the operation, not outside it. That "inside" position is the business-partner model — and it is a discrete, named structure, not a vibe.

The model has four load-bearing pieces: compensation structure, cadence, roster size, and relational contract. Miss any one and the model collapses back to agency.

## The Core Rule

> *"Make them feel like a business partner, NOT an agency client."*

This is Varano's public-positioning phrasing. The rule is not a tone preference — it is a structural claim about how compensation, access, and response time must be set. An operator who says the words but retains retainer pricing and weekly-only calls is running an agency with a partner label.

## The Five-Dimension Comparison

| Dimension | Agency-client (avoid) | Business-partner (target) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Response time | Next-business-day | Same-day |
| Implementation cadence | Weekly / project-based | 24-48hr turnaround |
| Contact frequency | Weekly call at most | Weekly call minimum + daily text |
| Initiative | Reactive — waits for creator ask | Proactive — ships suggestions unprompted |
| Compensation | Monthly retainer (flat) | Revenue share (10-30%) |

Each row is a testable operational metric. The agency-partner boundary is measurable in hours, turnaround days, weekly contact counts, and compensation mix. Operators who self-report "we're a partner shop" but measure themselves on the agency side of every row are running an agency — the self-report is downstream of the structure, not upstream.

## Compensation Structure

**Revenue share range:** 10-30% of net revenue. Typical contracts sit at 15-20% for an operator running content + funnel + ops for a $100K-$300K/mo creator.

**Why revenue share, not retainer:** Two reasons. First, alignment — a retainer pays the operator the same whether the creator has a record month or a collapse month, which selects for operator complacency. Revenue share forces the operator to care about revenue as if it were their own, because it partially is. Second, economics — a $10K/mo retainer cannot afford the daily-contact, 24-48hr-turnaround cadence described above; the math only supports that level of embedded work when the operator's upside scales with the creator's growth.

**Below-$100K/mo creators:** The revenue share math starves the operator. A 20% share of $60K/mo is $12K, which does not cover one FTE-level operator's embedded work. Varano does not take these engagements at the partner level; he refers them to agency-style providers or to his educational offer.

**Above-$400K/mo creators:** The revenue share math becomes prohibitive for the creator. A 20% share of $500K/mo is $100K/mo to one operator, which is unsustainable from the creator's P&L. At this tier the structure shifts toward equity, lower-percentage rev-share, or carve-outs to specific revenue lines.

## Cadence Architecture

**Same-day response.** Not "responsive" — same-day. Messages sent in the creator's morning get a response by the creator's evening, reliably. This is the single most measurable signal that the partner model is active.

**24-48hr implementation.** When the creator says "let's try X," X is shipped inside 48 hours. Not "scoped," not "ticketed" — shipped. This cadence is what distinguishes the partner from a vendor.

**Weekly call minimum.** Every week, without fail. 30-60 minutes. Agenda is strategic (where are we going) not tactical (what did you ship). Tactical updates happen asynchronously between calls.

**Daily text.** Not formal — literally, texts and messages across the day. Share wins, flag issues, ask quick questions. The creator should feel they have a partner who is thinking about the business at the same frequency they are.

**Proactive suggestions.** The partner ships ideas the creator did not ask for. Audit of the funnel, observation from last week's call metrics, new angle for next VSSL. A partner who only executes asked-for work is a vendor; a partner who pre-empts the creator's thinking is the real role.

## Roster Discipline

**Cap: 2-3 creators, maximum.** Varano is explicit: 2-3 solid creator partnerships is A LOT. The cap exists because the cadence requirements above cannot be sustained across more relationships. Same-day response across 7 creators is not possible for one operator; it becomes next-day response, which is agency-cadence, which breaks the model.

**The cap is policy, not capacity.** Capacity creeps upward over time — an operator who feels "I could take one more" is how 2-creator shops become 7-creator agencies. The cap is written into existing partner contracts (partners get veto rights over new engagements) to prevent drift.

**Selection criteria** for the 2-3 roster:
1. Creator is doing $100K-$400K/mo (revenue share math works).
2. Creator has a defined offer and voice (Flywheel has something to work with).
3. Creator has emotional capacity for a partner relationship (will actually respond to daily text, accept proactive suggestions, share revenue transparently).
4. Creator's ICP matches an audience surface the operator can run (usually YouTube long-form).
5. Creator is not working with another embedded operator (no split-partnership conflicts).

## The Profit-First Scoreboard

Varano's Profit-First thesis is the operating-metric layer of the partner model. The scoreboard leads with profit, not revenue.

**70%+ gross margin target.** Designed at the offer stage via delivery model and tech-stack decisions, not rescued at scale via layoffs. A creator whose offer is structurally below 70% margin cannot be run on this model — the margin has to exist before the operator embeds.

**Low-overhead operations.** Skool / Kajabi / one CRM / one form / one calendar. Quarterly audit asks "what can we remove?" before "what should we add?" Each tool is a margin leak and a cognitive tax on the operator.

**Delivery model as margin design choice.** 1:1 delivery: highest margin, no scale. Small-group (5-15): margin-preserving at scale. Large cohort: scale, thin margin. The partner selects the delivery model at the offer-blueprint stage to hit the 70% target.

**The $246K/mo profit example.** Varano's public framing of a $246K/mo profit operation as superior to a $1M/mo revenue operation with thin margins — on founder stress, pricing power, retention, and exit value — is the canonical rhetoric for the profit-first scoreboard.

## When The Model Does Not Apply

- **Enterprise B2B SaaS:** The partner-cadence does not match an enterprise sales cycle. Use standard agency/consultant engagements.
- **Early-stage creators (<$50K/mo):** Economics do not support embedded operator cost. Use educational / self-serve offers first.
- **Operators who cannot sustain the cadence:** Same-day response is not a preference — it is the structural requirement. Operators who need async-with-delays work should not import this model.
- **Capital-intensive delivery:** Physical product, in-person events, hardware — the 70% margin target is unrealistic and should be adjusted to the delivery model.

## Transition From Agency to Partner

**The uncomfortable truth: you cannot migrate mid-engagement.** A six-month retainer cannot convert to a revenue share at month seven without re-negotiating the entire relational contract. The model should be set at engagement start. If an existing agency relationship needs to become a partnership, it requires a reset conversation, a new contract, and typically a fresh start date.
