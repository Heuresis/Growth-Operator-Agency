# Full-Stack Sales Call — The 8-Stage Architecture for $2k–$50k Book-a-Call

> **Why this matters:** A $4,800 or $24,800 book-a-call is not a conversation. It is a staged procedure. Each of the eight stages has a goal, a time box, a script spine, an objection set, and a specific failure mode. Skipping a stage does not save time — it surfaces the missing stage as an objection later, usually at close. The default total is 40–50 minutes.

## Source / Lineage

- Alen Sultanic, *Ownership Selling* methodology
- Dustin Varano, high-ticket closing methodology
- Jeremy Miner (NEPQ), Chris Voss (*Never Split the Difference*)
- Distilled into eight stages by the Heuresis authoring line (2026), compatible with Cialdini / Kahneman / 4-belief layer frameworks

## The Eight Stages

| # | Stage | Time | Primary goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Frame-Setting | 2–4 min | Establish expert/client contract, not friend/friend |
| 2 | Discovery-Deep | 10–15 min | Map current state in forensic detail |
| 3 | Problem-Consequence | 5–8 min | Make cost of problem viscerally present |
| 4 | Vision-Construction | 5–7 min | Install a specific, owned future |
| 5 | Solution-Presentation | 5–8 min | Show how the path closes the gap, not a feature tour |
| 6 | Objection-Handling | 5–10 min | Uncover and dissolve real objections |
| 7 | Decision-Close | 2–5 min | Ask for the decision without pressure theater |
| 8 | Next-Steps-Lock | 2–3 min | Convert verbal yes into paid + calendared |

Total: 40–50 minutes.

## Stage 1 — Frame-Setting

**Goal.** Establish the call as a consultation between an expert and a potential client, not a friendly chat. This stage determines who is evaluating whom.

**Time.** 2–4 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "Thanks for making the time. Before we start, let me tell you how I run these calls."
- "For the first 15 minutes, I am going to ask you hard questions — not because I am vetting you emotionally, but because if I do not understand your situation precisely, I cannot tell you whether we can help."
- "At about minute 25, I will tell you whether I think what we do is a fit. If it is, I will walk you through it and tell you what it costs. If it is not, I will tell you what I would do in your position instead."
- "Fair?"

**Failure modes.**
- Opening with "so how are you?" small-talk longer than 45 seconds. Cedes the expert frame.
- Apologetic tone ("hope I am not wasting your time"). Flips the power dynamic.
- Skipping the explicit "fair?" confirmation — loses the micro-commitment that anchors the whole call.

**Objections at this stage.**
- "I only have 30 minutes." -> "Understood. Then we will skip the depth-dive and I will tell you at minute 20 whether it is a fit. Deal?"
- "I just want to hear about your program." -> "I can do that. But if I describe it before I know whether it fits, I will mislead you. Give me ten minutes of questions first."

## Stage 2 — Discovery-Deep

**Goal.** Map current state with forensic specificity. Numbers, workflows, stack, team, last-30-days pattern.

**Time.** 10–15 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "Walk me through your business. Start with revenue last month, then how you got to that number."
- "Who does what on the team? Name by name."
- "What did last Tuesday look like, hour by hour?"
- "What is the biggest thing currently broken?"
- "What have you tried for that? What happened?"

**Installation target.** Layer 1 belief ("this problem is mine") and Layer 2 belief ("it is solvable").

**Failure modes.**
- Yes/no questions. Use open-ended. "Walk me through" beats "do you."
- Accepting vague answers. If they say "things are fine," ask "what does fine mean in MRR? In hours worked last week?"
- Skipping specificity. "Tuesday hour-by-hour" unlocks details a generic "what is broken" never produces.

**Objections at this stage.**
- "Why do you need to know this?" -> "Because if I do not know the shape of the week, I cannot tell you whether we can change it."

## Stage 3 — Problem-Consequence

**Goal.** Make the cost of the problem viscerally present. Convert known pain into felt pain.

**Time.** 5–8 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "If nothing changes in the next 12 months, what does your business look like?"
- "And what does your week look like?"
- "You told me you are losing roughly 22 hours a week to this. That is 1,144 hours a year. At the rate you bill, that is $X. Does that math land?"
- "What is this costing you outside of business? Sleep, relationships, the kind of operator you want to be?"

**Installation target.** Loss aversion. The prospect should feel the cost of inaction as a live loss, not a theoretical gain foregone.

**Failure modes.**
- Over-agitating. Past a point, the prospect shuts down emotionally and the call goes transactional. Three consequence-angles is plenty.
- Leading with dollars when the prospect is identity-motivated. Match consequence to their stated priority.
- Consequences without specificity. "You will burn out" is weaker than "Your partner has mentioned twice in the last month that you are absent even when you are home."

## Stage 4 — Vision-Construction

**Goal.** Install a specific, owned future state that only the solution can produce. Not a fantasy — a specific changed week.

**Time.** 5–7 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "Let us flip it. If this was solved 90 days from now, what would Tuesday look like?"
- "Who would you be as an operator if this was behind you?"
- "What would that be worth — both in the business and outside it?"
- "Okay — so we are clear. The gap between today and that is what I am here to tell you whether we can close."

**Installation target.** The prospect describes the vision in their own words. A vision constructed by them sticks; one pitched by you does not.

**Failure modes.**
- Prescribing the vision. "You will have your mornings free and be down to 30-hour weeks." Wrong — let them say it.
- Settling for vague vision. If they say "more freedom," press: "Freedom to do what, specifically, on what day of the week?"

## Stage 5 — Solution-Presentation

**Goal.** Show how the specific path closes the specific gap you just made visible. This is not a feature tour.

**Time.** 5–8 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "Here is how we would close that gap for you. There are four phases."
- "Phase 1, week one: we audit your calendar and workflow and find the specific hours being lost. Most clients at your stage recover 6–10 hours in week one alone."
- "Phase 2, weeks two and three: we install the calendar topology. This is the piece that stops the bleeding."
- "Phase 3, weeks four through eight: we build your review loop and hire ladder so the hours stay recovered when we leave."
- "Phase 4, weeks nine through twelve: we certify. You run it for four weeks without us. We verify the hours held."
- "Outcome target: 22+ hours recovered per week, held through quarter-end. If you do not get that, you do not pay the final installment."

**Installation target.** Layer 3 belief ("this path is right") via specificity that only someone who has done this 100+ times could describe.

**Failure modes.**
- Feature dumping. "You get access to the portal, weekly group calls, Voxer coaching..." — lists features without the mechanism story.
- Overshooting the vision. If you promise bigger than they asked for, you sound desperate.
- Generic phases. "Discover, plan, execute, review" signals amateur. Name the specific work in the specific weeks.

## Stage 6 — Objection-Handling

**Goal.** Surface the real objection, not the stated one, and dissolve it.

**Time.** 5–10 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "So — before I give you the investment number, what concerns do you have about what I just described?"
- [Listen fully. Do not interrupt.]
- "Okay. Is that the only thing, or if we solved that, is there another?"
- [Continue until they say "no, that is it."]
- Then address, in order of easiest to hardest.

**Common objections and responses.**

| Stated | Usually means | Response |
|---|---|---|
| "I need to think about it" | Layer 4 missing | "What specifically do you need to think through? Let us think through it now." |
| "I need to talk to my partner" | Layer 3 or 4 | "Makes sense. Walk me through what your partner is going to ask. Let us answer those now." |
| "It is expensive" | Layer 3 (fit unclear) or Layer 2 (possibility unclear) | "Expensive compared to what? The alternative path, or the status quo?" |
| "Can we do payments?" | Often a buying signal | "Yes. Let me show you the options." |
| "Can I get back to you in a few months?" | Layer 4 | "What will be different in a few months that is not true today?" |

**Failure modes.**
- Answering the first objection before asking if there are others. Prospect saves the real one for last.
- Arguing. Never argue. Ask, reflect, ask again.
- Over-handling. If the third round of objection-handling has not moved them, it is not an objection — it is a no. Respect the no.

## Stage 7 — Decision-Close

**Goal.** Ask for the decision cleanly. No pressure theater.

**Time.** 2–5 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "Based on everything we have just talked through — does this sound like the right path for you?"
- [Silence. Do not fill.]
- If yes: "Great. Here is how we get you started."
- If unsure: "What is the one thing still in the way?"
- If no: "Appreciate the straight answer. Can I ask what it was?"

**Installation target.** Layer 4 belief ("right time") — but this stage assumes Layers 1–3 installed earlier. If close feels hard here, the work was missed in stages 2–5.

**Failure modes.**
- Trial closes every 30 seconds. Triggers reactance in sophisticated buyers.
- Discounting to close. Once price drops under pressure, the real price is the discounted one — and the discount telegraphs that value was inflated.
- Apologetic ask. "So, um, would you want to maybe get started?" — loses the close through tone alone.

## Stage 8 — Next-Steps-Lock

**Goal.** Convert verbal yes into paid and calendared inside the call. Otherwise, 30–50% of verbal yeses evaporate by next morning.

**Time.** 2–3 minutes.

**Script spine.**
- "Here is what happens in the next ten minutes: I am going to send you the contract and the payment link right now. You sign, pay the first installment, and we book your kickoff call."
- "Kickoff is a 90-minute working session. Calendar options: Tuesday 10am or Thursday 2pm this week — which works?"
- "Great. While I send the contract, can you pull up your calendar?"

**Failure modes.**
- Leaving to "send it over when we hang up." Verbal yeses cool by morning. Lock it on the call.
- Failing to book the kickoff. The calendar commitment is the real lock; the payment is the gate.
- Not preparing the contract / link in advance. Every minute of friction at this stage is conversion risk.

## Diagnostic — Which Stage Did I Miss?

| Outcome | Likely missed stage |
|---|---|
| "Not a fit right now" | Stage 2 (discovery) — you did not know enough to show fit |
| "Need to think about it" | Stage 3 (consequence) or Stage 4 (vision) |
| "How is this different from X" at close | Stage 5 (solution mechanism unclear) |
| "Talk to my partner" | Stage 6 (real objection not surfaced) |
| Verbal yes, no payment | Stage 8 (lock) |
| Paid, then ghosted before kickoff | Stage 1 (frame) or Stage 4 (vision not owned) |

## Non-applicable scenarios

- Sub-$2k offers where 48-minute calls are uneconomic; use async or group close
- Procurement-gated enterprise deals where the call is one of ten stages in a 90-day cycle
- Referral sales where stages 1–4 were installed by the referrer; start at stage 5
