# Market Hierarchy — The 25-Cell awareness-spectrum research x the VSL director Matrix

> **Why this matters:** Awareness (what the prospect knows) and Sophistication (how tired the market is of the category's claims) are independent axes. A product-aware prospect in a stage-1 market needs a completely different opening than a product-aware prospect in a stage-5 market. Miss the cell, miss the sale.

## Source / Lineage

- the awareness-spectrum author, the mid-20th-century market-awareness monograph (1966) — Prospect Awareness (5 levels)
- the awareness-spectrum author / the VSL director / the offer architect teaching line — Market Sophistication (5 stages)
- Matrix fusion formalized by the campaign director, the copy director, Ning Li

## The Two Axes

**Axis A — Prospect Awareness (rows)**
1. Unaware — does not yet know they have the problem
2. Problem-Aware — feels pain, cannot name it
3. Solution-Aware — knows category of solutions exists
4. Product-Aware — knows your product exists, not yet convinced
5. Most-Aware — ready to buy, needs deal/reason-to-act-now

**Axis B — Market Sophistication (columns)**
1. First claim — virgin market, no competition
2. Bigger claim — me-too competitors, direct amplification
3. Mechanism — claims exhausted, introduce how/why
4. Better mechanism — mechanisms exhausted, differentiate the mechanism
5. Identity/Feeling — mechanisms exhausted, sell who they become

## The 25-Cell Matrix

Row = Awareness. Column = Sophistication. Each cell names the dominant message frame.

### Row 1 — Unaware

| Cell | Sophistication | Dominant frame | Example opener |
|------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| 1.1 | First claim | Name the hidden pain | "You are losing three hours a day and you do not know where they go." |
| 1.2 | Bigger | Amplify the size of the hidden loss | "The average founder loses $412,000 a year to invisible context-switching." |
| 1.3 | Mechanism | Story that reveals the invisible cause | "There is a reason your week feels full yet nothing ships. It has a name." |
| 1.4 | Better mechanism | Contrast with the usual hidden-cause stories | "Most people blame discipline. The real cause is a broken calendar topology." |
| 1.5 | Identity | Paint the person they already are but cannot see | "You are already a systems-thinker trapped in an executor's schedule." |

### Row 2 — Problem-Aware

| Cell | Sophistication | Dominant frame | Example opener |
|------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| 2.1 | First claim | Name the problem by its sharp edge | "Stop losing afternoons to Slack." |
| 2.2 | Bigger | Bigger version of the same promise | "Reclaim 22 hours a week — not 2." |
| 2.3 | Mechanism | Introduce the cause | "The reason Slack eats your afternoon is the interruption residue effect." |
| 2.4 | Better mechanism | Contrast mechanisms | "Notification-batching is a bandaid. Interruption-residue needs a calendar topology." |
| 2.5 | Identity | Speak as peer | "For founders who refuse to let messaging apps run their company." |

## Rules of Diagonal Movement

1. You cannot skip rows. A stage-5 market with unaware prospects still needs problem-naming before identity-selling.
2. You can run two cells in one asset: VSL opens in 2.4 (problem + mechanism contrast), closes in 4.4 (offer + mechanism upgrade).
3. The more sophisticated the market, the more content-weight you need in row 3–4 cells. Stage-1 markets can sell from row 2 alone.
4. Identity (column 5) without mechanism (column 3–4) below it produces cult-adjacent copy that sells once and refunds.

## Diagnostic — Which Cell Are You In?

For any traffic source, ask:
- What do they already know when they land?  -> Row
- How many competitors are making the same claim at this price point?  -> Column

| Competitor count at same claim | Stage |
|---|---|
| 0 | 1 |
| 1–3 | 2 |
| 4–10 | 3 |
| 11–30 | 4 |
| 30+ | 5 |
