# Vertical — Health and Wellness Coaching

> **Who this is for:** creators coaching on health and wellness topics beyond pure fitness — hormones, gut health, sleep, energy, metabolic health, autoimmune, functional medicine adjunct, perimenopause, men's hormonal health, stress. Often positioned as functional, holistic, or integrative health coaching.

---

## 1. Audience Primer

Health and wellness buyers are often medically frustrated. They've been to doctors, taken prescriptions, done tests, and haven't resolved their issue. They arrive through search, podcast, or word-of-mouth looking for a root-cause approach. Many are on a personal research arc — they've read books, followed experts, tried protocols.

The defining characteristic: they're willing to invest in themselves once they believe the solution is real. Before that, they're careful with money because they've been burned by supplements, programs, and practitioners that didn't deliver.

Regulatory exposure is highest in this vertical. Every claim must be disclaimered. Health-adjacent coaching is not medical practice and cannot cross that line.

### Sub-niches

1. **Women's hormones / perimenopause** — highest growth sub-niche, 40+ demographic
2. **Gut health / digestive** — cross-demographic, high urgency
3. **Sleep optimization** — cross-demographic
4. **Energy / chronic fatigue / adrenal** — narrow but deep
5. **Metabolic / weight-loss-adjacent** — overlaps with fitness vertical
6. **Men's hormonal health (testosterone, etc.)** — growing
7. **Autoimmune protocols** — narrow, specialized
8. **Stress / nervous system regulation** — growing

### Search + research behavior

- Google (symptom search, specific condition names)
- YouTube (educational long-form)
- Podcasts (high-trust authority-building)
- Instagram (functional-medicine influencer space is dense)
- Specialist forums and communities
- Books by functional / integrative medicine authors

### What they already spend on

- Supplements ($50-$500/mo)
- Lab tests ($100-$1000+ per test)
- Functional medicine doctor visits (often cash-pay, $300-$1000)
- Apps (sleep trackers, HRV, continuous glucose monitors)
- Courses and programs

## 2. Sample ICP (Hormones Sub-Niche — Perimenopausal Women)

**Avatar name:** "The Medically Unseen Woman"

### 2.1 Demographic

- Age: 38-55
- Gender: female
- Geography: US + UK + AUS + CAN
- Income: $80K-$250K household
- Education: college+ typical
- Life-stage: career-established, kids adolescent-to-adult, entering perimenopause

### 2.2 4-Layer Pain

| Layer | Typical pain |

| L1 Functional | Weight gain, poor sleep, brain fog, mood swings, low energy |

### 2.3 Limiting belief pattern

- **Most common:** Hopeless ("medicine has failed me, this is just aging") → Safety transformation

- Solution-aware to Product-aware dominant
- High research-depth within the category

### 2.5 Sophistication stage

- **Skeptical to Exhausted** — they've heard many claims
- Respond to mechanism-based education, suspicious of hype

## 3. Offer Archetype

### 3.1 Typical shape

- 3-6 month group coaching program
- Individual lab-review element (often with practitioner partner)
- Protocol delivery (supplements, nutrition, lifestyle)
- Weekly group calls + Q&A
- Private community
- Sometimes includes licensed practitioner collaboration

### 3.2 Value stack components that resonate

- Personalized protocol (based on symptom inventory, not just generic)
- Lab test recommendation + interpretation guidance
- Supplement protocol (with specific brands and dosing)
- Nutrition framework (often specific to condition)
- Lifestyle protocol (sleep, stress, movement)
- Community / peer support
- Practitioner referral network (MD, ND, NP, RD)
- Weekly group coaching
- Educational library (condition-specific)

### 3.3 Bonuses that work

- Lab interpretation guide
- Supplement brand curation (with affiliate transparency)
- Emergency protocol library (flare-up response)
- Family-integration guide (cooking for household, explaining to partner)
- Direct DM access to creator (tier-dependent)
- Bonus topic modules (gut health, sleep, stress)

### 3.4 Guarantee patterns

- Effort-based: "follow the protocol for 90 days; if no measurable improvement, refund"
- Satisfaction-based: "30-day satisfaction window with full refund"
- Progress-documented: "complete the tracking; if metrics don't improve within X, refund + practitioner referral"

Avoid: outcome guarantees tied to medical measures (regulatory risk), "cure" language (legal risk).

## 6. Content Angles

### 6.1 Content pillars

- **Pillar 1 — Education** (mechanism-based, condition-specific)
- **Pillar 2 — Client transformations** (anonymized or released)
- **Pillar 3 — Myth-busting** (challenging conventional or popular narratives)
- **Pillar 4 — Personal story** (creator's own health journey)

### 6.2 Hook patterns

- "If you have [specific symptom], this is probably why..."
- "Your doctor probably didn't tell you this about [condition]"
- "The three tests your [practitioner type] is missing"
- "I used to have [symptom]. Here's what actually worked."
- "[Common advice] is wrong for [archetype]. Here's why."

### 6.3 Platforms

1. **Podcast (own)** — high-trust, long-form authority
2. **YouTube** — educational long-form dominant
3. **Instagram** — community + quick-hit education
4. **Email newsletter** — warmest buyers
5. **TikTok** — reach, especially for perimenopause topics
6. **Facebook groups** — specific conditions have dense community

### 6.4 What to avoid

- Specific health claims without disclaimers
- "Cure" language
- Before/after photos without medical context
- Criticism of medications by name
- Advice that could be construed as medical diagnosis or treatment

## 7. Proof Patterns

### 7.1 What works

- Symptom-reduction testimonials (archetype-matched, with qualitative detail)
- Lab-value changes (with client release, anonymized)
- Multi-dimensional case stories (symptoms + lifestyle + labs)
- Long-term follow-up stories (6 months, 1 year, 2 years)
- Practitioner endorsements (if creator is not licensed)
- Testimonials noting specifically what changed

### 7.2 What to avoid

- "Miraculous" transformations
- Diagnostic claims
- Photoshopped or edited lab reports
- Unverifiable extreme outcomes
- Claims of curing specific named diseases

## 10. Common Pitfalls + Fixes

| Pitfall | Why it happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Diagnostic language (crossing medical line) | Over-identification with medical framing | Train team on what can/can't be said; use "symptoms" not "diseases"; "support" not "treat" |
| Supplement over-recommendation | Affiliate or creator-product revenue | Transparent affiliate disclosure; recommend minimal effective protocols |
| Vague testimonials ("I feel better") | Not prompting for specifics | Use `testimonial-capture-kit.md` with specific symptom-tracking questions |
| Not working with licensed practitioners | Attempt to avoid regulatory overhead | Partnership or referral network enhances credibility AND reduces risk |
| Generic "wellness" positioning | Broad market default | Specific sub-niche with specific mechanism |
| Over-promising timelines | Sales pressure | Realistic timeline: most protocols need 3-6 months for measurable change |
| Case study gap | No systematic capture | Monthly check-ins with measurable metrics (not just "how do you feel?") |

## 12. Regulatory + Disclaimer Notes

**This vertical has the highest regulatory exposure of any in this workspace.**

### Required disclaimers

- "This information is for educational purposes only and is not intended as medical advice."
- "Consult your physician before starting any protocol."
- "Individual results vary."
- "I am not a licensed medical professional" (if true — be specific about credentials)
- "Statements have not been evaluated by the FDA" (for supplement adjacents)

### Hard rules

- **Do not diagnose.** Cannot identify a specific disease or condition in a prospect or client.
- **Do not treat.** Cannot claim to cure, treat, or prevent disease.
- **Do not prescribe.** Cannot recommend prescription medications or dosages.
- **Do not dispense.** If selling supplements, ensure proper labeling + compliance.
- **Respect medical privacy.** HIPAA doesn't apply to coaches generally, but client trust does.

### Credentialing

- Be explicit about what you are (coach, certified practitioner, etc.)
- Be explicit about what you are NOT (physician, dietitian, therapist)
- If collaborating with licensed professionals, state that clearly

### International considerations

- EU / UK / AUS have stricter rules on health claims (UK ASA; EU nutrition and health claims regulation; AUS TGA)
- Some supplement claims require regulator approval
- Practitioner scope of practice varies by country and state
