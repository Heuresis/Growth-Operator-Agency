# Email Screenshot Testimonial Anatomy

> **Anatomy of a compelling email screenshot.** Deeper than DMs. More formal. Different use cases.

---

## Why Email Screenshots Work

- Feel more considered than DMs (client took longer to write)
- Platform neutral (works across all channels)
- Often longer and more specific than DMs
- Signal a professional relationship, not just a casual one

Downsides vs DMs:
- Harder to crop cleanly (email UI varies)
- Less visual hook
- Can feel staged if over-produced

---

## The 6 Elements Of A Compelling Email Screenshot

```
1. Sender identity (name, email, sometimes company)
2. Subject line (often quotable on its own)
3. Date sent (proves recency)
4. The body content (the testimonial itself)
5. The specific context (what triggered the email)
6. The closing (handwritten-feel sign-off)
```

---

## The Weight Hierarchy

### Tier 1 — Unprompted, Mid-Engagement Or Post-Engagement
Client wrote you an email you didn't ask for.

Structure:
```
From: [Client Name]
Subject: [Specific subject — e.g., "Quick update on what happened this week"]
Date: [Recent]

Hi [You],

Wanted to send this before I get pulled into the next thing.

The [SPECIFIC MOVE] we talked about in our last call — I ran it exactly as you laid out. 
Here's what happened in the following [TIMEFRAME]:

— [SPECIFIC OUTCOME 1]
— [SPECIFIC OUTCOME 2]
— [SPECIFIC OUTCOME 3]

I've been thinking about [FOLLOW-UP QUESTION OR NEXT MOVE]. Let me know what you think.

Also — genuinely appreciate the work. This is the first time in [TIMEFRAME] I feel like 
I'm actually building vs just running.

[Sign-off]
[Client Name]
[Company]
```

Why it lands: unprompted, specific outcomes, genuine acknowledgment, professional context.

### Tier 2 — Response To A Check-In
You sent "how's it going?" — they replied with substance.

Structure:
```
From: [Client Name]
Subject: Re: [Your check-in subject]
Date: [Recent]

Appreciate the check-in. Quick update:

[SPECIFIC PROGRESS]

Where I'm landing after [TIMEFRAME]: [HONEST ASSESSMENT]

One thing I wasn't expecting: [SURPRISE OBSERVATION]

Next 30 days I'm focused on: [FORWARD PLAN]

Thanks for the nudge.

[Sign-off]
[Client Name]
```

Why it lands: honest, forward-looking, specific, includes unexpected outcome.

### Tier 3 — Referral-Trigger Email
Client is referring someone to you and CC's / forwards it.

Structure:
```
From: [Client Name]
Subject: Intro — [Referred Person's Name], meet [You]
Date: [Recent]

[REFERRED PERSON] — wanted to introduce you to [YOU]. 

The short version: I worked with [YOU] for [TIMEFRAME] and it changed [SPECIFIC THING]. 
If you're thinking about [YOUR DOMAIN], you should talk to [YOU].

[YOU] — [REFERRED PERSON] is [SITUATION]. Curious if this rhymes with the work you do.

I'll leave you two to it.

[Client Name]
```

Why it lands: referral is the ultimate testimonial signal. The client is putting their reputation on you.

### Tier 4 — Testimonial Written In Response To Your Ask
You asked; they wrote a considered email.

Structure: similar to written-case-study prompts but email-format.

Why it lands: less "unprompted" but still substantive when they took time.

## Collection Protocol

1. **Don't fabricate** — never write a testimonial "from" a client
2. **Ask for permission before screenshotting** — always
3. **Redact as requested** — offer to blur names, companies, specific numbers
4. **Confirm exact excerpts** — show client what you'll publish before publishing
5. **Store the original** — keep the source email in case the testimonial is challenged

---

## Permission Ask Template

```
"Hey — this email was so clear and specific I'd love to share it.

Before I do, want to check which level of visibility works for you:

Option A — Full screenshot, your name and company visible
Option B — Screenshot with company name blurred, first name only
Option C — Screenshot with all identifying details blurred
Option D — Just pull the key quote (no screenshot)
Option E — Keep it between us, don't share publicly

No pressure — just want to make sure I have your comfort level right."
```

---

## Design / Formatting

When using email screenshots in content:

- Use native email UI screenshot (looks authentic)
- Crop to show sender + subject + body + signature area
- Don't add heavy graphic overlays
- If blurring: blur cleanly, consistent level across all elements being hidden
- Date stamp visible

---

## When NOT To Use Email Screenshots

- Email contains sensitive business info about third parties
- Client is mid-engagement and a change in engagement status is imminent
- Email was clearly private in tone (reader will sense it)
- You don't have written permission
- The email was from 2+ years ago (reads stale)

---

## Combination Use

Email screenshots work best:
- On sales page "social proof" sections (alongside video testimonials)
- In email marketing (screenshot within an email feels meta-credible)
- In case-study posts as the "what the client said" evidence
- On landing pages as rotating social proof

They work poorly:
- As hero social proof (video or DM hit harder for scroll-stop)
- Stacked without breathing room (reads as noisy)

---

## Storage Protocol

Keep a private folder with:
- Original email (PDF or raw)
- Client's written permission
- List of where you've used it
- Any redaction levels applied

This folder protects you if the testimonial is later questioned or withdrawn.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Using a screenshot without permission
- Blurring inconsistently (name visible but company blurred, etc.)
- Over-production (adds graphics that make it look staged)
- Using stale emails
- Fabricating (zero tolerance)
- Missing redaction of third-party names in the email body
