# Revenue Dashboard Testimonial Anatomy

> **Dashboard and screenshot testimonials.** Numbers as social proof. Hardest to fake; highest credibility when real.

---

## Why Dashboard Screenshots Work

- Numbers read as objective
- Platform chrome (Stripe, PayPal, bank dashboard, analytics tool) adds authenticity
- Dated interfaces prove timeline
- Hardest category to fabricate convincingly

Downsides:
- Easy to fake (and readers know this)
- Requires client willingness to share sensitive data
- Need context to be meaningful

---

## The 7 Elements Of A Credible Dashboard Screenshot

```
1. Platform identity (Stripe, bank, analytics tool — visible chrome)
2. The metric itself (clearly labeled)
3. The timeframe (dates visible)
4. The account / identity (client's name or clearly theirs)
5. The context (what drove the numbers)
6. The comparison (before/after or vs. previous period)
7. The caveat (what this does / doesn't prove)
```

Missing any of these weakens the screenshot.

---

## Acceptable Platforms To Screenshot

### High Credibility
- Stripe dashboard (payment processor)
- PayPal dashboard
- Bank transaction history (sensitive — usually anonymized)
- Accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero, etc.)
- CRM revenue reports

### Medium Credibility
- Analytics tools (Google Analytics, platform-native analytics)
- Marketing dashboards (email platform revenue attribution)
- Course platform dashboards
- E-commerce platform dashboards

### Lower Credibility
- Spreadsheets (easily faked)
- Screenshots of internal slides (one remove from source)
- Third-party aggregation dashboards

The higher the platform credibility, the less additional context is needed.

## Anonymization Protocols

Most clients won't let you share a raw dashboard. Here's how to anonymize while preserving credibility:

### Level 1 — Minimal anonymization
- Blur account name / email
- Keep all numbers, dates, platform chrome
- Result: high credibility, client protected

### Level 2 — Moderate
- Blur account name, specific customer names
- Keep revenue totals, dates, platform chrome
- Result: good credibility, more protection

### Level 3 — Heavy
- Blur all identifying info
- Show numerical data only
- Add your own text annotation for context
- Result: moderate credibility — relies on your word for authenticity

### Level 4 — Derivative
- Make your own chart from the data
- No platform chrome
- Result: lowest credibility — reads as a claim, not proof

Push for Level 1 or 2 when possible.

## Example Structures

### Structure A — Stripe Dashboard (Monthly Revenue Growth)
```
[SCREENSHOT]: Stripe dashboard, monthly revenue view, covering [MONTH A] through [MONTH B].

[CAPTION]:
Client: [ANONYMIZED ICP TYPE]
Timeframe: [SPECIFIC MONTHS]
Starting MRR: $[AMOUNT]
Ending MRR: $[AMOUNT]
Delta: [X]×

What drove the change: [SPECIFIC INTERVENTION]. Not additional ad spend, not new product launch, not team expansion.

Caveat: this client had clear product-market fit. Results would not replicate for pre-PMF businesses.
```

### Structure B — Bank Transaction Summary
```
[SCREENSHOT]: Bank account activity, client-provided, anonymized with account holder blurred.

[CAPTION]:
[DATE]: $[AMOUNT] received from [ANONYMIZED SOURCE]
[DATE]: $[AMOUNT] received from [ANONYMIZED SOURCE]

All from the same [SPECIFIC CAMPAIGN / ENGAGEMENT] that ran [TIMEFRAME].

Total campaign revenue: $[AMOUNT]
Campaign cost: $[AMOUNT]
Net: $[AMOUNT]
ROI: [X]×

The campaign was built using [SPECIFIC METHOD]. Not the only way to do this; the specific way we did it for this client.
```

### Structure C — Analytics Before/After
```
[SCREENSHOT]: Google Analytics traffic comparison, Month A vs Month B.

[CAPTION]:
Month A (pre-intervention): [NUMBER] sessions, [%] conversion, $[AMOUNT] attributed revenue
Month B (90 days post-intervention): [NUMBER] sessions, [%] conversion, $[AMOUNT] attributed revenue

Delta: [X] more sessions, [Y]% higher conversion, $[Z] more attributed revenue.

Specific change: [WHAT WE DID]

This holds for sites with [SPECIFIC CHARACTERISTIC]. Different starting points produce different deltas.
```

## Context Paragraph Template

Every dashboard screenshot needs context. Template:

```
Client: [ICP TYPE, anonymized to appropriate level]
Engagement: [TIMEFRAME] of [TYPE OF WORK]
Starting state: [DATED METRIC]
Ending state: [DATED METRIC]
Delta: [MATH]
What drove it: [SPECIFIC INTERVENTION]
What this doesn't prove: [HONEST CAVEAT]
```

The caveat is what separates credible proof from hype. Include it.

---

## FTC Compliance

Per FTC guidelines:
- Results must be typical or include a "results not typical" disclaimer
- Specific dollar figures need context
- If the client was compensated for allowing the screenshot, disclose
- Avoid "anyone can do this" framing

Safe disclaimer template:
```
"Client results are not guarantees. This client [SPECIFIC PROFILE CONDITIONS]. 
Your results depend on [SPECIFIC VARIABLES]."
```

## Storage Protocol

Keep:
- Original unredacted screenshot (private file)
- Client's written permission specifying redaction level
- Your derivation notes (what you computed vs what came from source)
- Date of permission grant + expiration if applicable

---

## When To Use Dashboard Testimonials

Best for:
- Sales pages / landing pages (hero social proof band)
- Long-form case studies (proof block)
- Ad creative (high-credibility social proof)
- DR-heavy offers where numbers are the sell

Worse for:
- Humanization content (dashboards are cold)
- Early-stage relationship building (feels flex-y)
- Offers where emotional transformation is primary

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Using screenshots without permission
- Fabricating or editing numbers (zero tolerance violation)
- Missing platform chrome (looks like a spreadsheet, not a dashboard)
- No date range (reader can't assess timeframe)
- No context paragraph (reader sees numbers, can't assess replicability)
- No caveat (reads as guaranteed)
- Over-anonymizing to the point of no credibility
