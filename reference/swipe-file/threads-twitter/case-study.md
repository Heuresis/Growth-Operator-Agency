# Case Study Thread — X/Twitter

> **Hook → story → mechanism → result.** 8-14 tweets. Proof-forward. Works best when the case has clean before/after math.

---

## The Skeleton

```
Tweet 1 — HOOK
Specific outcome. Numbers. Timeframe.
"[ICP TYPE] went from [X] to [Y] in [N days/weeks]. Here's how:"

Tweets 2-3 — CONTEXT
Who, where they started, what wasn't working.

Tweets 4-7 — THE JOURNEY
What changed. Step by step. What was hard.

Tweets 8-10 — THE MECHANISM
Why it worked. Transferable principle.

Tweets 11-12 — RESULT
Dated numbers. Delta. Current state.

Final tweet — CTA
Follow / DM / Book call.
```

Target: 8-14 tweets. Case study threads tolerate more length because the story has inherent pull.

## Worked Example 1 — Pricing Model Shift Case Study

```
[Tweet 1 — HOOK]
A [ICP TYPE: B2B consultant] went from $[X]/month to $[3X]/month in 11 weeks.

One variable changed. Here's the variable, the full sequence, and what it means for anyone stuck at a plateau.

🧵

[Tweet 2]
Starting state (before Week 0):

— Offer: hourly consulting, $[X]/hr
— Engagements: 1-off, project-based
— Pipeline: referrals only
— Revenue: $[AMOUNT]/mo, high variance
— Client feedback: "good work, but expensive per hour"

[Tweet 3]
The plateau wasn't lack of demand.

Referrals kept coming. Calls got booked. Some closed, most didn't.

The constraint was structural. Hourly billing caps revenue at (hours × rate). And neither variable was moving.

[Tweet 4]
Week 1-2: rewrote the sales page.

Replaced "hourly consulting" with a 90-day outcome-guaranteed engagement.

Same underlying skill. Different container. Different price ($[3-5X] flat).

[Tweet 5]
Week 3-4: ran the new offer past 3 warm leads.

All 3 engaged. 2 closed at the new price.

The surprise: the new offer was easier to sell than the old one. The outcome was specific. The timeline was specific. The risk was transferred to the consultant.

[Tweet 6]
Week 5-8: rebuilt delivery to hit the 90-day outcome.

New structure:
— Weekly group of 1:1 calls (90 min)
— Async Slack access
— Defined milestones at day 15, 30, 60, 90
— Guarantee: specific outcome by day 90 or we keep going free until it hits

[Tweet 7]
Week 9-11: ran the same outreach sequence that had been running at the old price.

Close rate: 3× prior.
Deal size: ~4× prior.
Pipeline velocity: faster (specific offer attracts decisive buyers).

[Tweet 8]
End state:

— Offer: 90-day outcome, $[3-5X] flat
— Clients: same quality, different contract
— Revenue: 3.1× prior month
— Hours worked: roughly similar

Same market. Same skill. Different container.

[Tweet 9]
The mechanism:

Hourly billing sells time (commodity). Outcome billing sells risk transfer (premium).

Buyers pay exponentially more for risk transfer because that's the whole reason they hire a consultant.

Charge for the outcome, not the hours.

[Tweet 10]
The transferable move:

1. Identify the outcome the client actually wants (not the deliverable they ask for).
2. Offer that outcome with a timeline + guarantee.
3. Price 3-5× the equivalent hourly math.
4. Restructure delivery to be responsible for the outcome.

[Tweet 11 — CTA]
I write through this kind of restructure weekly — follow for more.

If you're hourly-billed and want to map the shift, DM me "restructure" and I'll send the worksheet.
```

Tweet count: 11. Type: Case Study. Cadence: monthly feature.

## Worked Example 2 — Retention-Fix Case Study

```
[Tweet 1 — HOOK]
[ICP: SaaS founder] was stuck at $[MRR] for 5 months despite tripling ad spend.

We stopped acquisition for 30 days. 90 days later: 3.4× MRR growth.

Here's the diagnostic + fix:

🧵

[Tweet 2]
Starting state (month 0):

— MRR: $[AMOUNT]
— Ad spend: $[AMOUNT]/month (tripled from prior quarter)
— 90-day retention: 52%
— Founder's theory: "more leads will fix this"

[Tweet 3]
The founder was wrong.

90-day retention at 52% means: for every 100 new users, 48 leave within 90 days.

More leads into a leaking bucket = a faster pump on a hole-in-the-bucket.

[Tweet 4]
We stopped all paid acquisition for 30 days. 

Terrifying for the founder. Logical for the business.

What we did with the 30 days:

[Tweet 5]
Built onboarding checkpoints at day 1, 3, 7, 14, 30.

Each checkpoint: a specific milestone the user had to hit.

If they missed it, a human (founder or first team member) reached out directly.

[Tweet 6]
Defined an activation metric: [SPECIFIC PRODUCT USE that correlates with retention].

Anyone who hit the metric by day 7 retained at 85%+.
Anyone who didn't retained at 30%.

The metric was invisible before we defined it.

[Tweet 7]
Added a founder touchpoint at day 7.

Personal email. 2-3 sentences. Asked about the specific use case they'd signed up for.

~40% replied. Those replies became the core retention feedback loop.

[Tweet 8]
After 30 days of no acquisition:

— 90-day retention: 78% (up from 52%)
— Activation metric hit rate: 68% (up from 31%)
— Founder touchpoint reply rate: 42%

[Tweet 9]
Turned ads back on with same budget as the stalled months.

New cohorts hit the fixed onboarding. Activation metric triggered early. Retention held.

120 days later: MRR 3.4× where we started.

[Tweet 10]
The mechanism:

Retention isn't an acquisition problem. It's an activation problem.

Users who activate, retain. Users who don't, churn. More leads without activation = faster churn, not growth.

[Tweet 11]
The transferable sequence:

1. Stop scaling acquisition until activation is fixed
2. Define one activation metric (specific product use)
3. Build checkpoints at days 1, 3, 7, 14, 30
4. Human touchpoint at the highest-churn day
5. Resume acquisition after retention hits 70%+

[Tweet 12 — CTA]
If you're leaking users and tempted to pour more acquisition on it — DM me "retention" and I'll send the checkpoint template.
```

Tweet count: 12. Type: Case Study.

## Worked Example 3 — Content Strategy Shift Case Study

```
[Tweet 1 — HOOK]
[ICP: solo creator] was posting 7×/week for 14 months. Followers growing slowly. Revenue flat.

Cut to 3 posts/week. 90 days later: 2× followers, 4× revenue.

The math behind why fewer posts worked:

🧵

[Tweet 2]
Starting state:

— Post cadence: daily (7/week)
— Followers: [NUMBER]
— Engagement/post: plateauing
— DMs into the offer: [LOW NUMBER]
— Revenue: flat for 6 months

[Tweet 3]
Daily posting felt productive.

Each post took 30-45 minutes. Across a week: 4-5 hours on content.

Quality per post: medium. Hook specificity varied. Saves per post: plateauing.

[Tweet 4]
We cut to 3 posts/week.

Reallocated the saved hours to quality:
— 90 minutes per post (vs 35 prior)
— Real research per hook
— Specific case studies in every post
— Revised drafts 24 hours later

[Tweet 5]
First month at 3/week:

Impressions per post: 2.1× prior.
Saves per post: 2.4× prior.
Long comments: 2.8× prior.

Total reach across the month: up, despite 16 fewer posts.

[Tweet 6]
Month 2-3:

Followers doubled.

DMs into the offer 4× prior.

Revenue (closed deals): 4.1× prior.

Same account. Same audience. Different content density.

[Tweet 7]
The mechanism:

Per the Dec 2025 Knowledge Graph update, LinkedIn discounts single-creator high-frequency posting to prevent topic-graph saturation.

7 posts/week get dampened individually. 3 posts/week don't.

[Tweet 8]
Also: attention scarcity.

7 mediocre posts ≠ 3 strong posts × 2.

Quality compounds. Consistency of quality matters more than consistency of posting.

[Tweet 9]
The transferable sequence:

1. Cut to 3-4 posts/week
2. Triple time-per-post
3. Measure saves + long comments (not likes)
4. Reply to own post 60-90 min later
5. Repeat for 60 days before assessing

[Tweet 10 — CTA]
Want the 90-minute post production process I use? DM me "process" and I'll send the template.
```

## Slot-Fill Rules

### Hook tweet
- Before/after numbers in the hook
- Specific (not rounded)
- Timeframe
- Promises the breakdown

### Context tweets (2-3)
- Starting state with numbers
- Named constraint (what was actually blocking growth)
- No hero-worship — the case study isn't about the creator, it's about the pattern

### Journey tweets (4-7)
- Sequential
- What changed + why + what was hard
- Specific moves, not vague decisions

### Mechanism tweets (1-2)
- Why this worked in structural terms
- Transferable principle (applies beyond this case)

### Result tweets (1-2)
- Dated numbers
- Delta math (before vs after)
- Honest caveats

### CTA tweet
- Follow for more
- OR DM for the resource tied to the mechanism

---

## Proof Integrity

Every case study thread must be backed by:
1. Real client or self-case (never fabricated)
2. Dated timeline
3. Actual numbers (not "increased significantly")
4. Permission if named client
5. Archetype framing if unnamed ("a consultant I worked with" is safe; specific name needs explicit permission)

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Hook is vague ("big growth story below")
- Context missing (reader can't tell where the case started)
- No mechanism (just a before/after with no transferable principle)
- Thread reads as self-congratulation, not teaching
- Made-up numbers (zero tolerance)
