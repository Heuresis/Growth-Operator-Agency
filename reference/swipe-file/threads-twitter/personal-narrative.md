# Personal Narrative / Story Thread — X/Twitter

> **3-A structure: Arrival / Adversity / Aftermath.** 10-15 tweets. Narrative pull. Humanization + authority.

---

## The Skeleton

```
Tweet 1 — HOOK
Open in medias res or with a precipitating event.
"N years ago I [SPECIFIC MOMENT]. Today I [SPECIFIC CONTRAST].
Here's how:"

Tweets 2-4 — ARRIVAL
Who you were, where you were, what the starting state was.

Tweets 5-9 — ADVERSITY
The hard middle. What broke. What you tried. What failed.

Tweets 10-12 — TURN + AFTERMATH
The pivot. What changed. How it turned.

Tweets 13-14 — LESSON
The transferable principle.

Final tweet — CTA
Follow / DM.
```

Target: 10-15 tweets. Story threads tolerate length when the narrative has real tension.

## Worked Example 1 — The Rebuild Story

```
[Tweet 1 — HOOK]
3 years ago I shut down a business I'd built for 6 years. Not slowly. Overnight.

Today I run [CURRENT BUSINESS] with [METRIC]. Different category. Different shape. Different outcome.

Here's the 3 years between the shutdown and today:

🧵

[Tweet 2]
The business I closed: [CATEGORY]. $[REVENUE]/year at peak. [N] team members.

On paper, successful.

In practice, I hated it. 80% of my time was spent on work I wasn't good at and didn't care about. Money was arriving. I was losing.

[Tweet 3]
The decision to close came on a [SPECIFIC DAY].

I was [SPECIFIC MUNDANE ACTIVITY]. Something snapped.

I spent the next weekend running the math: close it, pay out severance, walk away with ~[X months of runway].

Monday I told the team. Friday we closed operations.

[Tweet 4]
Month 0 after the shutdown.

Bank balance: [AMOUNT]. No income. No plan. 

What I had: 6 years of skill in the domain I was leaving. Zero validation of whether that skill transferred to anything I'd actually want to do.

[Tweet 5]
Month 1-3: tried three adjacent things.

Each one failed for a different reason.
— Thing 1: market existed, I didn't want to serve it
— Thing 2: I wanted to serve it, market didn't exist yet
— Thing 3: both existed, I wasn't differentiated enough

Burned $[N] of savings. No revenue.

[Tweet 6]
Month 4: I almost went back.

Got a $[AMOUNT] contracting offer from a company in the old category. One-year contract. Safety.

I sat with it for 2 weeks. Said no.

Still don't know if that was the brave decision or the stupid one.

[Tweet 7]
Month 5: started writing publicly.

Not strategically. Just the stuff I wished I'd known during the shutdown. Specific, long, uncommercial.

First post: 11 likes. Third post: went viral. Not in a "that's going to change your life" way. In a "suddenly 5 DMs a day" way.

[Tweet 8]
Month 6-8: those DMs turned into conversations.

~40% of the conversations surfaced the same problem I'd been writing about. People had it. Badly. And were paying.

I priced a small engagement at $[AMOUNT]. Closed 3 in a week.

That was the pivot.

[Tweet 9]
Month 9-12: built the core of the current business.

Same skill set as the old business, different container, different ICP, different economics.

Revenue by month 12: [NUMBER].
Revenue by month 24: [3-5× NUMBER].
Revenue today (month 36): [CURRENT METRIC].

[Tweet 10]
The turn wasn't an idea.

It was the decision to stop trying to re-create the thing I'd shut down.

The old business had trained me in skills I kept trying to use the same way. The new business required using those skills differently.

Took 8 months to see it.

[Tweet 11]
The aftermath — honest version:

I'm making more than the old business at peak. Working fewer hours. Enjoying more of the work.

I'm also less "secure" than I was 3 years ago, because this version depends on me staying useful publicly. That's fine with me. It wasn't a given.

[Tweet 12]
The transferable lesson:

When you shut down something that was working, the instinct is to re-create it in new clothes. Resist.

The skills transfer. The shape doesn't have to. Give the new thing 12 months to tell you what it actually wants to be.

[Tweet 13]
The second lesson:

Public writing was the unlock I didn't plan. 

I'd been told "build in public" for years and ignored it. Started writing only because nothing else was working. It turned out to be the infrastructure the whole business ran on.

Don't ignore the advice twice.

[Tweet 14 — CTA]
Follow for more — I write through the operating work weekly.

If you're in a shutdown + rebuild phase and want to talk through the structural version of your pivot, DM.
```

## Worked Example 2 — The Small Moment Story

```
[Tweet 1 — HOOK]
A stranger said one sentence to me on a flight in [YEAR].

I've built every decision in my business since around that sentence.

Here's the sentence and the 11 tweets of context:

🧵

[Tweet 2]
The flight: [ORIGIN] to [DESTINATION]. [N] hours.

I was [N] years old, running [BUSINESS] that was [STATE — stalled, leaking, growing without purpose].

I hadn't asked for advice. I wasn't looking for it. 

[Tweet 3]
The stranger was in the seat next to me. 

We'd barely spoken for the first [N] hours. Mid-flight, they asked what I did. I gave the elevator pitch I'd been giving for two years.

They listened. Then said:

[Tweet 4]
"You're describing a job you invented for yourself. 

What's the actual business?"

They said it without any edge. Just the observation.

I had no answer.

[Tweet 5]
For the next 3 hours I sat with it.

I'd been running what looked like a business and was actually a high-paying job I'd invented. The distinction hadn't been visible to me until someone said it out loud.

Job: trades time for money. Caps when time caps.
Business: owns an asset that produces without my time.

[Tweet 6]
Month 0 after the flight: audited everything.

Hours I spent on "business work" that was actually "doing the service" (job): 90%.
Hours I spent on "business work" that was actually building an asset (business): 10%.

The split was almost perfectly inverted from where it should have been.

[Tweet 7]
Month 1-3: restructured.

Slowly, painfully.

Shifted one service element per month into a productized version someone else could deliver. Fired myself from three tasks. Hired one person.

Revenue dipped. I expected that.

[Tweet 8]
Month 4-8: the dip reversed.

The productized elements scaled without my time. 

Revenue climbed past prior levels by month 6. By month 9, it was 2.4× prior and I was working 60% of the hours.

[Tweet 9]
Month 12-18: rebuilt the offering around what the business had become, not what I'd invented it as.

New positioning. New target. New pricing.

The job version of the work: $[AMOUNT]/year cap.
The business version: [3-5×] with structural room to grow.

[Tweet 10]
The lesson took years to operationalize. The sentence took 3 hours.

Most of the strategic unlocks in my career have been someone else saying out loud something I already half-knew.

You can't force the unlock. You can stay open to the stranger.

[Tweet 11]
The transferable move:

Ask yourself: "Am I describing a job I invented, or a business?"

Be honest. The answer is usually the first. Fixing that is usually the highest-leverage work available.

[Tweet 12]
The second move: who in your life sees your work clearly enough to ask the hard question?

Find them. Ask them. Pay if you have to.

Structured, specific outside feedback is one of the cheapest sources of strategic clarity.

[Tweet 13 — CTA]
I write through this kind of structural work weekly. Follow if you're in a "job vs business" diagnosis phase.

DM "diagnosis" and I'll send the audit worksheet I built after the flight.
```

Tweet count: 13. Type: Personal Narrative.

## Slot-Fill Rules

### Hook tweet
- Specific year or timeframe
- Clean contrast (where you were vs where you are)
- Promises the narrative

### Arrival tweets (2-4)
- Sensory detail
- Specific numbers (bank balance, team size, revenue)
- No dramatization — straight recounting

### Adversity tweets (4-5)
- What broke
- What you tried that failed
- Specific duration
- Honest (include the almost-went-back moments)

### Turn + Aftermath tweets (2-3)
- The pivot
- Why it worked
- Dated metrics

### Lesson tweets (1-2)
- Transferable principle
- Named

### CTA tweet
- Soft — the narrative did the emotional work
- Follow for more / DM for the resource

---

## Story Integrity

Per `INVARIANTS.md`:
- True event (real, not composite)
- Recent enough (or said explicitly if 10+ years old)
- First-person only
- Transferable lesson

---

## Pacing

Story threads die from pacing errors:
- Too much setup (readers drop before the turn)
- Rushed turn (reader doesn't feel the weight)
- No aftermath (story ends without payoff)

Balance: 25% arrival, 40% adversity, 20% turn, 15% aftermath + lesson.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Story is fake or composited
- Too much sanitization (readers smell it)
- Lesson disconnected from story
- Final CTA too hard (conversion ask after emotional narrative)
- Thread runs past 16 tweets (steep drop-off)
