# Personal-to-Business Structure — LinkedIn

> **Personal moment → Business lesson.** Humanizes the creator. Works when the personal story rhymes with a transferable principle.

---

## The Structure

```
[PERSONAL MOMENT]
Specific, sensory, small. 3-5 sentences. 
Tell the scene; don't narrate the lesson yet.

[THE BRIDGE]
One or two sentences that pivot from personal to business.
Earned, not forced — the business lesson should feel inevitable from the moment.

[THE BUSINESS LESSON]
The principle or mechanism the moment teaches.
Applied to the ICP's situation with specificity.

[APPLICATION]
One to three concrete moves the reader should take.

[CTA]
Follow / DM / comment — often softer than other structures.
```

### When To Use
- Authority pillar with humanization
- The creator has an actual personal story that teaches something business-useful
- Post is in the middle-to-late week (readers already warmed up)
- The creator's ICP wants to see the human behind the operator

## Worked Example 1 — Parenting-to-Pricing

```
[PERSONAL MOMENT]
My [child, N years old] asked for a specific [small-ticket item] at the store yesterday.

I said no — not because of the price. Because she hadn't finished [previous item] from the last trip.

She pushed back: "but I WANT it." I said: "I know. You also wanted [previous item]. And you didn't use it."

She thought about it. Put the thing down. Walked away from the shelf.

[BRIDGE]
I sat in the car afterward and realized the conversation I'd just had with my kid is the conversation I should be having with half my clients.

[BUSINESS LESSON]
The pattern: wanting is cheap. Using is expensive.

Most [ICP: consultants, coaches] build offers that clients want but don't use. The want gets them through the sales page. The "don't use" gets them to churn at 90 days.

The offer that retains isn't the one clients want most. It's the one they use most.

That changes what goes in the offer. Less "here's every possible thing," more "here's the 3 things you'll actually use weekly."

[APPLICATION]
Three moves this week:
1. Audit your current offer's deliverables. Which ones do clients actually use weekly? 
2. Remove anything clients never use (it's brand fat, not brand value).
3. Double down on the things clients use weekly — make them deeper, more personalized.

The retention gain from this shift usually beats any acquisition gain you'd get in the same month.

[CTA]
I'm building a framework for "use-density" offers. If that phrase lands, follow — the next post is the framework.
```

Character count: ~1580. Post type: Personal-to-Business.

## Worked Example 2 — Workout-to-Work

```
[PERSONAL MOMENT]
Hit a plateau at [specific weight] on [specific lift] about 8 months ago. Tried everything.

More volume. Less volume. Different reps. Better sleep. More protein. Less caffeine. More caffeine.

Nothing moved. Added 0 lbs to the lift in 8 months.

Changed one thing last month: dropped the lift from my program entirely for 3 weeks. Replaced it with [adjacent movement].

Came back to the original lift last week. Added [N] lbs on the first session. More this week.

[BRIDGE]
That plateau break on the lift is the same pattern I see in business when someone's stuck on a single growth lever.

[BUSINESS LESSON]
Adaptation has a ceiling. Do the same thing for long enough — even correctly — and the returns compress.

[ICP] get stuck on [common growth lever — outreach, ads, content] because they haven't stepped away from it long enough to let the adaptation reset.

The move isn't "try harder." It's "run an adjacent stimulus for 3-6 weeks, then return." The return performs better than continuing would have.

In business terms:
— If you've been doing cold outreach for 6 months with flat results, pause it. Run warm referral activation for a cycle. Return to cold outreach. The numbers will likely beat the prior plateau.
— If you've been running the same ad creative for 3 months at flat CAC, pause. Run organic content for 6 weeks. Re-launch the ad creative. Same pattern.

Adaptation needs de-loading.

[APPLICATION]
1. Identify the growth lever you've been on for longest.
2. Identify an adjacent lever (different channel, different audience, different format).
3. Pause the first for 3-6 weeks. Run the adjacent lever.
4. Return. Measure against prior plateau.

[CTA]
Where are you most plateaued right now? Comment the lever and I'll reply with the adjacent one I'd test.
```

Character count: ~1650. Post type: Personal-to-Business.

## Worked Example 3 — Friendship-to-Sales

```
[PERSONAL MOMENT]
Had dinner with a friend I hadn't seen in 2 years last week.

Ten minutes in I caught myself doing something weird: I was building an argument for why my life had gone well since we'd last seen each other. Scripting it in my head while he was still talking.

I stopped, listened, and for the rest of the dinner just asked him questions.

When we walked out he said "that was one of the best conversations I've had in months." I hadn't said much.

[BRIDGE]
I've been doing that same thing on sales calls for 6 years without realizing it.

[BUSINESS LESSON]
The sales conversation most [ICP] run is a performance. You're building a case for yourself in real time, filling silence with credentials, competing with an imagined better option the prospect might pick instead.

It reads as desperate, even when you're good.

The sales conversation that converts is the one where the buyer does 70% of the talking. You ask, they surface the problem, you ask deeper, they articulate the outcome they want, you reflect it back sharpened.

By the end, they've sold themselves. Your job was to hold space, listen, and direct the conversation with specific questions.

The conversion rate on a call where I talk 30% beats a call where I talk 70% by [X]× in my data.

[APPLICATION]
Three moves for your next sales call:
1. Prepare 5 specific open-ended diagnostic questions before the call.
2. Track your own talk-time (silently — use a timer).
3. Aim for under 35%.

You'll feel like you didn't do enough. The close rate tells a different story.

[CTA]
If you want the 5-question diagnostic I use at the top of every call, comment "diagnostic" and I'll DM it.
```

Character count: ~1700. Post type: Personal-to-Business + Lead Magnet.

## Slot-Fill Rules

### The Personal Moment slot
- Must be an actual moment, not a composite
- Sensory detail (what you saw, heard, felt)
- Small in scale (one scene, not a montage)
- No lesson embedded yet — just the scene

### The Bridge slot
- One or two sentences
- "I realized" is OK; "let me share a lesson" is not
- Earned — the reader should feel the pivot, not be lectured into it

### The Business Lesson slot
- Mechanism-explained
- Applied to ICP's specific situation
- Includes at least one structural claim (not just feeling)

### The Application slot
- 2-4 concrete moves
- Imperative mood
- This week / this month scope

### The CTA slot
- Softer than conversion posts
- Follow / DM / comment
- Not stacked

---

## Authenticity Test

If the personal moment reads as fabricated or composited, the whole post fails. Readers smell manufactured personal stories.

Tests:
- Can you name a specific day / time / place?
- Is there a sensory detail that proves you were there?
- Does the moment stand on its own even without the business lesson?

If any answer is no, the moment isn't ready to publish. Either wait for a real moment or choose a different post structure.

---

## First Comment Plan

- 60-90 min post-publish
- Share one more sensory detail or related moment
- OR ask for readers' versions of the same pattern
- Reply genuinely to personal stories shared in comments

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Personal moment is generic ("I was at a coffee shop one day")
- Bridge is forced ("this reminded me of a business principle")
- Business lesson is disconnected from the moment
- Post becomes all business, personal moment was decorative
- Uses real names without permission
