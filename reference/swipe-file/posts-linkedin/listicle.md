# Listicle Structure — LinkedIn

> **Hook → N items → Save-trigger close.** Save-optimized. Teaching frame. Highest bookmark rate of any LinkedIn format when executed well.

---

## The Structure

```
[HOOK]
Number-forward: "N [things] that..." or "N lessons from..."
First 1-2 lines, ≤140 chars pre-cutoff.

[FRAMING LINE]
1-2 sentences that establish credibility + prime the list.

[ITEMS]
N items (3-10 range). Each item = 2-5 lines.
Number or bullet formatting. Bold the item lead if possible.

[SAVE-TRIGGER CLOSE]
"Save this for [specific moment]." 
Or: "Come back to this when [specific situation]."

[CTA]
One action: follow / DM / comment.
```

### When To Use
- Save-optimization is the goal
- You have 5-10 discrete items that cohere under one theme
- Authority pillar
- Teaching / How-To / Framework / Checklist content

### Item Count Calibration
- 3 items: focused, high-impact (best for stronger individual items)
- 5 items: sweet spot for most listicles
- 7 items: maxes save rate (7 is the cognitive sweet spot for "comprehensive but not overwhelming")
- 10+ items: drop-off in engagement after ~#7

## Worked Example 1 — Mistake-Audit Listicle

```
[HOOK]
7 mistakes I made in my first year [as ICP type — solo consultant, coach, founder] that cost me ~$[FIGURE].
The first one is the one I'd undo first if I could.

[FRAMING]
Everyone warns you about the obvious mistakes. These are the mistakes nobody warned me about — because they look like good decisions until they compound.

[ITEMS]
**1. Saying yes to the wrong clients.**
I took anyone willing to pay because revenue was the only metric I looked at. 
6 months of grind-work for clients I should have turned away. 
The right filter: can I produce the outcome they want in the timeframe they expect?

**2. Pricing against my discomfort, not the market.**
I priced where I was comfortable asking, not where the market paid. 
Left ~$[AMOUNT] on the table in 12 months.
The right frame: ask the price that makes the offer economically sane for you at scale, not comfortable for you on call #1.

**3. Measuring hours worked, not revenue per hour.**
I thought "working hard" was the input. Revenue per hour is the metric that actually matters.
I worked [N] hours/week for [N] months before I computed my hourly. It was $[LOW].

**4. Building the offer before the audience.**
I designed my offer in isolation and then looked for who wanted it. 
Should have reversed: find the audience, talk to them for 6 weeks, then design.

**5. Not firing a single underperformer in year 1.**
Every drag on the team or the client roster I kept "just to be kind" cost 3× that kindness in operational tax.
The kindness was to everyone except me.

**6. Over-optimizing the tech stack.**
Spent [N] hours setting up [tools] I could have run on a spreadsheet.
Tools replace process, they don't create it.

**7. Refusing to charge for discovery calls.**
Free calls attract the cheapest prospects. The ones worth winning are rarely the ones who can't pay to talk to you.
Charging $[NUMBER] for a discovery call filtered the roster immediately.

[SAVE-TRIGGER CLOSE]
Save this if you're in year 1 of [ICP BUSINESS]. Come back to it each quarter and check yourself against the list. Each one I caught saved me 6-12 months of drift.

[CTA]
Want the full 30-item first-year audit I wrote for clients? Comment "audit" and I'll DM.
```

Character count: ~1950. Post type: Listicle.

## Worked Example 2 — Principle Listicle

```
[HOOK]
5 principles I use to decide whether to take on a new client or project.
Every "yes" gets run through all 5.

[FRAMING]
I used to take work based on gut. Gut was wrong ~40% of the time — the regret ratio was high enough to cost me $[AMOUNT] in opportunity over 2 years. These 5 principles replaced gut with checkboxes.

[ITEMS]
**1. The outcome is definable and measurable.**
If I can't say "[SPECIFIC METRIC] by [DATE]" before the engagement starts, I can't produce clarity for the client.
No definable outcome = no engagement.

**2. The client has skin in the game.**
If they're not paying for the call, they won't do the homework between calls. Asymmetric commitment kills results.
Price of entry is price of engagement.

**3. The domain rhymes with my existing work.**
I won't take first-of-kind work anymore — I'll take nth-of-kind work where the pattern is known to me.
Novelty is a drain, repetition is a lever.

**4. The timeline is reasonable for the outcome.**
If the client needs [OUTCOME] by [DATE] and the real timeframe is [2× DATE], the engagement fails before it starts.
I decline on timeline mismatch even when the client insists.

**5. The client is coachable.**
Signal: they push back on my advice with a specific reason (good) or they push back with "I don't think that'll work for us" (bad).
Uncoachable clients don't get results; the results get blamed on me.

[SAVE-TRIGGER CLOSE]
Print this. Put it next to the desk. Run every engagement request through it before replying. Say no faster, win better clients.

[CTA]
Want the full intake form I use? Comment "intake" and I'll DM.
```

Character count: ~1580. Post type: Listicle.

## Worked Example 3 — Tools / Process Listicle

```
[HOOK]
6 things I removed from my weekly workflow last quarter.
Revenue went up. Hours went down. Counterintuitive.

[FRAMING]
I had the bias most [ICP] have — "if it's producing any value, keep it." Audited the whole stack, kept what produced outsized value, cut the middle. Here's what got cut.

[ITEMS]
**1. Weekly 1:1s with direct reports.**
Replaced with async Loom updates + one 60-min open-office block per week.
Saved [N] hours/week. Team reported higher clarity.

**2. Morning social check.**
Cut the first 45 minutes of [PLATFORM] browsing.
Kept a 15-minute scheduled window at 2pm for intentional engagement.

**3. "Strategy" sessions with no decision.**
Banned meetings where no decision gets made by the end.
If we can't commit to a yes/no, the meeting gets rescheduled with better prep.

**4. Multi-tab content creation.**
Write one asset at a time, start to finish, no switching.
Finished posts doubled. Quality per post went up (no shallow edits between tabs).

**5. Overreacting to inbox.**
Moved to checking email 2× a day (11am, 4pm).
The world didn't collapse. Urgency was always self-imposed.

**6. "Let me know if you have questions" emails.**
Stopped ending with that. Stopped inviting friction. 
Replies dropped ~20%, but the replies I got were substantive, not reflexive.

[SAVE-TRIGGER CLOSE]
Save this when you do your next weekly workflow audit. Every item removed from the stack is a hidden revenue gain.

[CTA]
If you want the full audit template I used, comment "audit" and I'll DM.
```

Character count: ~1500. Post type: Listicle.

## Slot-Fill Rules

### The Hook slot
- Number in the first 5 words ("7 mistakes I made...")
- Specificity ≥ 8 (ICP + timeframe + stake)
- Second line widens stake or previews insight

### The Framing slot
- Establish credibility (first-person, dated)
- Prime the list (why these, why now)
- 2-4 lines max

### The Items slot
- Each item headed by a bold one-liner (the takeaway)
- 2-5 lines of expansion
- Numbered (1-N)
- Consistent structure across items (if item 1 has a mechanism, items 2-N should too)

### The Save-Trigger Close slot
- Explicit instruction to save ("Save this when...")
- Name the moment the reader will need it

### The CTA slot
- One action
- Often ties to a Lead Magnet ("Want the full version? DM me")

---

## Listicle Quality Tests

**Item independence test:** each item should stand alone. If item 3 only makes sense after reading item 2, collapse them.

**Parallel structure test:** every item should have the same shape (lead + expansion + why). Breaking parallelism hurts save rate.

**Density test:** if any item requires 10+ lines to explain, split it into a separate post.

---

## First Comment Plan

- 60-90 min post-publish
- Add "bonus item" the body didn't include
- OR drop a link to the related Lead Magnet (in comment)
- Reply to commenters with their most-relevant item

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Item count too low (3 feels thin; 5 is minimum for most)
- Item count too high (10+ drops engagement)
- Items aren't parallel in structure
- No save-trigger close (misses bookmark rate)
- Generic framing (any creator could have written it)
