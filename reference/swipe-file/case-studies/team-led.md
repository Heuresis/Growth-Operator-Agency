# Team-Led Case Study Structure

> **Client's team (not client personally) executed → outcome.** B2B. Organizational sale. Multi-stakeholder cases.

---

## The Structure

```
[THE ORGANIZATION]
Who the client is at the org level — sector, size, context.

[THE STAKEHOLDERS]
Named roles (not names) — who bought, who executed, who was affected.

[THE INITIAL STATE]
Organizational metrics, not just individual ones.

[THE ENGAGEMENT STRUCTURE]
How the work got done across roles.

[THE TEAM MOMENTS]
Specific moments where team execution — not founder effort — produced the outcome.

[THE ORGANIZATIONAL OUTCOME]
Company-level metrics, dated.

[THE TRANSFERABLE FRAME FOR OTHER ORGS]
What organizations matching this profile can take from the case.
```

## Worked Template — The Mid-Stage Company Restructure

```
[THE ORGANIZATION]
[ICP]: mid-stage [SECTOR] company, [EMPLOYEE BAND], $[REVENUE BAND] annual, 8 years in market. Founder-led but with a fully formed leadership team.

Engagement purpose: restructure the growth function (marketing + sales + partnerships) to scale past the current plateau.

[THE STAKEHOLDERS]
Who was in the room:

- CEO (founder) — engaged me, sponsored the work
- CRO — owned the growth function day-to-day; was my primary working partner
- Head of Marketing — reported to CRO; owned content + demand gen
- VP Sales — reported to CRO; owned the sales team
- Head of Partnerships — reported to CRO; owned JV / affiliate
- Ops Lead — reported to CEO; owned systems + reporting
- Finance Lead — reported to CEO; owned budget + forecasts

8-person leadership across 7 roles (Finance + Ops both report to CEO).

My role: engaged by CEO, worked primarily with CRO and the four function leads. Read-outs to CEO monthly.

[THE INITIAL STATE]

Revenue: flat for 3 quarters at $[REVENUE]
New logo acquisition: down 18% year-over-year
Customer retention: stable at 84%
Sales pipeline: front-loaded (lots of late-stage, thin early-stage)
Marketing pipeline contribution: 22%
Sales-sourced pipeline: 38%
Partnership-sourced: 12%
"Other" (inbound, event, outbound unattributed): 28%

Leadership diagnosis varied:
- CEO thought the issue was marketing
- CRO thought the issue was sales velocity
- Marketing thought the issue was product-market refresh
- Sales thought the issue was lead quality
- Partnerships thought the issue was under-investment

None of them were wrong. None of them were complete.

[THE ENGAGEMENT STRUCTURE]

Six-month engagement, three phases:

Phase 1 (month 1-2) — diagnostic.
- Interviews with each leader individually
- Full pipeline audit
- Customer interview batch (12 existing, 8 churned)
- Competitive position review

Phase 2 (month 3-4) — restructure.
- New function coordination protocol (weekly growth sync, not one-off meetings)
- Lead attribution model rebuilt
- Marketing / sales handoff redefined
- Partnership motion scoped to 2 channels (down from 4)

Phase 3 (month 5-6) — execution + measurement.
- Each function shipped defined quarterly outputs
- New attribution read-out monthly
- CEO + CRO quarterly review of function health

[THE TEAM MOMENTS]

Not about me. About the team:

Month 2 — Head of Marketing and VP Sales ran the first joint pipeline review in 18 months. They found a specific misalignment in lead qualification that had been causing 30%+ of MQLs to be rejected downstream. Fixed in 3 weeks.

Month 4 — Head of Partnerships proposed pruning 2 of the 4 partnership channels against initial plan. The pruning was counter-intuitive but based on real data. CRO approved. Revenue from the remaining 2 channels grew [%] within 90 days.

Month 5 — CRO led a rebuild of the sales comp plan that aligned reps to pipeline quality metrics, not just close. Short-term controversial, accepted by the sales team with clear rationale.

Month 6 — the Ops Lead and Finance Lead built the attribution model that finally let CEO see which functions were producing what ROI. That clarity alone changed Q+1 budget allocation meaningfully.

[THE ORGANIZATIONAL OUTCOME]

12 months after engagement end:

Revenue: [%] growth
New logo acquisition: up [%]
Marketing pipeline contribution: [from 22%] to [35%]
Sales-sourced: stable
Partnership-sourced: [from 12%] to [25%] (against expectation — the pruning worked)
Customer retention: stable

What was notable: the growth came from rebalancing across functions, not from adding people or budget. Headcount was flat. Spend was flat.

[THE TRANSFERABLE FRAME FOR OTHER ORGS]

For mid-stage [SECTOR] companies facing a similar plateau:

1. Cross-function misalignment almost always produces pipeline leakage. Fix the alignment before adding acquisition.

2. Each function's leader has partial truth about the plateau. The full picture emerges from composite diagnosis, not any single perspective.

3. Attribution models aren't about tracking — they're about budget allocation. Without attribution, budget goes to the loudest function. With attribution, budget goes to the highest-ROI function.

4. Team-led execution beats founder-driven execution in mid-stage orgs. The founder's role becomes sponsoring the work, not doing it. Organizations where the founder insists on driving growth directly rarely scale past this plateau.

5. Restructuring works without new spend if the coordination improvements are real. Most mid-stage plateaus are coordination problems disguised as acquisition problems.
```

## Slot-Fill Rules

### The Organization slot
- Sector, size, context
- Anonymized (org-level anonymization still possible)
- Stage (not startup, not enterprise)

### The Stakeholders slot
- Roles, not names
- Relationship map (who reports to whom)
- Your role clearly defined

### The Initial State slot
- Organizational metrics
- Each leader's diagnosis (often conflicting)

### The Engagement Structure slot
- Phases
- Cadence
- Who you worked with directly

### The Team Moments slot
- Specific moments — team-driven
- Named moves by named roles (not your name)
- Honest about what the team produced

### The Organizational Outcome slot
- Company-level metrics
- Dated
- Delta math

### The Transferable Frame slot
- For organizations matching the profile
- Multi-stakeholder lessons
- Honest about what replicates

---

## When Team-Led Wins

- B2B / organizational ICPs
- Mid-stage companies
- Multi-stakeholder sales (the decision maker isn't the only stakeholder)
- Cases where founder wasn't the hero

When it loses:
- Solo operator cases (use Before-After or Hero Cycle)
- Cases where individual transformation was the win

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Making yourself the hero (team-led means team moments)
- Using individual metrics on organizational cases
- Missing stakeholder complexity
- Not showing the composite diagnosis
- Generic transferable frame
