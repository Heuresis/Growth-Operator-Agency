# Obstacle-Decision-Outcome (ODO) Case Study Structure

> **Decision-focused.** Obstacle faced → decision made → outcome produced. Teaches judgment, not tactics.

---

## The Structure

```
[CONTEXT]
Who, when, what was at stake.

[OBSTACLE]
The specific structural or situational problem.
What made this obstacle hard — why the obvious fix wouldn't work.

[THE DECISION]
The fork in the road.
Options considered. Tradeoffs per option.
What the client chose + why.

[EXECUTION]
How the decision got implemented. Brief — the decision is the lesson, not execution.

[OUTCOME]
Result + dated metrics.

[THE TRANSFERABLE DECISION FRAME]
The judgment principle behind the choice.
What other [ICP] would face the same decision and how to think about it.
```

## Worked Template — The "Close or Restructure" Decision

```
[CONTEXT]
[ICP TYPE]: founder-operator, 6-year business, 12 team members, $[REVENUE] annual. End of [YEAR].

The business was on paper successful. In practice the founder was burning out and considering closing.

[OBSTACLE]
The team was profitable, the clients were happy, the product worked. But the founder was doing ~65 hours/week of work that no one else on the team could do — largely because of how the business had been built around the founder's specific skill.

Closing would lose a profitable business. Restructuring would take 6-12 months of grind during which the founder would have to do more work, not less, to train and systemize.

Neither option was obviously right. The founder had spent 3 months oscillating between the two.

[THE DECISION]
Three options considered:

Option A — close cleanly. 
Pro: immediate relief. 
Con: loss of $[AMOUNT]/year of income and 6 years of built asset.

Option B — sell the business. 
Pro: capital event. 
Con: valuation would be heavily discounted because founder-dependent operations don't sell well.

Option C — restructure to reduce founder dependency over 9-12 months.
Pro: preserves asset, builds sellable business.
Con: requires 6 months of harder work before things get easier.

The founder chose Option C.

The reason: the diagnostic question that broke the loop wasn't "which option is best?" It was "which option does the future version of me thank me for?"

The future version answering "C" independent of what the present version felt like.

[EXECUTION]
Not the lesson here. Briefly:
- Documented 12 founder-critical workflows into SOPs
- Hired a #2 (operations lead) with specific scope
- Gradually transitioned client relationships from founder to team over 6 months
- Pruned 30% of offerings to concentrate on the highest-margin 70%
- Founder hours dropped from 65 to 45 by month 9

[OUTCOME]
At month 12 after the decision:
- Founder hours: 38/week (from 65)
- Revenue: marginally up ([NUMBER]% growth)
- Team size: 14 (up 2)
- Founder's answer when asked "would you do it again?": "Yes, but 18 months earlier."

At month 24 (where we are now):
- Founder hours: 30/week
- Revenue: [%] growth
- Business in a shape that could be sold if desired — valuation now [X]× what Option B would have been at the decision point

[THE TRANSFERABLE DECISION FRAME]
The frame: when stuck between "close" and "rebuild," ask which option your future self thanks you for — not which option feels best right now.

The present version of you is biased by fatigue (toward closing) or sunk cost (toward preserving). The future version has a clearer line of sight.

A supporting principle: founder-dependency is the biggest valuation haircut in most small businesses. Rebuilding to reduce dependency isn't just "quality of life" work. It's asset-building.

Other [ICP] facing this fork: if you're burnt out on a profitable business, Option C is almost always the right call — if you have 9-12 months of runway to do it. If you don't have runway, Option A or B become more honest.
```

## Slot-Fill Rules

### The Context slot
- Who, when, stakes
- 2-3 sentences
- Anonymized unless permission

### The Obstacle slot
- Structural, not just situational
- Named specifically
- Why the obvious fix doesn't work

### The Decision slot
- 3 options minimum (binary decisions usually mean a missing option)
- Pros/cons per option
- What tilted the choice + why

### The Execution slot
- Brief — ODO stories are about the decision, not the execution
- 1 paragraph max

### The Outcome slot
- Dated metrics
- Both short-term (12 month) and longer-term if possible

### The Transferable Decision Frame slot
- The judgment principle behind the choice
- How another [ICP] should think if facing the same fork

---

## Decision Integrity

Per `INVARIANTS.md`:
- Real decision, real client
- Don't retroactively clean up the decision process
- Include the real uncertainty (the client oscillated for 3 months; don't sanitize)

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Decision framed binary when 3+ options existed
- Execution section swallows the decision (loses focus)
- Outcome is the only thing measured — missing the decision quality
- Frame is tactical ("what to do") instead of judgment-level ("how to think")
- Hero-worship of client's decision (reads as flattery, not teaching)
