# Hero-Cycle Case Study Structure

> **Call → refusal → mentor → transformation → return.** Narrative-heavy. Works when the client's identity transformed, not just metrics.

---

## The Structure

```
[THE CALL]
Something surfaced that the client couldn't ignore anymore.

[THE REFUSAL]
Why they didn't act immediately. What they tried to do instead.

[THE MENTOR / CATALYST]
The person, moment, or system that cracked open the refusal.
This is where you enter the story — carefully, not in hero pose.

[THE THRESHOLD]
The moment they actually committed. The first hard decision.

[THE TRANSFORMATION]
What changed — not just in metrics, but in how they operate and think.

[THE RETURN]
Where they are now. What they carry forward. Who they've become.

[THE TRANSFERABLE ARC]
What other [ICP] can recognize in this arc.
```

## Worked Template — The Identity Shift Case

```
[THE CALL]
[ICP]: agency owner, 5 years running a $[REVENUE]/year operation. The call came on a [SPECIFIC TRIGGER — e.g., third consecutive quarter of flat revenue; a client leaving for a competitor; a conversation with their partner about time away].

She described the moment to me as "the first week I couldn't talk my way out of the fact that something was structurally wrong." 

The call wasn't "I need more leads." It was "the business I built is the wrong shape for the life I want, and pretending otherwise has a cost I can finally see."

[THE REFUSAL]
She refused for about 9 months.

She tried:
- Hiring a VA to take administrative load off (didn't address the core issue)
- Taking a 2-week vacation (the issue was waiting for her on return)
- Adding a new revenue line (compounded the complexity)
- Reading more books about scaling (accumulated theory without application)

Each refusal was rational in isolation. Stacked, they looked like avoidance.

What broke the refusal: a specific conversation with a peer who named what she'd been avoiding: "You don't have a scaling problem. You have an offer problem. The business is structured around delivering what you're best at, not what's most scalable."

[THE MENTOR / CATALYST]
Came in as the catalyst for the restructure, not as a hero. My role was specific: name the obstacle, provide the frame, hold the accountability for the hard decisions.

The frame I offered: audit the revenue by deliverable type. Rank by margin × scalability × founder-dependency. Keep the top quartile. Prune or systemize the rest.

This wasn't new advice. It was the frame she needed at the moment she was ready to use it.

[THE THRESHOLD]
The first hard decision: stop taking a specific project type that was 35% of revenue but 70% of founder-dependent hours.

Revenue dropped 35% in month 2 of the restructure. 

She held. 

The pruning forced the remaining 65% of revenue to become more profitable per hour. Within 4 months, the 65% had grown to be equal in total revenue to the prior 100%, and required 40% fewer hours.

[THE TRANSFORMATION]
Beyond the metrics:

She changed how she talked about the business. "Agency owner" became "operator." The identity shift preceded the scaling unlocks, not the other way around.

She started saying no to fit-adjacent work — offers she would have taken reflexively a year earlier. The new filter: "does this move the business toward the shape I want, or does it distract from it?"

She added a weekly 90-minute block to audit her own decisions. Not reactive firefighting — structural review.

Metrics (month 12 after the threshold):
- Revenue: parity with peak (100% of prior)
- Hours: 60% of prior
- Margin: 1.8× prior
- Team size: same

[THE RETURN]
She's in month 18 now. The business is in a shape she could sell if she wanted — she doesn't — or hand to a #2 and step back.

The part she carries forward: "the hardest work was giving myself permission to shape the business around what I wanted, not what I thought I was supposed to do."

She's coaching two other [ICP] now. Informally. Not as a side business. Just because they ask and she has answers she didn't have two years ago.

[THE TRANSFERABLE ARC]
The recognizable shape: the call comes loud, the refusal lasts months, the catalyst isn't new information but a frame that lands at the right moment, the threshold decision costs revenue before it pays, the transformation is identity-level before it's metric-level.

Other [ICP] in refusal right now: the longer the refusal, the more specific the catalyst has to be. Generic motivation doesn't work. The catalyst has to name the exact thing you've been avoiding.

If you're 9+ months into a refusal, the catalyst probably exists in your life already — in a peer, a conversation, a frame you've been brushing past. Look for the thing that makes you uncomfortable when someone says it. That's the catalyst.
```

## Slot-Fill Rules

### The Call slot
- Specific trigger (dated if possible)
- Client's own words (paraphrased if not exact)
- The felt experience of the moment

### The Refusal slot
- What they tried instead
- How long they refused
- Honest about the refusal (don't sanitize)

### The Mentor / Catalyst slot
- You enter carefully — not hero pose
- Your role was specific, named
- The frame, not the message

### The Threshold slot
- The first hard decision
- The cost of the decision (revenue drop, lost clients, etc.)
- They held through the cost

### The Transformation slot
- Metrics + identity shift
- Changes in operation and thinking
- Before/after on the metric level + before/after on the identity level

### The Return slot
- Where they are now
- What they carry forward
- Their own voice on the arc

### The Transferable Arc slot
- What other [ICP] can recognize
- The shape of the arc as a pattern
- How to spot one's own version

---

## Hero-Cycle Ethics

- The client is the hero — not the consultant
- Catalyst role should be specific and modest
- The refusal has to be real (don't rush it)
- The transformation must be earned (showing only metrics hollows the arc)

---

## When Not To Use Hero-Cycle

- Case without real identity transformation (use Before-After)
- Case under 6 months (arc hasn't completed)
- Case where client didn't change how they operate
- Case that's mostly about tactics (use Obstacle-Decision-Outcome)

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Hero-worshipping the consultant
- Sanitizing the refusal
- No threshold cost (reads like easy win)
- Metrics-only return (misses identity transformation)
- Generic transferable arc ("work hard, trust the process")
