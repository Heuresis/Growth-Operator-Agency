# Numbers-First Case Study Structure

> **Lead with 3-5 numbers. Story supports the proof.** Proof-dominant. Short-form friendly.

---

## The Structure

```
[THE NUMBERS]
3-5 dated metrics. The headline deltas.

[CONTEXT PARAGRAPH]
Brief — who, when, stakes. 3-5 lines.

[THE PROCESS COMPRESSED]
The moves that produced the numbers. Compressed to essentials.

[THE MECHANISM]
Why the numbers moved.

[CAVEATS]
Honest conditions for replicability.
```

## Worked Template — The Revenue + Retention Case

```
[THE NUMBERS]

— MRR: $[START] → $[END] ([X]× in [TIMEFRAME])
— 90-day retention: [START %] → [END %]
— CAC: $[START] → $[END] ([Y]% down)
— Team size: [START] → [END]
— Founder hours: [START]/week → [END]/week

[CONTEXT]
[ICP]: SaaS founder, bootstrapped, 3 years into the business. Engagement was 9 months.

The starting state wasn't "failing." It was "plateaued and working harder." Classic mid-stage founder trap.

[THE PROCESS COMPRESSED]

Month 1: stopped all paid acquisition. Spent the month on retention diagnostics.
Month 2: built onboarding checkpoints + defined the activation metric.
Month 3: added a founder touchpoint at the highest-churn day.
Month 4: retention hit 70%+. Restarted paid acquisition at prior budget.
Month 5-9: acquisition scaled; retention held; MRR compounded.

Five moves. No new feature ship. No market expansion. No price increase. Just retention fix plus restart.

[THE MECHANISM]
The structural principle: retention isn't an acquisition problem.

Leaky bucket math: if 90-day retention is 52%, every new cohort of 100 users loses 48 in the first 90 days. Tripling acquisition just triples the churn velocity.

Fix the leak first. Activation metric + onboarding checkpoints + human touchpoint at the highest-churn day. Then scale acquisition.

The same CAC in month 5 produced multiple times the downstream LTV because churn was structurally lower.

[CAVEATS]

This result holds when:
- The product has clear product-market fit (retention problem is solvable, not fundamental)
- The founder has 30-60 days of runway to pause acquisition
- There's a definable activation metric available

It doesn't hold when:
- The product itself isn't working (retention problem is actually a product problem)
- The founder can't pause acquisition (too dependent on new revenue)
- The team lacks bandwidth for onboarding redesign

Different ICPs hit different parts of this arc faster or slower. The shape is consistent.
```

## Slot-Fill Rules

### The Numbers slot
- 3-5 metrics (more = dilutes)
- Dated
- Matched before/after pairs
- Not rounded in inflating ways

### The Context slot
- 3-5 lines max
- Who, when, stakes
- Anonymized unless permission

### The Process Compressed slot
- Monthly or weekly milestones
- Specific moves (not "we worked on retention")
- Tight — this is support, not the main act

### The Mechanism slot
- Why the numbers moved
- Transferable principle
- Structural, not tactical

### The Caveats slot
- Honest conditions for replicability
- When this works vs doesn't

---

## When Numbers-First Wins

- Audience is data-forward (SaaS founders, ops operators, analysts)
- Short-form platforms (X, Reels, quick emails)
- Decision-stage readers (already warm, need proof)
- Cases with clean metric deltas

When it loses:
- Audience prefers narrative
- Case is qualitative (identity shift, relationship changes)
- Long-form platforms where narrative pulls better

---

## Common Failure Modes

- Numbers without dates (looks fake)
- Too many numbers (6+ = noise)
- Process compressed so much it's unhelpful
- Missing mechanism (reader sees numbers, can't assess replicability)
- No caveats (reads as guaranteed outcome)
